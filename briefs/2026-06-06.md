## What Happened

### Good news everywhere, but the market still fell

Friday handed the market four reasons to rally and it fell anyway. The fourth-quarter GDP came in at 7.8% against a roughly 7.3% consensus, the full year at 7.7%, confirming India as the fastest-growing major economy. The Reserve Bank held the repo rate at 5.25% in a unanimous 6-0 vote, the rupee firmed to 94.95 against a rising dollar, and crude kept easing. Yet Nifty closed −0.21%, because foreign investors sold ₹8,776 crore of cash equities and shorted index futures heavily, with domestic institutions the only buyers. This is the cleanest signal of the session — the flow overhang now outweighs the macro data. For the principal the read is that a strong print no longer lifts the book on its own while foreign selling dominates; the whole portfolio carries that sentiment drag regardless of name-level news.

### The RBI's real headline was a bond-market reform, not the rate hold

Beneath the expected hold sits the actual news: the Reserve Bank, with the government, opened the foreign-investor door to Indian debt. It widened the Fully Accessible Route to all new 15, 30 and 40-year government bonds plus sovereign green bonds, exempted foreign investors from income tax on bond interest and capital gains from next April, removed sub-limits, and agreed to bear the hedging cost on fresh foreign-currency deposits into September. Foreign investors bought over ₹3,000 crore of these bonds the same day. This is a structural attempt to re-anchor India's external account through the debt channel — and it pulled the bond market hard, even as the equity side kept bleeding. The same morning the bank turned cautious on its forecasts: it cut next year's growth view to 6.6% from 6.9% and raised inflation to 5.1% from 4.6%, pushing rate-cut hopes further out. For the rate-sensitive book — Jio Financial — the funding backdrop is net constructive, but the central bank is now defending the rupee through flows rather than rates.

### Hind Rectifiers drops out of the portfolio track

Hind Rectifiers, the power-electronics and defence name, came off the tier-one track today, taking the count from 21 to 20. It had been promoted onto the track on 04 June on a strong price run, but its fourth-quarter deep research came back a PASS at conviction 2. Operating margins collapsed to about 3% from nearly 11% a year earlier, profit fell more than half on rising revenue, and a Q3 management promise to recover margins "in the next couple of quarters" was broken and pushed out again. Under the standing rule — the track is reserved for owned holdings plus buy-rated names only — a PASS verdict cannot hold a tier-one slot, the same rule that demoted eleven organic names on 02 June. So Hind Rectifiers moves to the watch tier. The 05 June run missed this only because the verdict had not yet propagated. No fresh research is warranted; the name is already researched and in cooldown.

## Cascade Watch

**The foreign-flow edge overran every macro positive.** The cause is the collision of good macro data with persistent foreign selling — a GDP beat and a rate hold against ₹8,776 crore of foreign cash exits plus heavy futures shorting. The mechanism is the foreign-flow edge running through every tier-one node, with domestic institutions the only absorbing buyer; the same channel the RBI's bond reforms target on the debt side is still draining the equity side. Tomorrow's read: whether the futures-short positioning unwinds next week or the equity-outflow channel keeps capping rallies.

**The bond-market-reform edge bull-flattened the belly.** The cause is the RBI's expanded Fully Accessible Route and the foreign-investor tax exemptions on government bonds. The mechanism is the rates edge pulling the front and belly of the curve down hard — the one-year fell 12.6 basis points and the five-year 15.6, while the ten-year barely moved — as foreign demand for longer bonds is priced in. Tomorrow's read: whether foreign bond buying builds on the ₹3,000-crore first-day flow, which would be the structural offset to the equity exit.

**The energy-input-cost edge stayed eased for the input-heavy cohorts.** The cause is crude holding its lower band, down roughly 2-3% as the Hormuz premium keeps unwinding. The mechanism is the energy-input-cost edge holding in the relief direction for the fertiliser, tyre and beverage cohorts (Coromandel, plus Varun Beverages in the book), with a small input-cost tailwind feeding through. Tomorrow's read: whether crude holds or the premium re-firms on any shipping setback.

**The metals edge split a global selloff from one idiosyncratic name.** The cause is a global precious-and-base-metals washout on a stronger dollar — gold −3.1%, silver −6.55%, copper −3.8% — that dragged Nifty Metal −1.6%. The mechanism is the metals-cohort edge carrying a genuine commodity shock plus one name-specific overhang (Hindustan Zinc, on a government stake-sale report); the gold crash is demand-supportive but inventory-negative for jewellers. Tomorrow's read: whether the dollar move extends or the metals complex stabilises.

## Rates Watch

**India yield curve: 3M 5.40% (−8.0 bps), 10Y 6.975% (−2.0 bps), 30Y 7.59% (−4.8 bps); slope 10Y−3M 157.5 bps.** Multiple instruments crossed the trigger and the belly led the rally — the one-year fell 12.6 basis points and the five-year 15.6, while the ten-year held almost flat: a clean bull-flattening at the front and belly. The driver is the RBI's expansion of foreign-investor access to long government bonds, which lifted demand expectations across the curve; the 7.8% GDP beat did not push yields up because the supply-and-access signal dominated. For non-bank lender funding cost (Jio Financial) the belly relief is the near-term tailwind; whole-curve banks were neutral with the ten-year unchanged, and long-duration assets got a mild lift from the 30-year falling 4.8 basis points. The curve stays positively sloped at 157.5 basis points, no inversion.
