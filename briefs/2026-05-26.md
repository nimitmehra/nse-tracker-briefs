## What Happened

### Iran strikes firm Brent and shut the rate-cut door

A weekend peace narrative reversed inside 48 hours and dragged Indian macro framing with it. US President Trump claimed a "largely negotiated" deal to reopen the Strait of Hormuz; on Monday, US forces struck Iranian missile sites and mine-laying boats, and he abandoned the deal language. Brent crude — captured at a deal-optimism low of $96.45 in the morning snapshot — firmed back to roughly $98 on the strike, leaving the week's near-10% fall intact but the floor restored. The rupee held near ₹95.69 as the Reserve Bank of India announced a fresh $5 billion currency swap auction. India imports about 85% of its crude, so sub-$100 Brent is a tailwind. The binary — Hormuz reopens or escalates — stays unresolved, so single-session moves are noise around it.

### The rate cycle hardens from hold to a possible hike

The domestic rate regime shifted right, repositioning every rate-sensitive holding. A Business Standard poll still expects the RBI's six-member rate-setting committee to hold the repo rate at 5.25% on June 5. The genuinely new development: Standard Chartered now expects the committee to begin raising rates from June, a cumulative 50 basis points across the next fiscal year — the first explicit sell-side hike call this cycle. FY27 inflation is projected near 4.6% against this year's 2.1%, with energy, a weak rupee and weather as upside risks. The live debate is no longer hold-versus-cut but hold-versus-hike. A hike would pressure the funding costs of affordable-housing lenders Aptus Value Housing (APTUS) and India Shelter Finance (INDIASHLTR), and the lending book at Jio Financial Services (JIOFIN). The rate-cut cycle is, for now, shelved.

### Coromandel's input-cost relief is the wrong story

The fertiliser cohort's "cheaper crude helps" framing is being quietly inverted. Coromandel International (COROMANDEL), the Murugappa-group phosphatic-fertiliser maker and a tier-one buy-cohort name on the accumulation watch, sits at ₹1,846.80, fractionally below its ₹1,850 accumulation line. The new vector: China's sulphuric-acid export ban plus Iran-conflict sulphur-supply disruption are pushing up the binding phosphatic input — sulphur, not crude. The transcript-grade research already on file cites sulphur at $850-900 a tonne, four-to-five times the pre-war reference, and ties the 76% Q4 profit fall directly to higher phosphoric-acid and sulphur costs. So the softer-Brent freight relief Chambal Fertilisers (CHAMBLFERT) rallied 3.1% on today is real but secondary. The dominant read for the cohort is input-cost stress, and a below-normal monsoon at 92% of the long-period average adds a mild volume caveat.

### Inventurus locks in TruBridge at $557 million and ~$600 million of debt

The healthcare-services thesis just gained scale and leverage in one move. Inventurus Knowledge Solutions (IKS), the US-focused care-enablement company, confirmed full terms for its TruBridge acquisition. The deal is $557 million at $26.25 cash a share, funded with roughly $600 million of debt over five years. It closes in the third calendar quarter of 2026, subject to antitrust and shareholder approvals. The combined entity will serve 2,000-plus healthcare organisations and 150,000-plus clinicians, pairing Inventurus care-enablement with TruBridge revenue-cycle software for rural hospitals. The structure is material for a company that had been deleveraging from ₹850 crore toward ₹251 crore of net debt. Scale and cross-sell are the bull; leverage and integration are the new bear. Net read: ambiguous, and now a balance-sheet event to track to close.

### Infosys carries a hold and a quantified AI overhang

The sell-side has stopped arguing the bull case on the portfolio's deepest drawdown. IDBI Capital's May-26 review of thirteen IT-services stocks carries Infosys (INFY) at hold with a ₹1,355 target, far below CLSA's March ₹1,653 call. The shares sit 31% below their 52-week high, the deepest derating in the tier-one cohort. The review quantifies the structural worry: the IT-services index has corrected about 24% on generative-AI recalibration, and "AI deflation risk" has expanded to 6-7% of portfolios. Chief executive Salil Parekh's resilient FY26 framing — 3.1% growth, $14.9 billion of large-deal wins, the Anthropic, Intel and Harness collaborations, a ₹25 dividend — is the partner-not-victim narrative. Consensus is not endorsing it aggressively. The parallel-narrative tension between AI partner and AI casualty stays unresolved, with the desk's read holding at hold.
