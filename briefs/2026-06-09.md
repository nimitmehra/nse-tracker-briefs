## What Happened

### A bank-led relief rebound, not a broad recovery

Tuesday reversed Monday's drop, but narrowly. Nifty rose 0.52% to 23,242, and the lifting was done almost entirely by financials — Nifty Bank up 2.09%, its biggest single-day gain since around 10 April, and the public-sector-bank index up 3.62%, the strongest sector of the day. The trigger was domestic: the Reserve Bank of India opened two special dollar-rupee swap windows on Monday, one for fresh long-tenor foreign-currency deposits and one for public-sector overseas borrowing, a funding-cost tailwind that hits public-sector deposit-takers hardest. This is a rupee-support measure, not a rate cut, but it landed as the reason banks led. The breadth tells the real story — IT was the only red sector and stayed down 8.4% on the week, while fear collapsed (India VIX −8.54% to 15.58). For the principal the read is a fear-unwind plus a single domestic catalyst, not a conviction turn — the book felt it as a beta bounce in the names that fell hardest Monday.

### Garware hits a record high while its finance chief's seat sits empty

Garware Hi-Tech Films, the specialty-films holding, was the biggest tier-one gainer on the day — up 6.57% to ₹6,225, closing exactly at its 52-week high and now up 100.8% on the year on battery-materials and fluoropolymer re-rating momentum, with no single dated catalyst behind the move. The tension is that this record print sits on top of an unresolved governance flag: the chief-financial-officer seat has now been empty about 25 days since the 15 May resignation, with no successor named and no filing on Tuesday. A new independent director joins on 25 June, but that does not fill the finance role. The price strength is genuine, but it is masking a live risk, not retiring it — the escalation point is the 26 June shareholder-ballot result, and if the seat is still empty by then the flag firms. Surface the successor appointment the moment it lands; do not let the 52-week high read as an all-clear.

### West Asia stepped back, but did not sign anything

The macro driver underneath Tuesday's relief was a partial de-escalation. After Monday's exchange of strikes, Iran announced it was halting attacks, and Israel — at President Trump's request — agreed to refrain from further strikes on Iran. Crude unwound the spike, with Brent easing 1.73% to $92.62, and that removed Monday's input-cost headwind for the import-heavy book. But this is one step, not a settlement: Iran's halt is conditional and reversible, Israel's Lebanon offensive continues, and the Strait of Hormuz reopening is still being negotiated, not signed. Crude direction stays the single most thesis-load-bearing variable in the portfolio, since India imports roughly 85% of its oil — and the floor under it is conflict-contingent, not structural. For the principal the read is that the war-premium can re-spike on any Lebanon or Hormuz flare-up, so Tuesday's crude relief is a reprieve, not a resolution.
