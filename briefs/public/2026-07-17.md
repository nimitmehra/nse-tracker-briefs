## The Lead

India's June-quarter earnings season is starting to separate the strong companies from the weak ones, and on Friday that pulled the Nifty 50 up 1.09% to 24,334.30 while most of Asia fell hard. Record results from Federal Bank and beats across IT lifted private banks and software, the day's two engines. The catch worth remembering: this was home-grown, not a foreign vote of confidence — Indian institutions bought a net ₹1,018 crore while foreign investors were small net sellers. And the earnings story cut both ways, with tyre-maker CEAT crashing 7.3% after its quarterly profit fell 96% on costlier rubber and crude — a reminder that the oil premium is back, not gone.

---

## Global Setup

The global backdrop that India traded alongside on Friday was firmly risk-off, and India went the other way. The Asian markets that trade during Indian hours sold off hard — Japan's Nikkei fell 4.03%, Korea's KOSPI dropped 3.66% — and the broader emerging-market complex was red (the MSCI EM equity ETF −1.4%). Yet the Nifty rose over 1%, decoupling on the strength of its own results. The dollar was flat (dollar index 100.74) and the rupee firmed, an India-specific bid rather than a broad-dollar move. Crude was the notable turn: Brent rose 2.2% to $86.08, now up about 9% over the month — the "cheap oil" relief that helped markets a few weeks ago has faded. One honesty note: the big US fall on Friday night (S&P 500 −1.01%, Nasdaq −1.4%) came after India had closed, so it is Monday's input, not Friday's driver.

## Macro & Policy

The macro picture this week leaned mixed, with one clearly supportive read and one fresh worry, both new since the last brief seven days ago.

**On the supportive side,** the Reserve Bank's Weekly Statistical Supplement (released ~5 PM Friday) showed foreign-exchange reserves *rose* $964 million to $675.16 billion in the week to 10 July — a third straight weekly build. This is the print that finally captures the oil-shock window, and it shows no sign of the central bank burning reserves to defend the rupee; a modest build points the other way. Separately, the US inflation reading for June came in cooler at 3.5%, which pulled the US 10-year bond yield down to 4.54% and gave global risk appetite a small lift into Friday.

**On the worrying side,** India's own June inflation ran hot. Retail inflation accelerated to 4.38% year-on-year, the highest since December 2024, with food inflation jumping to 5.32% as vegetable prices surged (ginger up 50%, tomatoes up 32%). The monsoon-deficit-to-food-prices channel we have been tracking is now visible in the data — and it complicates the Reserve Bank's room to cut rates at its early-August meeting, which had looked more open a month ago.

**Watch next:** the US–India trade deadline on 24 July (below), Infosys results on 23 July as the real test of the IT recovery, and the RBI rate decision in early August, now reframed by that hot inflation print.

## The Market

The headline gain was broad-based but earnings-led, not a rising tide lifting everything. The Bank Nifty did the heaviest lifting, up 1.63%, on private-bank optimism after Federal Bank's record quarter, and Nifty IT rose 1.75% as software carried Thursday night's Tech Mahindra and Wipro results into Friday. The clear laggard was pharma — the Nifty Pharma index fell 1.40%, led lower by diagnostics names, as money rotated out of the defensive corner into the day's growth stories. (A feed gap left most other sector indices without a clean daily figure this session, so only IT and pharma are quantified with confidence.)

The rupee firmed about 0.4% to ₹96.27 even though the dollar was flat, an India-specific move that fits the earnings-relief tone. But the signal in the flows is the important correction to the week's story: foreign investors were net sellers of ₹376 crore, and it was domestic institutions, buying ₹1,018 crore, that carried the market. The multi-week "foreign money is returning to India" narrative is genuinely true over July (about ₹15,000 crore of net equity inflow month-to-date), but Friday itself does not prove it — this specific up day was home-grown. The fear gauge, India VIX, ticked up 2.1% to 13.15 but stayed subdued, telling you this was a calm, orderly advance.

## Earnings

The day's notable results, biggest companies first — what they reported, regardless of how the share moved:

- **Reliance Industries (RELIANCE, market value ₹17.96 lakh crore)** — reported after Friday's close, so this is a Monday reaction, not a Friday driver. Revenue rose 27% year-on-year to ₹3,09,468 crore, but reported net profit fell about 25% to ₹23,196 crore, distorted by a year-ago one-off gain — the underlying operating trend is the read on Monday. The stock had risen 2.36% during Friday's session, ahead of the numbers.
- **Federal Bank (FEDERALBNK, ₹86,165 crore)** — the marquee beat and the private-bank engine of the day. Net profit rose 37% year-on-year to ₹1,302 crore on 9.9% revenue growth, with net interest income growing, asset quality improving to a decadal-low bad-loan level, and lower provisions. Exactly the clean quarter the market wanted from a private lender; the stock jumped 6.86% to a 52-week high (its move is in Big Movers).
- **JSW Steel (JSWSTEEL, ₹3.03 lakh crore)** — a strong steel quarter. Net profit more than doubled (up 113%) to ₹4,696 crore on 9.8% revenue growth, as steel spreads recovered. The stock added a modest 1.33%, the metal cohort a quiet participant on an IT-and-banks day.
- **CEAT (CEATLTD, ₹14,359 crore)** — the cautionary counterpoint. Revenue grew a healthy 22% to ₹4,318 crore, but net profit collapsed 96% to just ₹4 crore (from ₹112 crore), hit by a ~₹50 crore currency loss and more than 20% raw-material inflation (natural rubber ₹190→₹250 a kg, plus crude-linked inputs). The revenue is fine; the profit is a margin-and-forex story. Shares fell 7.3% (see Big Movers and The Connections).
- **Tech Mahindra (TECHM, ₹1.54 lakh crore)** — reported Thursday post-market; its Q1 profit rose 32% to ₹1,486 crore on 17.7% revenue growth, and that read-through was a big part of Friday's IT bid. The stock rose 4.14%, the top Nifty gainer.

*Omission note:* **Jio Financial** reported a more-than-doubling of quarterly profit across its lending, payments and asset-management arms, part of the same strong financials cohort; **Havells** and **Oberoi Realty** also filed in-window.

## The Connections

*Behind the session — what triggered the moves, why, and which sectors to watch next.*

**The oil premium is back, and CEAT is where you see it hit a company's profit.** For weeks the market traded a "cheap oil helps India" story. That has quietly reversed: Brent is back at $86, up about 9% over the month. You do not see it in the oil-refiner stocks — the single-day tick isn't big enough to move them — but you see it clearly in a company that *buys* oil-linked inputs. CEAT's revenue grew 22%, yet its profit fell 96%, because natural rubber and crude-derivative costs jumped more than 20% and a currency loss did the rest. Watch the whole tyre and auto-parts cohort: rising crude now shows up as a margin line, not a relief.

**Federal Bank's clean quarter turned private banks into the day's engine — and the read runs across the cohort.** The bank did not just beat on profit; its bad-loan ratio fell to a decadal low with lower provisions, the exact combination the market has rewarded all season. That anchored a private-bank rally and, with NBFC names like Poonawalla Fincorp also posting sharp profit growth, the read is that lending profitability is broadening — not one strong bank, but a healthy financials cohort. The real cohort test now is whether the mid-week bank results keep the pattern.

**Friday's rise was domestic money, which is the important correction to the week's headline.** The story running all month is "foreign investors are returning to India," and over July that is genuinely true (about ₹15,000 crore of net equity inflow). But Friday itself does not prove it: foreign investors sold a net ₹376 crore, and domestic institutions, buying ₹1,018 crore, carried the market. This is the same pattern as the prior brief a week ago — the multi-week return is real, but the specific up-days keep being home-grown. Do not read a domestic-led rally as foreign conviction.

**India rose while Asia fell — and this time it was India's own story, not a global tide.** Japan's Nikkei fell 4% and Korea's KOSPI 3.7% during the same hours India rose over 1%. That is the reverse of late May, when global capital was leaving India for North Asia's chip trade. This session India decoupled *upward*, on its own results and its own domestic buyers, against a weak regional tape. The signal is that the earnings season, not foreign flows or regional beta, is now driving the Indian market.
