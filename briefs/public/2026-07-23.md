## The Lead

Indian shares fell for a fourth straight day on Thursday, and the cause was the one that has driven the whole slide: oil. Brent crude crossed $100 a barrel intraday, its highest since late May, after fresh attacks on tankers off Saudi Arabia and in the Strait of Hormuz and a halt to a major Caspian pipeline. For a country that imports about 85% of its crude, that lands straight on the import bill and the rupee. The Nifty 50 slipped 0.53% to 23,869.60. But the fall was orderly, not a panic, with the fear gauge barely up. Foreign investors sold ₹2,999 crore of shares, and domestic funds bought almost exactly as much, which is why India held up better than most of the world.

---

## Global Setup

The global mood was risk-off, and oil was the wire running through it. Wall Street had closed lower overnight (the S&P 500 down about 1.2%, the Nasdaq down 2.1%) as rising US bond yields and a wobble in the big tech names soured sentiment; the US 10-year yield ticked up to 4.70% and the dollar index firmed 0.34% to 101.5. The one commodity that mattered for India was crude, and it was climbing hard toward $100 on the fresh Middle East supply scare. Gold caught a safe-haven bid on the same nerves. The read for India was simple and unhelpful: a firmer dollar and costlier oil both push on the rupee and the import bill at the same time, and that is the pressure the market was selling.

## Macro & Policy

The day's macro was almost all one-directional, and the direction was negative.

**The heavy negative was oil, and it got worse.** Brent crossed $100, its highest since late May, after Houthi militants struck two Saudi tankers in the Red Sea (a second supply front alongside the Strait of Hormuz) and Kazakhstan halted crude exports through the Caspian pipeline after drone attacks. For India, a large net importer, costlier crude re-opens the imported-inflation channel, widens the current-account gap and presses the rupee, which is why the market sold the rate-sensitive corners. The two-sided nuance: the same rising crude actually helps pure refiners even as it squeezes fuel-retailer margins, and a sustained $100 could push back the RBI's room to cut rates later this year.

**The one domestic cushion was flows.** Foreign investors sold ₹2,999 crore, but domestic institutions bought ₹2,947 crore, soaking up almost the entire foreign exit. That is why India fell far less than its peers on a global risk-off day.

**Watch next:** the US-India tariff deadline falls on Friday 24 July, and it is the single biggest scheduled event. The temporary trade window put in place after a US court struck the earlier tariff framework expires that day; the interim deal is still parked at what both sides call "the last 1%" of legal text, with an around-18% US tariff on Indian goods on the table. Nothing is signed, so treat it as reported, not confirmed.

## The Market

The headline fall was moderate but broad. Financials were the softest large corner, with the Bank Nifty down 0.94%, as the oil-into-inflation read pushed out hopes of an early rate cut and hit rate-sensitive lenders. Declining shares outnumbered advancing ones by roughly 1.6 to 1, and the mid- and small-cap indices fell about 1%, so the damage went wider than the index number suggests.

The striking non-move was IT. The Nifty IT index closed flat (−0.06%) even though a near-record-weak rupee normally helps software exporters, who earn dollars and spend rupees. On Thursday that tailwind was cancelled twice over: by the overnight global tech sell-off, and by traders sitting on their hands ahead of Infosys, whose results were due after the close. Pharma slipped 0.38%. The rupee ended at ₹96.56, a shade weaker and holding just below its record-weak level. The VIX rose only 1.39% to 13.48 — a low, orderly reading that says this was a grind lower, not fear.

## Earnings

A heavy results day, biggest companies first, with what they reported and how the market read it:

- **InterGlobe Aviation (INDIGO, market value ₹1.9 lakh crore)** — swung to a ₹238 crore Q1 loss, from a ₹2,176 crore profit a year earlier, as fuel costs jumped about 86% and an adverse currency swing bit; revenue still grew 19.9% to ₹24,584 crore. Strip out the forex hit and the quarter was near-breakeven, so the fall was contained. Results landed right at the close, so the real market vote is Friday. The oil story, straight through the P&L.
- **HPCL (HINDPETRO, ₹0.8 lakh crore) and BPCL (₹1.3 lakh crore)** — both state fuel retailers posted quarterly losses (HPCL −₹12,265 crore, its first loss in about 14 quarters; BPCL −₹1,873 crore) as the crude spike squeezed the margins they make selling petrol and diesel. This is the other side of the same oil shock. Shares fell 2.5% and 1.3%.
- **SRF (SRF, ₹0.8 lakh crore)** — a record quarter, with revenue up 31.8% and profit up 75.7% to ₹759 crore, yet the stock fell 8.5%. On the call, management guided the next two quarters softer on pricing and volume, and the market sold the forward downgrade, not the excellent past quarter. Guidance beat the headline.
- **IndusInd Bank (INDUSINDBK, ₹0.8 lakh crore)** — a 72% "profit jump" to ₹1,037 crore that was really lower bad-loan provisions, not core growth; net interest income (the bank's main engine) was flat, up just 1%. The market saw through the optics, and the stock fell about 6%.

- **Two clean beats bucked the trend:** NTPC Green Energy (NTPCGREEN, ₹0.8 lakh crore) reported profit up 38% and revenue up 63% and rose 6.5%; Tanla Platforms (TANLA) posted profit up 20% and jumped 10.1%, the day's best green name.
- **The biggest single fallers were both results-driven:** Control Print (CONTROLPR) fell 12.65% after profit dropped 42% on cost inflation and a forex swing, and CIE Automotive India (CIEINDIA) fell 11.32% on a margin miss despite 11% revenue growth.

## The Connections

*Behind the session: what triggered the moves, why, and which sectors to watch next.*

**One oil shock, and it hit the market in three different places.** Brent crossing $100 did not just lift a number on a screen. It squeezed the state fuel retailers, whose petrol-and-diesel margins collapsed into losses (HPCL's first loss in about 14 quarters, BPCL alongside); it blew up IndiGo's fuel bill, pushing the airline to a quarterly loss; and it pressed the rupee toward its record-weak level, which raises the cost of everything India imports. Same trigger, three separate wounds. What to watch is whether $100 sticks, because a sustained spike feeds inflation and narrows the RBI's room to cut rates.

**A near-record-weak rupee usually helps IT, and on Thursday it did nothing.** Software exporters earn in dollars and spend in rupees, so a weak rupee normally pads their rupee revenue and lifts the sector. But the IT index closed flat, because the global tech sell-off overnight cancelled the currency tailwind, and traders were waiting for Infosys, which reported only after the close. The read: on a genuine risk-off day the currency math gets ignored, and the real IT verdict is Friday's reaction to the Infosys guidance cut.

**India fell, but it fell less than the world, and domestic money is why.** The Nifty's 0.53% dip sat against a Nasdaq down about 2.1% overnight and a savage Friday-morning Asia (more on that in What to Watch). Foreign investors sold ₹2,999 crore, and domestic funds bought ₹2,947 crore, almost matching them share for share. When domestic flows absorb the foreign exit like this, India can hold its ground on a day the rest of the region is being sold. The thing to watch is whether that domestic bid keeps showing up if oil stays bid.

**Two results this week show the market pays for the future, not the past.** SRF put up a record quarter and fell 8.5% because it guided the next two quarters softer. IndusInd Bank reported a 72% "profit jump" and fell 6% because the jump came from lower provisions while its core lending income was flat. In both, the headline number was the trap and the detail underneath was the story.

## What to Watch Tomorrow

- **Friday 24 July**: the **US-India tariff cliff**, the largest scheduled event. The temporary trade window expires, the interim deal is still parked at "the last 1%" of legal text with an around-18% US tariff on the table, and nothing is signed. Reported, not confirmed.
- **Friday 24 July**: the **Infosys (INFY) market vote**. The company reported after Thursday's close — a revenue beat but profit down about 9% quarter-on-quarter, its FY27 constant-currency growth guidance trimmed at the top end to 1.5-3.0%, and a leadership change, with Ashiss Kumar Dash named to succeed Salil Parekh as CEO from 1 April 2027. The US-listed shares were already down about 5% overnight; the cash-market reaction sets Friday's tone for the whole IT pack.
- **Friday 24 July**: the **RBI Weekly Statistical Supplement** (~5 PM), the first reserves read spanning the fresh oil spike, worth watching for any sign the central bank has been defending the rupee.
- **The Friday backdrop**: Asian markets fell hard overnight into Friday (Korea's KOSPI −4.82%, Japan's Nikkei −2.79%, Taiwan −2.30%) on the same oil shock plus Wall Street's overnight drop. That is the setup India opens into, having fallen far less on Thursday.
- **Saturday 25 July**: **IDFC First Bank (IDFCFIRSTB) Q1 results**, extending the private-bank margin read into the mid-caps.
- **Rolling**: **Iran / Red Sea / Hormuz / the Caspian pipeline**, the highest-tempo variable; any fresh tanker or pipeline hit, or a genuine ceasefire, moves Brent and the rupee before the next open.
- **28-29 July**: the **US Federal Reserve decision**, a read-through for the dollar, US yields and flows into India. **Thursday 30 July**: NSE monthly F&O expiry. **Early August**: the RBI rate decision, now genuinely two-sided with oil back at $100.
