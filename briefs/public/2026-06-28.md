## The Lead

Two global forces moved over India's long weekend, and they pull the Monday open in opposite directions. The tailwind: oil fell again, with Brent dropping to about $72 — a four-month low and an over-10% weekly fall — as tankers moved back through the Strait of Hormuz and Gulf supply normalised. That keeps the cheaper-fuel story that lifted autos and airlines on Thursday running. The headwind is new: a global selling in AI and technology shares (the US Nasdaq fell a fifth straight session Friday, and Asian memory-chip giants Samsung and SK Hynix dropped hard) puts Indian IT as the most exposed corner at Monday's open. The two forces roughly offset, which is why futures point to a flat-to-mildly-higher start rather than a jump.

## Global Setup

Wall Street drifted lower into the weekend rather than broke — the S&P 500 was barely changed Friday (−0.05%), the Nasdaq slipped 0.24% for a fifth straight loss, and the Dow was flat (−0.09%); Europe was the weaker corner (Germany's DAX −1.29%). The bigger story is what drove that drift: a repricing of the AI build-out. Crude kept falling (Brent ~$72), the US 10-year yield eased to 4.37% and the dollar softened. For India, the read is two-sided: cheaper oil and a soft dollar help, but the global tech wobble is a sentiment drag for IT — so the open is muted, not directional.

## Macro & Policy

**The positive side.** Crude is the standing tailwind and it deepened over the weekend — Brent near $72, a four-month low, after Hormuz transits accelerated, Saudi Arabia resumed loading at Ras Tanura, and Gulf exports recovered to roughly three-quarters of pre-war levels. For an economy that imports about 85% of its oil, that keeps the import bill, inflation and the rupee pointed the right way. Friday's easing in the US 10-year yield (to 4.37%) and a softer dollar are a quiet, tactical positive for rate-sensitive stocks. This looks like a one-week move on an in-line US inflation reading, though, not a turn in the global rate path, so it is best read as a tactical positive rather than a pivot.

**The negative side.** The monsoon is still the home-grown worry. June remains on track to be the driest in about 146 years (a rainfall deficit near 46%), even though the rains revived from around 23-June into Maharashtra, Telangana, Odisha and the eastern states. The full-season forecast is held at 90% of normal — below-normal — so the July catch-up is the swing factor for rural demand and food prices.

**What to watch.** The India-US trade round on 22–24 June closed with progress but nothing signed; the 24-July deadline, when the temporary 10% US tariff on Indian goods expires, is the fixed date. The Iran oil licence runs out around 21-August, so the cheap-crude relief has a clock on it.

## The Connections

*How the weekend's global moves set up Monday across Indian sectors — the part most recaps skip.*

**The same cheap oil that helps India still hurts the oil producers.** Brent near $72 lowers running costs for the fuel users — car-makers, airlines, paint firms — and eases the country's import bill, inflation and the rupee. But for an oil producer like ONGC, a lower crude price is exactly what shrinks its revenue, so the upstream names sit on the wrong side of the same move. If autos and airlines lead Monday and ONGC lags, that is this split playing out — and if they don't lead despite four-month-low crude, that non-move would itself be the signal that the market is worrying about demand, not celebrating cheaper input costs.

**The weekend's global tech selling lands on Indian IT, not on the rest of the market.** The trigger was abroad — a tightening in how the AI build-out is funded, which knocked the US Nasdaq and the Asian memory-chip makers. The mechanism into India runs through sentiment: when global technology de-rates, Indian IT services (TCS, Infosys, HCL Tech) tend to move with the global mood even when their own order books haven't changed. So Monday's most likely soft corner is IT, and it is a sentiment read-through from overseas, not anything India did.

**The crude trade rests on one fragile thing: the Strait of Hormuz holding.** The entire cheaper-oil setup assumes Gulf supply keeps flowing. Over the weekend the Iran-US ceasefire frayed — a tanker was hit in Hormuz, the US struck Iranian military targets, and drones reached Bahrain and Kuwait. Crude ignored it and kept falling, betting that supply stays normal. But a real escalation that actually threatens Hormuz shipping is the single biggest risk to the whole cheap-oil trade: it would push crude back up, pressure the rupee, and flip the import-bill relief into an inflation worry. This is the one thing that could turn Monday's calm open into a nervous one.
