## The Lead

On Thursday the Indian market turned cheaper oil into its single biggest trade. The Nifty barely moved (up 0.14% to 24,056), but underneath it the auto pack ran hard — the Auto index rose 2.25%, with Maruti, M&M and the airline IndiGo (+4.66%) all bid up on the logic that cheaper fuel lifts demand and cuts running costs. Then, over the long weekend India was shut, that exact trade came under threat. The US-Iran ceasefire collapsed into open conflict at the Strait of Hormuz — a two-million-barrel tanker was hit, the US struck Iran, and Iran fired back at US bases in Kuwait and Bahrain. So the cheap oil that powered Thursday's rally may reverse at Monday's open: an oil spike would hurt the very autos and airlines that led, and it stacks on top of a weekend global tech selloff that hits Indian IT. Monday opens nervous, not muted, with oil the binary swing variable.

## Global Setup

Two things moved while India was shut, and they point the same risk-off way for Monday. First, oil. Brent fell all of last week as Gulf shipping recovered, closing Friday at $71.99 — its lowest since 27 February, on a de-escalation that held *through Friday*. But that calm broke over the weekend (see Macro & Policy), so Friday's $72 is the last clean print *before* the shock, not a settled level. Second, global technology sold off: the US Nasdaq fell a fifth straight session Friday on a repricing of how the AI build-out is funded, and Asian memory-chip makers Samsung and SK Hynix dropped hard. The US 10-year yield eased to about 4.37% Friday and the dollar softened, which would normally be a small relief for rate-sensitive stocks. For India, the read is no longer two forces offsetting — it is two risks compounding: an oil spike threatens the import bill and the rupee, while the tech wobble pressures IT. The soft dollar is the only crumb on the other side.

## Macro & Policy

**The negative side — the swing variable now.** The cheap-oil tailwind that supported India for weeks is suddenly at risk. Through Friday the story was de-escalation: vessels were openly transiting the Strait of Hormuz again (about 220 verified crossings in the week to 26 June, per Kpler), Saudi Arabia resumed loading at Ras Tanura, and Gulf exports recovered to roughly three-quarters of pre-war levels — which is why Brent closed Friday at a four-month low. Then the ceasefire collapsed *after* that close. On Thursday an Iranian drone hit the container ship *Ever Lovely* near Hormuz; Friday the US struck Iranian targets; Saturday an Iranian drone struck the tanker *M/T Kiku*, carrying more than two million barrels of crude, and the US retaliated against Iranian air-defence, drone-storage and minelaying sites; Sunday Iran's IRGC struck US bases in Kuwait and Bahrain. This is now being called the 2026 Strait of Hormuz crisis. For an economy that imports about 85% of its oil, a renewed Hormuz disruption is the worst macro combination: it pushes up the import bill, feeds inflation, and pressures the rupee, flipping the relief of recent weeks into a fresh worry.

**The positive side — thinning.** Friday's easing in the US 10-year yield (to about 4.37%) and a softer dollar are a small tactical positive for rate-sensitive names, and they read as a one-week move on an in-line US inflation print, not a turn in the global rate path. That is now the only clear support on the board, and it is modest against the oil risk.

**What to watch.** The India-US trade round on 22–24 June closed with progress but nothing signed; the 24-July deadline, when the temporary 10% US tariff on Indian goods expires, is the fixed date. The monsoon remains the home-grown worry: June is on track to be the driest in about 146 years (a rainfall deficit near 46%), though the rains revived from around 23-June into Maharashtra, Telangana, Odisha and the eastern states. The full-season forecast is held at 90% of normal, so July's catch-up is the swing factor for rural demand and food prices.

None of this refreshes until Monday actually trades, and the cohort to watch at the open is now twofold: the autos and airlines that led Thursday (most exposed if oil spikes) and Indian IT (TCS, Infosys, HCL Tech, most exposed to the weekend global tech selloff).

## The Connections

*How Thursday's session and the weekend's escalation set up Monday across Indian sectors — the part most recaps skip.*

**The cheap-oil trade that led Thursday is exactly what the weekend put at risk.** On Thursday the market played falling crude through the *users* of fuel — autos rose 2.25%, IndiGo rose 4.66% — while the oil *producer* ONGC fell 2.87%, because a lower crude price shrinks its revenue. That whole setup rests on one fragile thing: Gulf supply continuing to flow. Over the weekend that assumption broke. A tanker carrying two million barrels was hit in Hormuz, the US struck Iran, and Iran fired on US bases in Kuwait and Bahrain. If oil spikes Monday, the trade inverts: the autos and airlines that led become the names that hurt, and the import-bill relief flips to an inflation worry. So Monday's first signal is the autos — if they hold despite the oil shock, the market is shrugging it off; if they crack, the inversion is live.

**The weekend's global tech selling lands on Indian IT — and it compounds, not offsets, the oil risk.** The trigger was abroad: a repricing of how the AI build-out is funded knocked the US Nasdaq and the Asian memory-chip makers. The mechanism into India runs through sentiment — when global technology gets repriced lower, Indian IT services (TCS, Infosys, HCL Tech) tend to move with the global mood even when their own order books haven't changed. The earlier draft of this brief read these as two forces offsetting each other; they are not. An oil spike is risk-off for India, and a global tech selloff is risk-off for India. Both push the same way, which is why Monday sets up nervous rather than balanced.

**The one piece of relief — easing US yields — is the smallest force on the board.** Friday's softer US 10-year and weaker dollar would, on a normal day, support the rate-sensitive lenders that ran Thursday (Repco, Fusion, M&M Financial) and ease the rupee. But that is a tactical, one-week move on an in-line inflation print, not a regime change, and it is modest against a live Hormuz shock. If the lenders that led Thursday give back ground Monday, it tells you the oil-and-rupee worry is overwhelming the yield relief — that would be the cleanest read on which force is winning.
