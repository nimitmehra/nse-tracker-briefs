## The Lead

Foreign investors have turned buyers of Indian shares again, and that quiet shift matters more than Monday's modest 0.66% Nifty gain. After pulling ₹49,340 crore out of the market in June, one of the heaviest monthly exits in years, foreign portfolio investors bought a net ₹708 crore across July's first three sessions, and today they stayed on the buy side (FII +₹243 crore, DII +₹3,791 crore, both buyers). The turn is small and early, but the direction has flipped, and part of it looks tied to money rotating back toward India while the volatile North-Asia chip trade wobbles. The Nifty 50 closed at 24,430, a fourth straight gain, led by a rate-cut-hopeful bid into rate-sensitive shares, real estate the clear leader.

---

## Global Setup

Monday's backdrop was built over a long weekend: US markets were shut Friday for July 4, so the last Wall Street close India could trade was Thursday's. The weekend's real work came from Asia and oil. South Korea's KOSPI jumped 5.76% on Friday on a Samsung and SK Hynix chip rally, OPEC+ agreed on Sunday to raise August output by 188,000 barrels a day (keeping Brent near $72), and emerging-market equities caught a strong bid (the MSCI EM ETF +3.06%). For India that mix — cheap oil, an EM risk-on, and a chip-trade wobble nudging some capital back this way — set a constructive tone. Monday's US session opened after India had closed (S&P +0.7%, Nasdaq +1.3%); that is tomorrow's input, not today's cause.

## Macro & Policy

**The positive side.** Crude did the heavy lifting. OPEC+'s Sunday supply hike and the continuing recovery of Gulf exports through the Strait of Hormuz held Brent near $72, roughly 26% cheaper than a month ago. That eases India's import bill and cools inflation. Thursday's US jobs report was soft (57,000 jobs added versus 113,000 expected), which took some heat out of the "US rates stay high for longer" fear and fed a rate-cut hope into Indian rate-sensitives — India's own front-end government-bond yields eased on the day (1-year to 5.68%, 2-year to 5.95%), the direct domestic confirmation of that rate-cut bid. India's own June services activity stayed firmly in growth (HSBC Services PMI 57.4), with input-cost inflation at a five-month low, a disinflationary read.

**The worrying side.** The rupee is the honest counterweight: it was little changed on the day (₹95.39, −0.15%) but is down about 1% on the week, soft even with cheap oil and a broad EM risk-on, which points to lingering flow pressure. One correction matters here. Last week's headlines flagged India's forex reserves falling $5.65 billion to $666.93 billion, and that looks like the RBI spending dollars to defend the rupee. It is not. Almost the entire drop is a gold price mark-down (gold holdings fell $5.39 billion; actual foreign-currency assets fell just $150 million). So this is a valuation effect, not the central bank selling reserves. The July monsoon outlook, at about 90% of the long-period average, stays the standing rural-demand watch.

**What to watch.** TCS opens June-quarter earnings on 9 July, the first hard test of the year-long IT slide. June CPI lands around 13-14 July. The India-US trade deal is still unsigned, with a first tranche targeted for mid-July ahead of a hard 24 July tariff deadline, so it must not be read as done.

## The Market

The headline was calm and green, but underneath it money moved into rate-sensitive corners, not a broad surge across everything. The Nifty 50's +0.66% was a fourth straight gain, and the leadership was unambiguous: Nifty Realty +1.81% to a six-month high (up almost 19% over the month), the cleanest expression of the rate-cut-hope trade, as cheaper money and cheaper construction inputs and heavy pre-earnings positioning re-rated the whole property cohort. Auto (+1.36%), Metal (+0.98%) and Energy (+0.77%) firmed; FMCG (+0.20%) and Pharma (+0.47%) barely moved. The laggards tell the story: Nifty IT −0.59% at fresh 52-week lows, PSU Bank −0.88%, Media −0.95%. Bank Nifty (+0.61%) trailed the broad index because the private-bank space split on Q1 business updates: HDFC Bank rose about 3.4% on strong advances and deposit growth, while Kotak Mahindra Bank fell about 4% on a slower deposit read. Note the IT direction carefully: the rupee is down about 1% on the week, and a softer rupee normally *helps* IT (they earn dollars, spend rupees), yet the sector still made fresh lows, which says the pressure on it is fundamental, not currency. India VIX at 11.82 is a very calm, low-conviction advance.

## The Connections

*Behind the numbers — what moved, why, and which signal actually matters.*

**For once, the AI-chip trade's volatility is pushing money toward India, not away.** For months the story was capital draining out of India and into the North-Asia semiconductor boom. This week it ran the other way: KOSPI swung violently (a roughly 10% two-day drop, then +5.76% on Friday), and Tier-1 flow commentary attributes part of India's early-July turn to foreign buyers to that wobble redirecting some capital back this way. It is early and reversible, but it is the mechanism behind the flow turn in The Lead. Watch whether it holds once the Korean chip names settle.

**Indian IT fell even with a weekly currency tailwind at its back, and that is the real signal.** The rupee is down about 1% on the week, and a softer rupee lifts the earnings of companies that bill in dollars, so IT should have had support. Instead it made fresh 52-week lows. The reason is fundamental, not financial: Nomura has pre-marked FY27 sector growth as "anaemic," citing AI pricing pressure and weak US client spending, and the market is pricing that ahead of results. The non-move confirms the stock got cheaper because expectations fell for the business, not because of the exchange rate. TCS on 9 July is the binary test.

**Real estate leading is the cleanest read on rate-cut hope, but the low VIX says nobody is convinced yet.** Cheaper crude, a softer US jobs number, an RBI holding its repo rate, and a 70% jump in institutional money into real estate all point the same way for property developers, and Realty's six-month high reflects it. But the fear gauge sat at 11.82. When a leadership move happens on this little conviction, it is positioning ahead of earnings, not a crowd piling in. The Q1 results will decide if it sticks.

**The rupee is soft, but not because the RBI ran out of dollars.** Last week's reserves drop read like heavy intervention. It was not: almost all of it was gold losing value on the books, with actual dollar assets barely touched. So the rupee's weakness is a flow story (the June selling still working through), not a sign the central bank is spending its defence down. That distinction changes how you read the next weekly reserves update on 10 July: watch the foreign-currency-asset line, not the gold-inflated headline.
