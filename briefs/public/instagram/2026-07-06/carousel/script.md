## Card 1 — Market (what + why)
- numlabel: NIFTY 50
- number: +0.66% to 24,430.35   [brief line 8, 16]   · cls: up
- headline: A fourth straight green day
- why: The index closed higher for a fourth day running, but the strength was narrow, not broad. Money moved into rate-sensitive corners on rate-cut hope, real estate leading at a six-month high, while IT fell to fresh 52-week lows. Banks capped the index because the private lenders split on their Q1 updates, HDFC Bank up about 3.4% on strong loan and deposit growth, Kotak Bank down about 4% on a slower deposit read.

## Card 2 — Top riser (+why)
- numlabel: DIXON
- number: +6.81%   [brief line 80]   · cls: up
- headline: A JV clearance moves it up the chain
- why: Dixon Technologies assembles electronics for global brands, the low-margin end of the business. An inter-ministerial group cleared its proposed joint venture with Vivo, with only the formal government letter now pending, and a foreign brokerage raised its price target to ₹16,200. The market read it as Dixon moving beyond assembly into higher-value components, deeper into the manufacturing chain.

## Card 3 — Sharpest fall (+why)
- numlabel: APOLLO
- number: −5.82%   [brief line 69, 87]   · cls: down
- headline: A dilution plan after a 114% run
- why: Apollo Micro Systems makes defence and aerospace electronics. Its board met to consider a preferential issue of shares and warrants, which raises fresh capital by creating new shares. On a stock already up 114% in three months, more shares means each existing holder owns a smaller slice, so the market read the dilution negatively and sold it down.

## Card 4 — The interesting thing
- kicker: The signal under the rally
- headline: Foreign money turned buyer again
- why: After pulling ₹49,340 crore out of Indian shares in June, one of the heaviest monthly exits in years, foreign investors bought a net ₹708 crore across July's first three sessions, and Monday they stayed on the buy side. Both foreign and domestic funds bought (FII +₹243 crore, DII +₹3,791 crore). The turn is small and early, but the direction has flipped, and part of it looks like money rotating back to India while the North-Asia chip trade wobbles.
