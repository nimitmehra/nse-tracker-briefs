- headline: Foreign buyers came back
- why: After selling for 13 sessions in a row, foreign investors looked to turn net buyers of Indian shares on Tuesday, the first sign the money that left through May may be returning. Cheaper oil pulled them back, and the Nifty rose for a third straight day.

## Card 2 — The teaching idea (why foreign flows matter)
- kicker: Why this matters
- headline: Whose money is moving the market
- why: Two big pools move Indian stocks: foreign investors (FIIs) and domestic funds (DIIs). For weeks the Nifty was held up only by domestic money buying while foreigners sold, which is why it is still down about 8% this year. If foreign buying sustains for a second and third day, that is what turns a relief bounce into a real recovery, because the same selling pressure flips into demand. So the next two days of flow data matter more than any single stock.

## Card 3 — Top sourced riser (+why)
- numlabel: MMTC
- number: +7.91%   [brief line 66, 76]   · cls: up
- headline: A rule re-rated the stock
- why: MMTC is a state-backed trading firm, and it jumped on roughly 338% of its normal volume after gold-import applications were curbed from April 1. With rivals shut out, MMTC is one of the few channels still cleared to import bullion, so a rule that squeezes everyone else made its franchise more valuable. That is a regulatory-privilege re-rating, not an earnings story.

## Card 4 — The other side of cheap oil (+why)
- numlabel: HINDALCO
- number: −3.11%   [brief line 42, 93]   · cls: down
- headline: Peace took aluminium's premium
- why: Hindalco is India's largest aluminium maker, and it was the biggest Nifty 50 faller. The same Hormuz peace that crashed crude and helped oil importers also removed the war-premium that had been propping up aluminium, which fell about 4.4% to its lowest since March. Peace is not uniformly good news. It helps the buyers of oil and pressures the sellers of war-premium metals at the same time.
