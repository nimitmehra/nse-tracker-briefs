## The Lead

The National Stock Exchange (NSE) filed papers for its own stock-market listing on Wednesday, a ₹30,000 crore IPO that values the exchange above ₹5 lakh crore and would be India's largest-ever share sale. The whole deal is an Offer for Sale, so existing shareholders sell about 6% of the company and no new money goes to NSE itself. That filing drove Thursday's session. NSE's listed shareholders and the wider finance crowd, including insurers and asset managers, re-rated on the prospect of cashing in their NSE stakes, and that financials bid offset a falling IT sector to keep the Nifty 50 green for a fifth straight day (+0.34% to 24,168). Rival exchange BSE, which now faces a publicly-traded competitor, fell about 4.3%.

---

## Global Setup

The backdrop that shaped Thursday was last night's US Federal Reserve decision. The Fed, in Kevin Warsh's first meeting as Chair, held rates at 3.50–3.75% — but the message turned hawkish: its rate-path projection (the "dot-plot") now sees rates ending 2026 near 3.8% versus 3.4% in March, dropping the prior expected cut and tilting toward a hike. That lifted the dollar (DXY +0.7% to 100.79) and routed precious metals — gold fell 3.57% and silver washed out harder. After the decision cleared as a known quantity, global equities rallied in relief: S&P 500 +1.08%, Nasdaq +1.91%, KOSPI +2.06%, Taiwan TAIEX +1.28%, and the broad emerging-market equity ETF (EEM) jumped 3.25%. The read for India is mixed: the relief rally was real, but a stronger dollar and higher US rates are a headwind to Indian IT and to foreign inflows — and India captured only part of the bid.

## Macro & Policy

The day's macro story had a clear positive, a clear negative, and one fresh watch-item.

**On the supportive side**, the US-Iran agreement is now in force. A correction to what was reported earlier: there is no Geneva signing ceremony. Both sides signed the 14-point understanding electronically on 17-Jun (US Vice-President J.D. Vance and Iran's parliament speaker Mohammad-Bagher Ghalibaf, with Presidents Trump and Pezeshkian also signing), and Iran's foreign ministry said the deal took effect on 19-Jun, making Friday's in-person ceremony "no longer necessary." Delegations may still meet, but the signing already happened. The deal covers ending hostilities, extending the ceasefire and reopening the Strait of Hormuz. This is why Brent has settled around the $80 line rather than collapsing — the political deal is done, but the physical mine-clearance that reopens the strait still runs a contested 40 days to six months. `[REPORTED]` on the clearance timeline.

**On the worrying side**, the monsoon has stalled. After a slightly late Kerala onset on 4-Jun and a decent spread by mid-month, the India Meteorological Department's advance maps show "hardly any change" between 12-Jun and 18-Jun — the Somali Jet (the wind that drives moisture inland) is exceptionally weak, and progression has been stuck since around 8-Jun. The next advance is forecast only around 23-Jun, with the jet possibly strengthening near 20-Jun. The season call is still "normal despite El Niño," but a mid-June stall on top of an early-season deficit keeps the rural-demand and food-inflation question open into the back half of the year.

**Watch next:** the RBI's Weekly Statistical Supplement lands Friday 19-Jun (~5 PM IST) — the forex-reserves print, worth watching for any large move that would hint at the central bank acting around the rupee near 94.3.

## The Market

It was a fifth straight up-day, but a narrow and lagging one — and the real story sits in the sectors, not the flat headline. The Nifty 50 added just 0.34% to 24,168, the Sensex 0.33%, while the Bank Nifty did better at +0.66%, carried by the same financials bid. Under the surface, the spread between the best and worst sector was tight, about 2.2 percentage points — a low-dispersion grind rather than a violent rotation.

**Leaders.** Healthcare topped the table (Nifty Healthcare +0.98%), with Realty (+0.69%), Financial Services (+0.67%) and PSU Bank (+0.66%) close behind. Two forces explain the leadership. The financials cluster — Financial Services, PSU Bank, Private Bank (+0.49%) — rose on the NSE-IPO value-unlock read-through (covered in The Lead). Healthcare and pharma (+0.56%) caught a defensive bid: with the hawkish Fed lifting the dollar, money leaned toward domestic, rate-insulated sectors that don't earn in dollars.

**The lone laggard.** Nifty IT was the only sector to close red, down 1.19%, and it fell for a clean reason — it took the full force of the hawkish Fed twice over. First, higher-for-longer US rates lower the value the market places on IT's future earnings and squeeze discretionary US tech budgets. Second, a firm rupee against a rising dollar trims the rupee value of their dollar revenue. Infosys (−2.3%), HCL Tech (−1.8%), TCS (−1.3%), Tech Mahindra (−1.3%) and Wipro (−0.9%) all fell together.

## Corporate News

- **NSE (National Stock Exchange)** — the day's defining filing (see The Lead). The IPO is backed by a record 20-bank syndicate (Kotak, JM Financial, Morgan Stanley, JP Morgan, HSBC, Citi, SBI Capital, Axis, ICICI Securities and others), with listing targeted before December 2026. NSE was the world's largest exchange by cash-equity trades and equity-derivative contracts in FY26. The read-through: since the IPO is all an Offer for Sale, the value flows to the shareholders selling down — which is why insurers and asset managers that own NSE stakes were the ones that re-rated. The IPO is still at the draft (DRHP) stage; SEBI's review comes next.
- **Bata India (BATAINDIA)** — appointed **Sanjay Rao**, formerly a senior director at Nike and earlier the builder of Zara's India business at Inditex, as Managing Director and CEO effective 24-Aug-2026, succeeding Gunjan Shah. The market read it as a turnaround signal for a brand that has lagged; the shares jumped 16.42%, reportedly the biggest single-day gain in about two decades. (Move covered in Big Movers.)
- **Redington (REDINGTON)** — rose on reports that Apple may raise product prices as AI demand pushes up memory and storage costs. As a major India distributor of Apple products, higher selling prices lift Redington's distribution revenue. It cuts both ways — the distribution model runs on thin margins and dearer products can dent demand — but the market took it as a positive. (Move covered in Big Movers.)

## The Connections

*How the day's events ripple across industries — the part most market reports skip.*

**One filing re-priced an entire corner of the market without a single rupee changing hands at NSE.** Because the NSE IPO is purely an Offer for Sale, no money flows to the exchange — the value flows to whoever owns NSE shares and can now sell them at a ₹5-lakh-crore valuation. That is why the move showed up not in "exchange" stocks but across insurers and asset managers (New India Assurance +7.98%, listed AMCs +5-6%): the market is marking up everyone holding a stake. The flip side is BSE, down ~4.3% — the only listed exchange in India is about to get a much larger listed rival. Watch which NSE shareholders are named in the final prospectus; that list is the map of who actually unlocks value.

**India under-participated in a global relief rally — and that was the foreign money's choice, not an accident.** The world's equity markets bounced once the Fed decision cleared (EEM +3.25%, KOSPI +2.06%, S&P +1.08%), yet the Nifty added only 0.34% — because foreign investors *sold* ₹855 crore of Indian shares while a strong dollar (DXY +0.7%) capped any foreign bid. Domestic institutions bought ₹3,130 crore and carried the day on their own. This is not capital fleeing India for North Asia; it is India simply not catching the global bid, with the rally resting on local money. The thing to watch is whether the foreign selling deepens or reverses in the coming sessions.

**The metal stocks ignored a falling metal price again.** Industrial metals were soft this week, yet the Nifty Metal index closed dead flat (−0.01%) rather than falling with the commodity. This is a pattern worth knowing: Indian metal *equities* increasingly trade on domestic demand and company-specific stories, not the global commodity move. When you see "aluminium crashed, so sell metal stocks," remember the equity has been decoupling from the commodity for a while now.

**The hawkish Fed found exactly one door into India — and it was IT.** Of fifteen sectors, only IT fell, and it fell for a textbook reason: software exporters earn in dollars and are valued on long-dated future earnings, so higher US rates and a firmer rupee hit them from both sides at once. Everything else — financials, pharma, realty — is domestic-facing and was insulated. The read for the cohort is that on days the Fed surprises hawkish, IT is the cleanest place the pressure lands, regardless of how each company is actually doing.
