## The Lead

The Reserve Bank's window to pull foreign-currency deposits into Indian banks went live on Tuesday, and that is what lifted the market. Under the scheme, the RBI itself absorbs the full cost of hedging fresh three-to-five-year foreign-currency (FCNR-B) deposits that banks raise from non-residents through end-September, and frees those deposits from the usual cash-reserve rules. That cuts banks' funding cost directly, so Bank Nifty jumped 2.09% and the PSU bank index 3.62%, and the financials did all the index lifting. The Nifty 50 closed up just 0.52% at 23,242, a thin bank-led green rather than broad buying. Abroad it was a far bigger day for North Asia's chip stocks, a trade India barely owns, so India sat out most of the rally even as it eked out a gain on its own policy.

## Macro & Policy

The day turned almost entirely on India's own capital-flow policy, which is pulling in two supportive directions at once.

**On the supportive side**, the RBI's foreign-currency deposit package is now operational. The central bank will bear the entire hedging cost on fresh FCNR-B deposits (minimum three-year, maximum five-year) that banks raise from non-residents up to 30 September, and has exempted those deposits from cash-reserve and statutory-liquidity requirements and from banks' open-position limits. In plain terms, the RBI is subsidising the dollar-rupee hedge so banks can offer NRIs sharply higher deposit rates and bring in dollars to steady the rupee. This sits on top of the FPI bond-tax ordinance from June 5, which scrapped the capital-gains and interest-income taxes foreign investors pay on Indian government bonds and was paired with opening long-dated G-secs to unlimited foreign buying. That package pushed the India 10-year yield to a four-week low of 6.90%, down 4.9 basis points on the day, with the five-year down 7.8 bps. One sell-side desk, Jefferies, put a $50-70 billion figure on what the deposit-and-bond measures could attract; treat that as a single-desk magnitude, not a settled number.

**On the worrying side**, the rupee still weakened and foreign investors stayed net sellers of ₹4,566 crore, against domestic funds buying ₹6,159 crore — the policy is the response to capital pressure that has not yet reversed. The monsoon is the standing rural-and-food worry: it set in over Kerala on June 4 and has now advanced into Maharashtra, Karnataka and Andhra Pradesh, but the seasonal forecast is still a below-normal 90% of the long-period average.

**Watch next week:** India's May inflation print on Friday June 12, the first read after this policy package; and the RBI's Friday forex-reserves supplement, which is the cleanest proxy for how hard the central bank is leaning to defend the rupee.

## The Market

The headline gain hides how narrow the day was. The fear gauge collapsed: India VIX fell 8.54% to 15.58, the signature of a relief session rather than a conviction breakout. The signal on flows was the rupee. It weakened even as the dollar softened, and foreign investors were net sellers while domestic funds bought — so the green close was a home-grown policy bid, not money coming back to India.

## The Connections

*How the day's events ripple across industries — the part most market reports skip.*

**India's small gain was a home-made story, and that is the whole point.** Across Asia, capital poured into the North-Asia chip complex — Korea up 8%, Taiwan up 2.8% — on memory-chip optimism. India put up just half a percent, and it did so without that foreign flow: foreign investors were net sellers, the rupee weakened on its own, and domestic funds did the buying. So India's green came from a domestic policy lever (the RBI deposit scheme), not from the global risk-on. The read for an Indian investor is that this AI-hardware rally keeps bypassing the market, because India's tech weight is services, not chips. Watch whether the foreign-bond and deposit measures start turning the FII selling, because that — not the chip trade — is the flow that would lift India broadly.

**One policy lever moved an entire sector, and it shows how this rally is built.** The RBI scheme lowers what banks pay to raise foreign-currency deposits, and the market repriced every deposit-taker at once: PSU banks up 3.6%, private lenders close behind, 13 of 14 Bank Nifty names green. But the relief is specific. Affordable-housing financiers, which do not fund themselves with NRI dollars, sat out — one such name was essentially flat while banks ran. The lesson is that a funding-cost subsidy helps the banks that actually use that funding, and the reader should not assume it spreads to every lender.

**Cheaper crude split the oil chain in the expected direction.** Brent fell about 1.7% on the Israel-Iran pause. That is a cost cut for fuel buyers and a revenue cut for producers, and that is exactly what happened: IndiGo rose on lower jet-fuel costs while ONGC, an upstream producer, fell on weaker crude realisations. No paradox here; it is the clean version of a crude move, useful as a reminder that "lower oil" is good for some Indian names and bad for others.
