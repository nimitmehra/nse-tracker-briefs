## The Lead

India's IT pack led the market lower on Thursday, and the rest of the index drifted red with it: the Nifty 50 closed at 23,161.60, down 0.23%, and the Sensex at 73,832.55, down 0.20%. The selling started overnight, when US tech tumbled (the Nasdaq fell about 2%, with chipmakers Nvidia and Broadcom hit hardest), and Indian IT, which earns most of its money from US clients, tracked it straight down, with Nifty IT off 1.62%, the worst sector. A midday recovery, which briefly took the Sensex back above 74,000, faded after Iran declared the Strait of Hormuz "closed" following fresh US strikes, and crude jumped about 2.5% to roughly $95. Then, after the 3:30 PM close, the picture flipped: the US cancelled the planned strike and signalled a deal, oil crashed back to about $90, and US and emerging markets surged, which sets up tomorrow's May inflation print in a suddenly calmer oil backdrop.

## Global Setup

The backdrop India traded into was mixed and turned on oil. Last night's US close was lower (the Nasdaq off about 2% on a tech-led selloff led by Nvidia and Broadcom), and that came before our open, so it shaped today's session — it is what pulled Indian IT down. During Indian hours, the live worry was the Middle East: Iran declared the Strait of Hormuz "closed" after fresh US strikes, and Brent rose about 2.5% to near $95, the structural negative for an oil importer that capped India's midday bounce. The dollar was firm (DXY 100.28, +0.33%) and gold sat flat at about $4,102 — no broad fear bid. One important caveat on the feed: any data showing Asian markets (Korea, Taiwan, Japan) sharply lower is a stale Tuesday-into-Wednesday print, not today's Asia, so we do not read it as today's driver.

## Macro & Policy

The day was geopolitics-first, but the policy threads kept running underneath.

**On the supportive side**, India's capital-flow package is still feeding through. The RBI's FCNR-B deposit window stays open — the central bank is bearing the full cost of hedging fresh three-to-five-year foreign-currency deposits banks raise from non-residents through end-September — and the June 5 ordinance that scrapped the capital-gains and interest taxes foreign investors pay on Indian government bonds keeps transmitting. There is a first, tentative sign it is working: foreign investors turned small net buyers of Indian debt in June so far (about ₹377 crore), even while pulling money out of equities. The India 10-year yield eased slightly to 6.90%, a small bond-side counter-current against a red equity tape.

**On the worrying side**, the same Middle-East flare-up is the live risk to tomorrow's inflation read. The intraday crude spike feeds straight into fuel costs, and a Reuters poll already sees May CPI rising to 4.0% from 3.48% in April, back to the RBI's 4% target, largely on that fuel pass-through. There is a genuine two-sidedness here: the May data captures the May crude spike, but the post-close oil crash (if it holds) points the *other* way for June, so a hot May number could already be the high-water mark. The monsoon is the standing rural watch-item — it advanced further today, now into parts of Bihar, but the seasonal forecast stays a below-normal 90% of the long-period average.

**Watch tomorrow:** India's May inflation on Friday June 12, the first read that captures the crude spike; and the RBI's Friday forex-reserves supplement, the cleanest gauge of how hard the central bank is leaning to defend the rupee.

## The Market

The modest red close masked a one-sided story underneath: this was an IT problem, not a broad one. Nifty IT fell 1.62%, the worst sector and now down about 5% on the week — it tracked the overnight US-tech selloff because the sector earns in dollars from American clients, so when US tech demand gets questioned, Indian IT trades with it. Against that drag, the defensives held: pharma rose 0.61% and Bank Nifty actually closed green (+0.14%), with private banks the offset that kept the index from falling further. The rupee was the soft spot — it weakened 0.41% to ₹95.75, pressured by the foreign equity selling and the intraday crude spike. The flow pattern was the now-familiar one: foreign investors sold (FII about −₹1,987 crore) and domestic funds absorbed it. A note worth making: IT exporters would normally get a lift from a weaker rupee (they earn dollars), but today the global demand worry overwhelmed that currency math — the sector traded its US read, not its FX read.

## The Connections

*How the day's events ripple across industries — the part most market reports skip.*

**The day's whole Indian story was a single sector borrowing a foreign problem.** Indian IT does not have an India problem — it has a US-client problem. When the Nasdaq fell about 2% overnight on worries about American tech demand, Indian IT fell 1.62% because it sells its services to the same US companies; if those buyers tighten budgets, Indian IT bills less. The read for a reader who owns the large IT names: today's weakness was imported, not domestic, so the thing to track is US tech sentiment, not anything in the Indian economy.

**A weaker rupee should help IT, and today it didn't. The non-move is the signal.** Indian IT earns dollars and spends rupees, so a softer rupee (down 0.41% today) normally pads its margins. Yet IT was the worst sector. That tells you the global demand worry was the dominant force — strong enough to override a currency tailwind that usually shows up. When the cushion that should have softened a fall is overwhelmed, it is a sign the market is pricing the bigger structural worry, not the day-to-day math.

**Tomorrow's inflation number and the oil round-trip pull in opposite directions.** The May CPI print due Friday will reflect May's high fuel costs, so a hot reading near 4% is on the cards. But oil's late round-trip — up during the day's escalation, then down sharply after the post-close de-escalation — points the *other* way for June. So even a high May number may be backward-looking if the crude relief holds, and the same data point is supportive for an oil-importer's bond and currency picture while being a near-term inflation worry. The reader should hold both readings at once rather than treat a single number as the verdict.
