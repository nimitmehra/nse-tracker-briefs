## The Lead

Indian IT cracked on Friday, and it was an imported shock. Accenture — the global bellwether for the outsourcing-and-software-services business that Infosys and TCS live in — cut its full-year revenue-growth guidance and missed on its quarter, and its New York shares fell about 18%, their worst single day ever. That broke before India opened, so it hit our IT names the same session: the Nifty IT index closed down 3.65% after sinking roughly 6% intraday, and dragged the whole market a touch lower to snap a five-day winning streak (Nifty 50 −0.64% to 24,013). The detail that matters for the next few months is buried in Accenture's own commentary: AI demand is strong, but companies are *reallocating* their tech budgets to AI, not *expanding* them. So this reads as a worry about the size of the overall demand pie, not a one-company stumble.

## Global Setup

The global backdrop that shaped Friday's session was one company, not one country. Accenture's results landed after the US close on Thursday and re-priced software-services names worldwide overnight, so India's IT cohort opened already gapped down. Outside that, the picture was risk-off but quiet: gold slipped about 1.2% to $4,173 and silver fell harder (−2%) as money trimmed havens, while base metals washed out separately — LME aluminium fell nearly 6% to $3,475, a two-session unwind that hits metal *makers'* realisations but eases costs for the firms that buy aluminium. Brent ticked up 0.93% to $80.59, holding the $80 line after a heavy week (−7.7%). The one-line read for India: the day was a single global thread — software demand — and India sat on the wrong end of it.

## Macro & Policy

The day's macro story had a clear negative and one supportive cross-current.

**On the worrying side**, the monsoon has stalled at the worst possible moment for the rural economy. The weather office formally flagged the onset of El Niño — the Pacific warming pattern that tends to weaken Indian rains — on 11-Jun, and the rains have gone quiet since: the 1–16 June shortfall is 41% below normal nationally and a steep 62% over central India, the country's main farm belt. Less rain in the sowing window threatens the kharif (monsoon) crop and keeps the food-inflation question open into the second half. The one relief: the forecaster expects the rains to revive in the next four to five days, which would neutralise the scare. This is the single thing to watch for the farm-and-agri-input names.

**On the supportive side**, the oil-disruption premium kept draining out of crude. With the US–Iran understanding taking effect on the 19th, stranded tankers began leaving the Strait of Hormuz and producers lifted their force-majeure declarations — cheaper, calmer oil eases costs for paints, beverages and airlines and supports the rupee. But it is not one-way: the in-person talks were abruptly postponed and fresh fighting flared in Lebanon, which is why Brent actually rose on the day even as it fell sharply on the week.

**Watch next:** the four-day week ahead (markets are shut Friday 26-Jun for Muharram) and the monsoon prints — the cumulative-rainfall numbers, not the seasonal forecast, are what matter now.

## The Market

This was a single-factor day. Strip out IT and the market was essentially flat — fourteen of the fifteen Nifty sector indices closed within about one percentage point of unchanged, and only Nifty IT (−3.65%) moved with any real force, more than three points worse than the next-weakest sector. The classic defensive corners led: Pharma (+0.73%) and Healthcare (+0.74%) caught the flight-to-safety bid you get on an IT-shock day, and Energy (+0.22%) held green on the steadier-crude read.

The more useful story sits *underneath* the flat headline: money rotated hard out of IT and into domestic, rate-sensitive names. With IT effectively un-ownable for the session, cash moved into financials, hospitality and capital-goods stocks — the non-banking lenders ran (Tata Capital +6.2%, Piramal Finance +7.7%, Poonawalla +5.5%), hotels bid (Leela +8.0%, ITC Hotels +6.1%), and the power-grid and engineering names lifted. This rotation does *not* show up at the sector-index level — the broad Financial Services index actually fell 0.57%, because the big private banks in it move with IT-correlated foreign money even as the smaller lenders ran. So the rotation is visible only in the individual names, not the index.

The flows were the day's puzzle, and worth slowing down on. ## Corporate News

- **Jio Platforms (the Reliance digital-and-telecom arm)** — Reliance Industries (RELIANCE) said at its annual meeting on Friday that Jio Platforms has filed draft papers for what would be India's largest-ever IPO, an all-fresh issue of up to ~₹38,000 crore (about $4 billion). Two things make it notable. First, it is *all* new shares with no promoter selling, and the bulk of it — up to ₹27,500 crore — is earmarked to repay Jio's overseas borrowings due in 2028 — so this is a balance-sheet clean-up, not a cash-out. Second, it lands just three days after the National Stock Exchange's own roughly ₹30,000 crore filing (yesterday's Lead), which is the mirror image — a pure offer-for-sale where the exchange itself receives nothing. Two record-breaking filings in a single week point to a heavy second-half supply pipeline; when a lot of new paper hits the market, it competes for the same pool of money and can be a drag on existing shares. There is no clean pure-play to own here, but the NSE sale already lifted **New India Assurance (NIACL)** +13.23% on Friday, since the insurer is a selling shareholder in that offer.
- **VA Tech Wabag (WABAG)** — the water-engineering firm won a desalination contract worth more than ₹1,000 crore in Kuwait, its first entry into that market, and the stock rose to a fresh one-year high (move in Big Movers). A genuine order win on a name already riding the Gulf water-infrastructure theme — though a contract award is future revenue, not money in the bank yet.

## The Connections

*How the day's events ripple across industries — the part most market reports skip.*

**One US company's warning re-rated an entire Indian sector in a single morning — because the cohort trades as that company's proxy.** Accenture is the size-leader the whole offshore-services trade watches; when it says next year's demand is softer, the market assumes the same for Infosys, TCS and the rest, before any of them have reported. So the warning re-rated the cohort at once: Infosys −8.2%, TCS about −6.5%, the mid-caps up to −8%, all on zero company-specific news. The thing to understand is *why* it bit — Accenture didn't say AI demand is weak; it said companies are *moving* their tech budgets into AI rather than *growing* them. That is a worry about the size of the overall pie, which is harder to shrug off than a one-quarter miss. The real test is Indian IT's own July-quarter commentary on deal pipelines.

**The money that left IT didn't leave the market — it rotated, and the index hid where it went.** On a day a whole sector becomes un-ownable, cash doesn't sit idle; it looks for the opposite kind of stock. Friday that meant domestic, rate-sensitive names — non-banking lenders, hotels, power-grid builders — all of which ran 5-8% while IT fell. But the broad Financial Services index still closed *red*, because the large private banks inside it move with the same foreign money that was selling IT. The lesson worth keeping: on rotation days, the sector index can completely mask where the money actually went. You have to read the individual names.

**Foreigners bought shares on a down day — which sounds like a contradiction and isn't.** Foreign investors were net buyers of ₹4,859 crore in the cash market even as the Nifty fell. The trick is that they did two things at once: they *bought* non-IT large-caps (the rotation above) while *selling* about 2.26 lakh Nifty futures as a hedge against a further fall. The index dropped anyway because the selling in the heavy IT names — done by everyone, not just foreigners — outweighed the buying elsewhere. Net cash buying plus a futures short is a cautious, hedged stance, not conviction. Watch whether the cash buying holds once IT stabilises.

## What to Watch Tomorrow

- **A four-session week, then a long weekend** — markets are shut Friday 26-Jun for Muharram, so trading runs Monday 22 to Thursday 25, with the monthly derivatives expiry on Thursday 25. Expect compressed, defensive positioning into the holiday.
- **Indian IT's reaction to Accenture** — watch whether brokerages cut their numbers on the cohort early next week, and whether the sector steadies or keeps falling once the first wave of selling clears.
- **The monsoon prints, next four to five days** — the cumulative-rainfall numbers are the swing variable for the farm and agri-input names after the El Niño flag and the 41% national rain deficit; the forecaster expects a revival.
- **The IPO pipeline** — SEBI's review of both the NSE (₹30,000 cr) and Jio Platforms (~₹38,000 cr) draft filings; two record issues queued for the second half are a real call on secondary-market liquidity.
- **Foreign vs domestic flows** — Friday's split (FII buying cash, DII selling) was unusual; whether it persists tells you who is actually driving this market right now.
