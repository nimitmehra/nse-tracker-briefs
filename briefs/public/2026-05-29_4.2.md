## The Lead

A strange day: the news was good, the market fell. Oil has crashed nearly 20% this month and the rupee firmed over 1% on hopes the US and Iran will extend their ceasefire — both clearly good for India. Yet Indian stocks dropped 1.5%, because that ceasefire deal is still unsigned and a fresh worry landed: the weather office cut its monsoon forecast, reviving fears about food prices.

---

## Global Setup

India opened after a holiday into a calm overnight abroad. US markets edged up (S&P 500 +0.31%, Dow +0.79%), Asia was mixed (Japan's Nikkei −0.47%, Hong Kong's Hang Seng +0.70%), and the US 10-year government bond yield eased to 4.44%. The dollar was flat and gold firmed to $4,554. The dominant global thread remains oil: Brent crude has fallen about 20% over the month on hopes of a US-Iran ceasefire extension. For India — which imports roughly 85% of its oil — that is the single biggest tailwind in months: cheaper imports, a firmer rupee, and relief for refiners. The catch: the deal is reported but unsigned, and strikes reportedly continued this week, so treat it as a hopeful headline, not a settled one.

---

## Macro & Policy

The day turned on two opposing forces. On the supportive side, the Iran situation de-escalated: the US and Iran are reported to have "mostly agreed" a 60-day understanding to extend the ceasefire and reopen the Strait of Hormuz, which crashed oil and lifted the rupee. The rupee's 1.1% jump — while the dollar sat flat — was India-specific, helped by Reserve Bank Governor Sanjay Malhotra's remarks that the central bank "will do whatever is required" and that the rupee "appears undervalued."

On the worrying side, the weather office cut its monsoon forecast to 90% of the long-period average and flagged a rising El Niño risk — a direct threat to the rural economy and food prices. That fear hit fertiliser, agrochemical and consumer-goods shares. One clear policy positive landed for fertiliser makers: the Cabinet approved an 11.6% higher nutrient subsidy (₹41,533 crore) for the Kharif season, cushioning their input costs.

Two events loom next week: the Reserve Bank's rate decision on June 3-5 (a hold is expected; the oil crash has eased the inflation worry that was building) and an India-US trade round on June 1-4, with talk of cutting the US tariff on Indian goods to 18% from an effective ~50%. India's Q4 GDP figure was also due today but had not been published at writing time.

## The Market

It was a broad, orderly sell-off — every headline index red, midcaps falling with the large-caps. The one green corner was IT (+0.6%), led by Tech Mahindra and HCL Tech — a flight-to-safety bid into big, cash-rich exporters on a risk-off day. Note the point often gotten backwards: IT firms earn in dollars, so a *stronger* rupee is actually a mild headwind for them — the green close was defensive positioning, not a currency tailwind. Everything else fell — Metals −2.0%, Autos −2.0%, Consumption −1.9%, Energy −1.6%, FMCG and Pharma both −1.5%.

The day's most revealing signal was the rupee. It strengthened 1.1% even though the dollar was flat — an India-specific move driven by the oil crash and the Reserve Bank's support. Normally a firmer rupee and cheaper oil lift stocks; that they didn't tells you the sell-off was about the unsigned Iran deal and the monsoon scare, not India's own economy. The fear gauge (India VIX) rose 8% but stayed contained — an orderly retreat, not a panic. Foreign investors continued to sell (they've pulled a record ₹1.92 lakh crore this year), with domestic funds absorbing most of it through monthly SIP flows.

## The Connections

*How today's events ripple across industries — the part most market reports skip.*

**The same oil crash that helps India hurts the oil-shippers.** Cheaper crude is great for an importer — lower bills, firmer rupee, relief for refiners and paint makers. But for a company that *earns its money shipping oil*, falling prices mean lower future freight rates. That's why Great Eastern Shipping fell 6% for a third straight session even after a record quarter — the market is pricing the outlook, not the past.

**The monsoon cut and the oil crash pull fertiliser makers in opposite directions.** Two days ago, conflict-driven sulphur costs were the worry for fertiliser firms. Today the picture flipped favourably on inputs — cheaper oil, easing sulphur supply, *and* a Cabinet subsidy hike. But the same day brought the monsoon cut to 90% of normal, which threatens *demand* (weak rain → farmers buy fewer inputs). So Coromandel's costs eased while its demand outlook worsened — and the stock fell to a 52-week low. For this group, watch the monsoon, not the oil price.

**IT was the day's hideout — for defensive reasons, not a currency tailwind.** IT was the only green sector because investors hid in big, stable, dollar-earning exporters on a risk-off day. The point usually gotten backwards: a *stronger* rupee actually hurts IT (they earn in dollars and spend in rupees), so today's firmer rupee was a mild headwind — which makes the green close a pure flight-to-safety move. And the deeper worry is unchanged: that artificial intelligence permanently shrinks the work these firms get paid for. A defensive bid, not a re-rating.

**The jewellers' strong results were built on gold prices, and that prop may wobble.** Recent jewellery results (Sky Gold up 7% today) leaned on rising gold prices lifting inventory values, more than on demand — India's gold-jewellery consumption actually fell 19% last quarter, with a 15% import duty biting. If the Iran de-escalation cools the gold price, that earnings prop weakens. The strong results flatter a softer underlying demand picture.
