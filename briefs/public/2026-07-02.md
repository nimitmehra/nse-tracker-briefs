## The Lead

The rupee slipped to a fresh record low on Thursday, past 95.4 to the dollar, and that quiet move mattered more than the loud one. It fell even though the dollar was weakening worldwide and oil stayed cheap — two things that normally lift the rupee. When a currency drops against a falling dollar, the usual cause is money leaving the country, and that is what showed up: foreign investors sold Indian shares again (a net ₹312 crore), a fifth straight day of outflows that domestic funds keep absorbing. Above that caution, the visible market went the other way — the Nifty 50 rose 0.71% to 24,175.70, lifted almost entirely by a 4.64% bounce in the beaten-down IT index. That was bargain-buying after a brutal fall, not proof the sector's year-long slide is over.

## Global Setup

The backdrop India traded through was quiet and constructive: oil soft (Brent −1.44% to $70.54), gold firm (+1.8% to about $4,142), the dollar broadly weaker (dollar index 100.77, −0.62%). Asian markets were light and a day stale over the holiday-shortened week. The one thing to carry into Friday landed after India shut: the US June jobs report came out early (6 PM IST) because US markets are closed Friday for July 4, and it was read hawkishly. Hiring was weak (57,000 jobs) but wages ran hot (+3.5%), so US bond yields backed up (the 10-year to 4.49%) and US tech fell (Nasdaq −1.37%). That is a next-session input and a mild headwind — not what drove today.

## Macro & Policy

**The positive side.** Cheap crude keeps doing quiet work — Brent under $71 eases the import bill, cools inflation and helps oil-users from paints to airlines to tyres. The bond market agrees the pressure is off: the 10-year government yield eased about 5 basis points to 6.71%, and volatility collapsed (India VIX −7.19% to 12.29), a calm, un-fearful session.

**The worrying side.** The rupee at a record low (see The Lead) is the honest counterweight — it says foreign money is still leaving. And the global rate backdrop turned less friendly after India closed: the weak-headline, hot-wages US jobs report pushed US yields up and kept alive the "US rates stay high for longer" view. That view is one of the forces grinding down Indian IT all year (the sector earns in dollars and leans on free-spending US clients), so the after-close data cuts against the very rate-relief hope IT bought during the day.

**What to watch.** US markets are shut Friday, so India trades thin liquidity and the read-through of that jobs report. The RBI's weekly statistics land Friday evening — watch how hard the central bank is spending reserves to defend the rupee. Then the real test: TCS opens June-quarter earnings on 9 July, and the India-US trade deadline falls on 24 July.

## The Market

The headline gain was modest, but almost all of it came from one corner. Nifty IT jumped 4.64% — the top sector by a mile, all ten members green, Infosys up more than 5%. This was the mirror image of the past few sessions: after four losing days had dragged IT to a three-year low, buyers came back for the bargain. Everything else was muted — Bank Nifty flat, PSU Bank down (−0.43%), the broad market mixed. Realty (+1.45%), Consumer Durables (+1.43%) and Auto (+1.21%) firmed but came nowhere near IT. That narrowness — one sector doing all the lifting while banks sat out — is why a +4.64% IT move became only a +0.71% index. The rupee is the signal to carry: at a record low against a falling dollar and cheap crude, it points to flows leaving, not to anything the session did today.

## The Connections

*Behind the numbers — what moved, why, and which signal actually matters.*

**The rupee, not the rally, was the real signal.** Cheap crude and a falling dollar both argue the rupee should have firmed; instead it hit a record low. When a currency weakens against a weakening dollar, money is leaving the country — foreign investors selling shares (fifth straight day) and the RBI drawing down reserves to slow the fall. The equity green sat on top of this, not because of it. Watch Friday's RBI weekly statistics: a large drop in reserves would confirm how hard the central bank is fighting the slide.

**The IT bounce was a spring uncoiling, not a trend turning.** Four losing sessions had pushed IT to a three-year low; Thursday's +4.64% was the snap-back oversold selling usually produces. Nothing fundamental changed — the three things weighing on IT (slow US client spending, AI eating into outsourcing, high US rates) are intact, and the after-close US data made the rate part worse. The first real test is not the chart but the earnings: TCS on 9 July.

**A narrow bid, not a broad rally.** The clue is who did not move. Banks — the market's biggest weight and the usual engine of a genuine broad rally — sat flat, and PSU banks fell. If Thursday were a real "own everything" session, banks would have led. They didn't. That confirms the day was a targeted bounce in one beaten-down corner, not money returning to Indian equities across the board.
