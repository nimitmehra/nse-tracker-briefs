## The Lead

The oil shock the markets feared all weekend did not arrive. The Gulf flared up over the long weekend, with US and Iranian strikes around the Strait of Hormuz, and India reopened Monday braced for a crude spike. But on Sunday the US and Iran agreed to halt the fighting and talk, so oil rose only about 1% (Brent near $72.80, still a four-month low), the rupee held flat, and the Nifty slipped just 0.46% to 23,946. The real story underneath was a defensive shuffle: money moved into pharma and healthcare and out of IT and autos. A weekend that could have hit India hard ended as a cautious reopen, not a crisis.

---

## Global Setup

The backdrop India traded into Monday was a relief, not a threat. Last night's Wall Street was firm — the S&P 500 and Nasdaq both closed higher Friday-into-the-weekend on the de-escalation news — and the dollar softened a touch (the dollar index eased 0.26%). The one weekend wrinkle that did reach India was Friday's Wall Street wobble in artificial-intelligence stocks, where investors questioned how richly AI names are valued; that mood spilled into a few Indian AI-linked tech stocks on Monday. One thing to read carefully: the big falls in Asian markets you may have seen quoted (Korea's KOSPI, Taiwan, Japan) are Friday 26-June prints from *before* the Sunday peace deal — they describe the weekend scare, not Monday's session, so don't treat them as today's Asian read. The clean read for India: with oil contained and the dollar soft, the global setup removed the worst-case and left India to trade its own internal story.

## Macro & Policy

The day turned on one big relief and two domestic policy threads.

**The positive side — the feared oil shock was defused.** Through the weekend the Strait of Hormuz looked like a real crisis: US and Iranian strikes, tankers hit, US bases fired on. For an economy that imports about 85% of its oil, that is the worst macro combination — a higher import bill, more inflation, a weaker rupee. But on Sunday the US and Iran agreed to halt hostilities and let commercial ships transit the strait freely. So crude reopened only modestly firmer — Brent around $72.80, up roughly 1%, still near a four-month low — instead of spiking. With oil contained, the import-bill-and-rupee channel never fired: the rupee actually closed flat at 94.53. The scare resolved into relief.

**Two policy threads shaped sectors.** First, a clear positive for drug-makers: the United States extended its pause on new pharmaceutical tariffs to 9 July, easing the overhang on Indian generic exporters — that relief drove the pharma and healthcare bid (see The Market). It is a *pause*, though, not a removal — the underlying US tariff timeline still escalates later in the year, so the relief is real but time-boxed. Second, the Delhi Cabinet approved its EV Policy 2.0, effective 1 July: over ₹7,000 crore of incentives, a ₹30,000 subsidy per electric two-wheeler, and a planned ban on new petrol and CNG two-wheeler registrations from April 2028. That single policy split the auto pack in two (see The Connections).

**What to watch.** The India-US trade deal is still unsigned, with the temporary 10% US tariff on Indian goods set to expire on 24 July. And the monsoon remains the home-grown worry: June is on track to be the driest in about 146 years (a rainfall deficit near 41-46%), though the rains have started reviving in pockets. July's catch-up is the swing factor for rural demand and food prices.

## The Market

The headline fall was small — Nifty −0.46%, Sensex −0.48%, Bank Nifty a bit softer at −0.77% — but under it sat a clean defensive split. The green corners were the safe ones: Pharma (+1.03%) led, with Healthcare (+0.94%) close behind, both bid up as money looked for shelter on a cautious reopen and on the US pharma-tariff relief. Capital goods and electrification names also held up well (Schneider, Thermax — see Big Movers), and Metal eked out a small gain (+0.80%). The red side was led by Auto (−2.08%, the worst sector) — but that was a Delhi EV-policy story, not an oil story (see The Connections). IT fell next (−1.07%) on Friday's global AI-valuation wobble carrying into Indian tech, with Media (−1.32%), Oil & Gas (−1.18%) and Realty (−0.90%) the other soft corners.

The most informative thing on the day was a *non*-move: the rupee. On a weekend that started as a Hormuz crisis, the rupee should have weakened on India's oil-import exposure — instead it closed flat at 94.53, because oil never spiked, and the dollar was soft anyway. That flat rupee is the cleanest sign the market read the weekend as a scare that passed, not a shock that landed. The fear gauge (India VIX) ticked up to 13.61 (+4.3%) but stayed deep in calm territory. Foreign investors sold ₹1,350 crore while domestic funds bought ₹2,801 crore — the same pattern of foreign selling absorbed by Indian institutions that has run for months. (One flow aggregator showed FIIs as small buyers; the official NSE cash figure of −₹1,350 crore is the one we use.)

## The Connections

*How Monday's reopen rippled across Indian sectors — the part most recaps skip.*

**The biggest signal today was a non-move, not a move.** On a weekend that began as a Strait-of-Hormuz crisis, the things that *should* have been hit weren't. Oil-marketing companies, the rupee and airlines all carry the import-cost risk of an oil spike — and none of them cracked. The reason is simple: oil never spiked. The Sunday US-Iran halt-and-talks deal capped Brent at about a 1% rise instead of the double-digit jump a real Hormuz closure would have caused. So the rupee held flat and the import-bill worry never fired. When the feared chain doesn't fire, the non-event is the story — and it tells you the market judged this a scare that passed.

**The worst-performing sector fell for a policy reason, not an oil reason.** Nifty Auto dropped 2.08% and looked like the day's casualty, but pull it apart and it splits cleanly in two. Delhi's EV Policy 2.0 — a fat subsidy on electric two-wheelers plus a planned ban on new petrol and CNG two-wheelers from 2028 — sent electric-scooter maker Ather Energy up 8.5%, while the legacy petrol-engine makers and their parts suppliers sold off. The index number is the legacy-auto leg of that split. So the read isn't "autos got hit by fuel costs" — it's "policy is picking winners inside the auto pack, and the market is repricing who's on which side."

**The global tech selloff hit single Indian names, not the whole sector.** Friday's Wall Street wobble in AI stocks did reach India — but narrowly. Netweb, an AI-server maker trading at about 126 times earnings, fell 9.9% because an expensive, AI-linked name is exactly what gets sold when AI valuations are questioned (a fund-raise announcement made it worse). MTAR fell on a paused US AI data-centre project hitting one of its customers. But the biggest IT faller, Persistent down 11%, was its *own* M&A story — a Nagarro takeover — not the sector. The lesson: when a global theme arrives, check whether it hit the sector or just a few exposed names. Monday, it was the latter — Indian IT fell only about 1% as a whole.
