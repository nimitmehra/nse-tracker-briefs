## The Lead

The biggest news yesterday came from oil, not Indian shares. Brent crude fell 6% on hopes the US and Iran will extend their ceasefire and reopen a key shipping route. Because India imports most of its oil, cheaper crude helps the country. Indian shares themselves barely moved — but underneath that calm, money shifted sharply out of IT and into metals.

---

## Global Setup

Indian markets opened on 27 May after a steady night abroad. US markets had risen the day before — the tech-heavy Nasdaq gained over 1% — while Asian markets were mixed and Europe was flat. The US 10-year government bond yield, a key global interest-rate signal, held near 4.5%, and the dollar was slightly weaker. But the move that mattered most for India happened during its own trading day: Brent crude fell 6% to $93 a barrel on reports the US and Iran are close to extending their ceasefire and reopening the Strait of Hormuz, a vital oil route. India buys about 85% of its oil from abroad, so cheaper crude is good for its import bill and its refiners, and bad for its own oil producers. One caution: this is talk, not a signed deal — treat it as a hopeful headline, not a done thing.

## Macro & Policy

Two pieces of domestic news set the backdrop. First, inflation: prices rose 3.48% in April from a year earlier — the highest in a year, but lower than the 3.8% economists expected, so still mild. Second, the Reserve Bank of India meets on 3-5 June to decide interest rates. With inflation contained but oil prices jumpy and the rupee weak, economists expect the Bank to hold rates steady at 5.25% rather than cut. Separately, the weather office expects a slightly below-average monsoon this year. That matters because India's farm economy — and the companies that sell to it, like fertiliser, tractor and consumer-goods makers — depends on the rains.

A trade note worth tracking, not acting on: India and the US are reported to hold another round of trade talks on 1-4 June, with talk of cutting the US tariff on Indian goods from 25% to 18%. This is government rhetoric until a signed agreement actually lands.

## The Market

India's main index, the Nifty 50, ended flat — but that calm hid a big shift in where money went. Metal shares jumped to their highest in a year: Hindalco rose 4.2%, Tata Steel 2%, helped by strong global demand for metals. At the same time, IT shares fell to a 22-month low. Banks were soft, with HDFC Bank down 2.6% — the single biggest drag on the index.

The oddest signal came from the rupee. It weakened even though oil, India's biggest import, got cheaper — and normally cheaper oil strengthens the rupee. When the rupee falls anyway, it usually means foreign investors are pulling money out of Indian shares. The data backed that up: foreign institutions sold ₹2,408 crore of shares, while Indian institutions bought ₹1,361 crore. Market fear eased, with the volatility gauge (India VIX) down 7%.

## Earnings

Several companies reported strong results and saw their shares fall anyway. In most cases the reason is the same: the good news was already expected, so investors who had bought in earlier used the results as a chance to take profits.

- **Carraro India** (tractor parts) reported quarterly profit up 76% — and the share fell 8.8%. The stock had climbed for months in anticipation; when results merely confirmed what buyers expected, sellers outnumbered them.
- **Pondy Oxides** (India's largest lead recycler) posted record profit (+111%) and announced a share split, yet fell 6.4%. It trades at a high price (about 30 times earnings) for a business whose profits swing with metal prices — so a record quarter at a rich valuation invited profit-taking.
- **Great Eastern Shipping** fell 8.4% for a more telling reason. It earns its money shipping oil — and the same crude collapse that helped India means lower future shipping rates. Its record quarter is already in the past; the outlook just got worse. This is the one case where the market is looking ahead, not back.

One company fell for a genuinely worse reason. **Transrail Lighting** (which builds power-transmission towers) fell 5.3% — and unlike the others, its actual quarterly profit dropped 24%, a real decline rather than profit-taking. There is a second concern worth flagging: its auditor disclosed, in the annual report, that the Income Tax Department searched the company's offices and some directors' homes in March. The company's own results announcement never mentioned it. No money was seized and the accounts are clean — but a company staying silent about a tax raid is worth noting.

Beyond the names above, the day's other large results included engineering major **Siemens** (a big dividend), **JK Tyre** (profit up 80%), and online retailer **FirstCry** (losses narrowing). State oil producer **ONGC** fell 4.7% on a weak quarter — production down 6% and profit well below estimates — which fits the cheaper-oil story above.

## The Connections

*How yesterday's events ripple across different industries — the part most market reports skip.*

**Cheaper oil does not help fertiliser companies — it may hurt them.** You would think a 6% drop in oil cuts costs for everyone. But fertiliser makers like Coromandel and Chambal don't run on oil — their key raw material is sulphur. And sulphur is getting *more* expensive, because China has banned exports and the Iran conflict is disrupting supply. So while the rest of the market cheered cheaper crude, the cost pressure on fertiliser makers actually went up. For this group, watch sulphur prices, not oil.

**The Middle East war now has a price tag for Indian engineering firms.** Several Indian companies that build power lines and infrastructure — Engineers India, Power Mech, Transrail — do work in the Middle East. Until now the risk from the conflict was vague. This week a peer company, Kalpataru Projects, put a number on it: a ₹200-300 crore hit to one quarter's revenue, from 10-11% of its order book being in the war zone. That gives a real sense of what the others may face if the conflict widens.

**A weaker monsoon helps some companies and hurts others.** The forecast is for slightly below-average rain — and that is not all bad. For a drinks company like Varun Beverages, a hotter, drier summer means people buy more cold drinks for longer. But for fertiliser and pesticide makers like Dhanuka and Coromandel, weak rain can mean farmers plant less and buy fewer inputs. The full picture won't be clear until end-July, when we see how much rain has actually fallen.

**IT shares are falling for a deeper reason than one bad quarter.** India's IT index hit a 22-month low. This isn't about a single weak result — investors are worried that artificial intelligence will permanently reduce the work these companies get paid for. The industry itself estimates AI could shrink its revenue by 2-3% a year, affecting up to 40% of the services it sells. So for a stock like Infosys, the question has changed from "how fast will it grow" to "is it cheap enough given the decline" — a very different debate.
