## The Lead

India scrapped the capital-gains and interest-income taxes that foreign investors pay on its government bonds on Friday, an ordinance aimed at pulling overseas money into Indian debt. The rupee firmed on the expectation of those inflows, even as the dollar rose against other currencies. The change landed on a rough global day, with Asian markets selling off hard on the AI-chip trade, but Indian shares closed nearly flat (Nifty −0.21%), shielded by the policy and by India's small exposure to that trade. The RBI held rates steady and the economy grew 7.8% last quarter, ahead of expectations, though the RBI cut its growth forecast to 6.6% and raised inflation to 5.1% for the year ahead.

## Global Setup

The global backdrop was a rates-driven, not fear-driven, risk-off — and its *timing* matters for reading India. Through Friday's Asian session the AI-chip complex was already under pressure, concentrated in North Asia's chip makers — KOSPI −5.5%, Taiwan TAIEX −1.3%, with Samsung and SK Hynix down hard. The heavier leg came *after* India closed: the US May payroll print landed at 6pm IST too strong — 172,000 jobs versus ~85,000 expected — so the market scrubbed its rate-cut bets, sending the US 10-year yield up to 4.54% (the 30-year breached 5%). Higher discount rates hit the longest-duration, most expensive part of the market hardest, and the Nasdaq fell 4.2%, the S&P 2.6%, with US-listed EM equity ETFs off more than 6%. The dollar firmed (DXY +0.66%) and gold actually *fell* 3.1% — a tell that this was a yields-up unwind, not a panic into safety. For India, the read is the inverse of recent months: the AI trade that had been pulling money *out* of India was itself the epicentre this time. India was insulated by composition through Friday's Asian-hours leg — and the post-close US leg is what Monday's open has to absorb.

## Macro & Policy

Friday was an unusually dense policy day in India — three pre-known events landed in one morning, against that violent global tape.

**On the supportive side**, two strong reads. GDP surprised up — the economy grew 7.8% in the March quarter against a ~7.2% forecast (FY26 at 7.7%, the fastest in two years), so growth is not the worry it was feared to be. The bigger structural mover was the government's Income-tax Ordinance exempting foreign investors from both capital-gains AND interest-income tax on Indian government bonds, backdated to April 1, and removing three caps on foreign holdings. The aim is plain: foreigners hold only ~3.3% of eligible government bonds, so there is huge headroom, and the package is meant to pull stable foreign money into Indian debt and steady a rupee down ~7% this year.

**On the worrying side**, the RBI itself sounded cautious. It held the repo rate at 5.25% (unanimous, neutral) but *raised* its FY27 inflation forecast to 5.1% and *trimmed* its growth projection to 6.6%, flagging a likely deficient monsoon and elevated energy prices. A higher-inflation-plus-lower-growth read pushes back any near-term rate cut.

## The Market

Under the flat headline, the Indian tape was quietly two-sided. Banks led the green corner — Bank Nifty +0.35%, PSU Bank +0.48% — because the foreign-capital package and the held rate read straight through to lenders. Realty (+0.56%), Pharma (+0.29%), FMCG (+0.18%) and Auto (+0.08%) were the steady domestic corners that absorbed the global shock. On the red side, IT fell 0.99% — a sympathy move with the chip-led weakness running through Asian hours (the heavier US leg only hit after the close), and contained anyway because Indian IT sells services, not chips — and Metal was the worst sector at −1.60%, dragged by the global precious-and-base-metals washout on the stronger dollar (gold −3.1%, silver −6.6%, copper −3.8%).

The signal worth reading was the rupee. It firmed 0.88% to ₹94.95 *even as the dollar rose* against the euro, pound and Australian dollar — so this was an India-specific bid, traceable to the GDP beat and the bet that the new bond-tax package brings overseas money in. The bond market agreed: the belly of the yield curve rallied hard (1Y −12.6 bps, 5Y −15.6 bps). India VIX eased to 15.79 — no nerves around a session that got the policy outcome it expected.

## Corporate News

Q4 reporting season is behind us, so no marquee result landed Friday (the Earnings scorecard is omitted for this session). But the post-close filing window carried real developments worth flagging:

- **Hindustan Zinc (HINDZINC)** — the day's principal company story. The stock fell ~4-6% on Bloomberg and Business Standard reports that the government (DIPAM) is weighing a 2% stake sale (~₹5,000 crore) in June-July. The exchanges sought a clarification; the company replied it "does not have any information" — believable, because a government stake sale is the majority-owner's decision, not the company's. What makes it more than a one-day wobble is the cluster: in eight days HINDZINC has had its CEO's tenure extended only two months with a new CFO, an ED search at its premises over a foreign-exchange matter, and now this stake-sale overhang. Three negative chapters in a week; parent Vedanta also fell ~5%. Treat the stake-sale itself as reported, not confirmed.
- **Allied Blenders & Distillers (ABDL)** — Managing Director Alok Gupta is stepping down a few months early, but the company named his successor (Amar Sinha, for a three-year term) at the same time and had disclosed the board decision three weeks ago. An orderly, pre-announced succession rather than the abrupt-exit pattern that usually signals trouble.
- **KP Green Engineering (KPIGREEN)** — the promoter filed his routine annual pledge declaration; the exact encumbrance numbers couldn't be read off the filing (a scanned document), so it is recorded as a calendar-driven annual filing pending the figures, not an event.

## The Connections

*How the day's events ripple across industries — the part most market reports skip.*

**The same package that lifted the rupee lifted the banks — and the bond curve.** The government's bond-tax exemption and the RBI's foreign-access measures are aimed at the rupee, but the read-through runs straight to lenders. More stable foreign money in Indian bonds means easier funding and a firmer currency — both good for banks, which is why Bank Nifty and PSU Bank were green while the index slipped, and why the belly of the yield curve rallied 12-16 bps. The test next week is whether the inflows show up in the actual bond-flow data, not just the announcement.

**India's flat close masked how hard the world fell, and the decoupling test isn't over.** It is easy to read a −0.21% close as a quiet day; it was the opposite. Through Asian hours Korea fell 5.5% and the AI-chip trade was already selling off, yet India barely moved — it carries little of that semiconductor cycle and had three home cushions firing at once. The catch: the heaviest leg of the rout — the Nasdaq −4.2%, EM ETFs off 6% — landed *after* India closed, so Friday only proves India held up against the Asian-hours move. Whether it also shrugs off the bigger US leg is Monday's question; GIFT Nifty's −0.2% points to a near-flat start rather than a catch-down, but that is the real test. The broader read-through stands: when a global wobble is rates-and-tech-led, India's lack of chip-cycle exposure is a feature, not a bug.

**The metals fall was a price story, not a demand story.** It is tempting to tie Metal's −1.60% to the RBI's trimmed growth forecast. The bigger driver was simpler: the stronger dollar and rates-up move crushed gold (−3.1%), silver and copper globally, and Indian metal stocks track those prices. The forward-demand worry is real, but Friday's move was mostly the commodity-price tide — and the same gold crash hit jeweller Sky Gold's inventory marks.
