## The Lead

The big twin event the whole week had waited for finally landed Friday morning — and the market's verdict was a shrug, not a celebration. The Reserve Bank held its rate at 5.25% as expected, and GDP came in surprisingly strong (7.8% for the March quarter, beating forecasts). Yet the Nifty closed down a hair (−0.21%). The real story is underneath: banks rose while IT and metals fell, and the rupee bounced off a near-record low. The RBI paired its hold with a package to pull foreign money back into India — which the banks liked — while a worrying cut to its own growth forecast hurt the metal names.

## Global Setup

The one thing that did bite Friday's session here was IT, tracking overnight weakness in US semiconductor shares — Indian IT firms get re-priced in sympathy with the global tech mood. ## Macro & Policy

Friday's session turned entirely on the 10 AM policy event, and it split cleanly.

**On the supportive side**, two strong reads. First, GDP surprised to the upside: the economy grew 7.8% in the March quarter against a ~7.2% forecast, and full-year FY26 came in at 7.7% — the fastest in two years. Growth is not the worry it was feared to be. Second, and the bigger market mover, the government and RBI jointly rolled out a package to attract foreign capital. The government issued an Ordinance exempting foreign institutional investors from capital-gains AND interest-income tax on Indian government bonds, backdated to April 1, 2026. The RBI added five measures widening foreign access — opening long-tenor bonds to overseas buyers, removing investment caps, and easing NRI equity buying and foreign-currency deposits. The aim is plain: stem the rupee's slide (down about 7% this year, among the worst emerging-market currencies) by pulling stable foreign money into bonds. The rupee bounced on the news.

**On the worrying side**, the RBI itself sounded cautious. The hold was unanimous with a neutral stance, but it *raised* its FY27 inflation forecast to 5.1% and *trimmed* its GDP growth projection to 6.6%, citing higher crude and global uncertainty. A higher inflation read plus a lower growth read together pushed back the hope of a rate cut later this year — and that is exactly what hurt the metal stocks, which trade on the industrial-demand outlook.

## The Market

It was a quietly two-sided session under a flat headline. Banks led the green side — Bank Nifty +0.35%, PSU Bank +0.48% — because the foreign-capital package and supportive stance read straight through to lenders, who benefit when capital flows in and funding stays easy. Realty (+0.56%) firmed for the same reason. On the red side, IT fell 0.99% on the overnight US chip-stock weakness, and Metal was the worst sector at −1.60%, hit by the RBI's lowered growth forecast denting the industrial-demand outlook.

The signal worth reading was the rupee. It strengthened about 0.4% to ₹95.79, bouncing off ₹96.16 — close to a record low — the day before. That reversal traces directly to the foreign-capital package: the market is betting the tax exemption and the RBI measures will bring overseas money back into Indian bonds, which supports the currency. India VIX eased to 15.79 — no nerves around the big event, which fits a market that got the hold it expected.

## The Connections

*How the day's events ripple across industries — the part most market reports skip.*

**The same policy package that lifted the rupee lifted the banks.** The government's tax exemption on foreign bond holdings and the RBI's five access measures are aimed at the rupee, but the read-through runs straight to lenders: more stable foreign money in Indian bonds means easier funding and a firmer currency — both good for banks, which is why Bank Nifty and PSU Bank were green while the broad index fell. The watch-item next week is whether the promised inflows actually arrive in the bond data, not just in the announcement.

**A strong GDP print did NOT lift metals — because the RBI's own forecast pointed the other way.** It looks odd: the economy grew 7.8% last quarter, yet Hindalco was the worst large-cap of the day. Metals trade on the *forward* demand outlook, and the RBI just trimmed its growth projection to 6.6% and raised its inflation read — a signal of slower industrial demand and a pushed-back rate cut. The backward-looking GDP win lost to the forward-looking RBI caution. The same funding-channel relief, conversely, is part of why rate-sensitive realty held green while the broad index slipped.
