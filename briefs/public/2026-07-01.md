## The Lead

India and the United States are within touching distance of an interim trade deal, and Indian shares rose on the hope of it on Wednesday. Commerce Minister Piyush Goyal calls the talks "very close," and the US ambassador says only about 1% is left. But nothing is signed, and a hard deadline looms: the temporary 10% US tariff on Indian goods expires on 24 July. That optimism, together with cheaper oil and record June tax collections, pushed the Nifty 50 up 0.59% to 24,005.85 and snapped a two-day losing run. Underneath the green, one corner kept falling: the IT index dropped another 2.01% to a fresh three-year low.

---

## Global Setup

The backdrop India opened into on Wednesday was calm. GIFT Nifty, the futures that hint at the likely open, pointed up about 0.3% before the bell, and the overnight US close was mixed. During India's own hours the Asian read was light and a day stale over a regional holiday, with Korea, Japan and Taiwan sitting green from Tuesday. Crude stayed soft (Brent −2.19% to $71.32), gold firmed to about $4,094, and the dollar was broadly flat (dollar index 101.4). The one thing to carry into tomorrow: after India had closed, US bond yields backed up hard, the 10-year to 4.47%, and the big emerging-market equity fund (the EEM ETF) fell 2.32%. That is an overnight, next-session input, not what drove today. India, notably, had already closed green against that weaker emerging-market backdrop.

## Macro & Policy

Wednesday, the first day of the month, brought a cluster of fresh Indian data, and the balance leaned supportive.

**The positive side.** Cheaper oil is now doing visible work: Hindustan Unilever said its input-cost pressure should ease as crude retreats, which is part of why consumer-goods shares led. June's goods-and-services-tax collection hit a record ₹1,94,812 crore, up 13.9% on a year earlier, though the strength was import-led (import GST rose 34.6% while domestic GST grew a softer 6.5%), so the headline flatters the home economy a little. Factory activity stayed in expansion, with the final June manufacturing PMI at 54.2 (any reading above 50 means growth), even if that was among the weakest improvements since mid-2022. June car and two-wheeler dispatches from the big makers were strong.

**The worrying side.** The monsoon is the standing risk. June closed roughly 40% short of normal rain, with central India near 50% short, a direct threat to rural incomes and food prices. Separately, the global rate backdrop turned less friendly: US bond yields rose again, keeping alive the view that US interest rates stay high for longer, which is one of the forces weighing on Indian IT (it earns in dollars and leans on free-spending US clients).

**What to watch next week.** The weather office has flagged a Bay-of-Bengal system around 3 July as the trigger for July rains to catch up. US jobs data lands Friday 3 July, the RBI's weekly statistics the same evening, and the India-US trade deadline falls on 24 July.

## The Market

The headline gain was modest (Nifty +0.59%, Sensex +0.58%, Bank Nifty +0.85%), but the story sat beneath it in the gap between sectors. Money moved out of technology and into domestic-facing shares. Realty led hard (+3.58%), with consumer goods (FMCG +2.08%), Media (+2.07%), Consumption (+1.54%) and Auto (+1.15%) all firm, while IT alone fell (−2.01%) to a three-year low. That is a spread of more than five percentage points between the best and worst corners on a day the index barely rose, so the small green number understates how two-speed the session was.

The rupee slipped 0.47% to 95.24 a dollar, and that weakness was India-specific rather than a strong-dollar story: the dollar index was flat and the pound and Australian dollar firmed against it. Foreign investors sold ₹1,140.5 crore while domestic funds bought ₹3,159.2 crore, the same pattern of local money absorbing the foreign exit that has run for months. The fear gauge (India VIX) eased to 13.24, so there was no anxiety under the surface.

## Earnings

No marquee results to score. India is between seasons, with the finished March quarter behind it and the June (Q1 FY27) season not yet open. The one earnings-related filing on Wednesday's feed, from Finolex Industries (FINPIPE), was a routine reply to a clarification on already-published numbers, not a fresh result, and the stock barely moved. The season proper begins with Tata Consultancy Services (TCS) on 9 July, and given the IT weakness that print is now the calendar's most-watched event; Infosys follows on 23 July.

## The Connections

*Behind Wednesday's session: what moved, why, and which sectors it points to next.*

**IT fell even though the currency should have helped it, and that is the signal.** A weaker rupee normally lifts dollar-earning IT exporters, because each dollar of billing converts to more rupees. On Wednesday the rupee weakened 0.47% and IT still dropped to a three-year low. When a sector ignores its own tailwind, the weakness is about demand, not the currency. The worry is structural: soft guidance from Accenture, growing evidence that artificial intelligence is doing the routine work these firms used to bill for, and US rates staying high. KPIT's warning of its first revenue fall in 23 quarters put a single name to it. The first real test comes when TCS reports on 9 July.

**Realty rose even though global borrowing costs went up, which tells you this was a home-grown move.** Property shares are usually sensitive to interest rates, so a jump in US bond yields (the 10-year to 4.47%) should have weighed on them. Instead Realty led the market up 3.58%. The read: this was a domestic story, driven by risk appetite, the RBI's cumulative rate cuts and housing demand, not a global cost-of-money story. The imported yield pressure simply did not bind here.

**Cheap crude stopped being a theory and showed up in a company's own words.** Brent is down about 30% over the June quarter, and on Wednesday Hindustan Unilever said its input costs should ease as oil falls. That comment is part of why consumer-goods shares led. The read-through runs wider than one company: the same relief flows to oil-marketing firms, paint-makers, tyre-makers and airlines, and it eases the inflation the RBI watches. Worth tracking as the June-quarter results roll in.

**India closed green while emerging markets fell, but that is relative strength, not money leaving India.** After India's close, the big emerging-market equity fund fell 2.32% on the US yield jump, while the Asian numbers in the data were a day stale over a holiday. So Wednesday was a home-driven Indian up-day standing apart from a pressured emerging-market crowd, an India-favourable read for now. The one caution: if that emerging-market weakness carries into Thursday's Indian session, it becomes a live drag rather than an overnight footnote.

## What to Watch Tomorrow

- **Thursday 2 to Friday 3 July, the monsoon-revival window.** A Bay-of-Bengal low-pressure system is expected around 3 July, the flagged trigger for July rains to make up June's roughly 40% deficit. The single biggest swing factor for rural demand and food prices.
- **Friday 3 July, US jobs data (non-farm payrolls).** India trades the US-rate read-through, and it matters more after Wednesday's yield back-up and the IT worry tied to US rates.
- **Friday 3 July, RBI Weekly Statistical Supplement (about 5 PM).** Shows the forex-reserves draw and any currency intervention, worth watching with the rupee at 95.24.
- **Wednesday 9 July, TCS Q1 FY27 results** (after market hours), the first hard test of whether the IT sell-off is justified. Infosys follows on 23 July.
- **Thursday 24 July, the India-US trade-deal deadline,** when the temporary 10% US tariff on Indian goods expires. Still unsigned.
- **Emerging-market shares** fell 2.32% overnight (the EEM fund); watch whether that pressure carries into Thursday’s Indian flows.
