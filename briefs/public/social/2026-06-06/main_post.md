India did something unusual on Friday. It scrapped two taxes that foreign investors pay on its government bonds.

Until now, foreigners owning Indian government debt paid tax on the interest they earned and on any gain when they sold. The government removed both, backdated to April, and lifted the limits on how much foreigners can hold.

The aim is plain. Overseas investors own barely three percent of these bonds today. The room to bring money in is large, and that money would steady a rupee that has fallen about seven percent this year.

It landed on a rough global day. Chip stocks were being sold hard across Asia, and a strong US jobs report later pushed up American bond yields. Indian shares closed nearly flat anyway, helped by this policy and by two more supports the same morning, a steady interest rate from the Reserve Bank and growth of 7.8 percent last quarter.

A country cuts taxes to win something it wants. Watch whether the foreign money actually arrives.
