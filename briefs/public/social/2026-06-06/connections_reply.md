A flat close hid how hard the rest of the world fell on Friday.

During Asian trading hours Korea dropped five and a half percent as chipmakers were sold, and India barely moved. The reason is what India does not own. It carries very little of the semiconductor cycle that was being unwound.

But the test is not over. The heaviest part of the fall came after India had closed, when a strong US jobs report sent American bond yields up and the Nasdaq down more than four percent. Indian shares never got the chance to trade that leg.

So Friday only proves India held up against the lighter move. Monday is when it has to absorb the harder one.

The lesson holds anyway. When a global wobble is driven by interest rates and technology, India's lack of chip exposure is a help, not a weakness.
