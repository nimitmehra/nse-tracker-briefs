The clearest signal on Monday was something that did not happen.

A real closure of the Strait of Hormuz would have hammered three things first: India's oil-marketing companies, which buy crude in dollars and import most of it, and the airlines, whose biggest cost is jet fuel. On a weekend that began as a Gulf crisis, all of them should have been hit hard.

None of them cracked. The reason is simple. The shock never came, because the Sunday halt-and-talks deal capped crude at a small rise instead of the double-digit jump a closed strait would have forced.

When the chain you fear doesn't fire, the non-event is the read. The companies most exposed to an oil spike sitting quiet is the market telling you, in its own way, that it judged the weekend a scare that passed.
