A few cross-currents from Thursday worth thinking about.

The rupee, not the rally, was the real signal. Cheap oil and a falling dollar both argue it should have firmed, but it hit a record low instead. When a currency weakens against a weakening dollar, money is leaving, and it showed up as a fifth straight day of foreign selling plus the central bank drawing down reserves to slow the fall. The green equity close sat on top of that, not because of it. Friday's RBI weekly reserves figure will show how hard that fight is.

The IT bounce was a spring uncoiling, not a trend turning. Four losing sessions had pushed the sector to a three-year low, and Thursday's jump was the snap-back oversold selling usually produces. Nothing underneath changed, and the after-close jobs data made the high-rates part worse. The first real test is the earnings, when TCS reports on 9 July.

One last tell was who did not move. Banks are the market's biggest weight and the usual engine of a broad rally, and on Thursday they sat flat while PSU banks fell. A genuine market-wide session leads with the banks. This one didn't, which marks a targeted bounce in one beaten-down corner, not money returning across the board.
