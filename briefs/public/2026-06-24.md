## The Lead

Crude oil fell another 4.5% on Wednesday to about $74 a barrel, down nearly 29% in a single month, as the Iran crisis turned from a war scare into actual supply: the United States granted Iran a 60-day licence to sell its oil again, tankers resumed moving through the Strait of Hormuz, and Gulf producers began raising output. For India, which imports about 85% of the oil it uses, this is the cleanest macro win there is. Cheaper crude lowers the import bill, takes pressure off inflation, and supports the rupee, all at the same time. That is why the rupee actually strengthened today even as the US dollar rose, why bond yields fell, and why the Nifty recovered 0.83% to 24,021, erasing Tuesday's selloff. The 0.83% on the index will be forgotten by next week; oil at a three-month low changes the country's economic maths for months.

## Global Setup

The global tech panic that hammered markets on Tuesday eased today. South Korea, which was the epicentre of the selloff, bounced back hard: its main index, the KOSPI, rose 3.3% after Samsung announced a huge share buyback and the chipmaker SK Hynix sped up a big US listing. Japan slipped slightly. India traded with that Asian recovery rather than the lingering tech worry, and added its own oil tailwind on top. One detail worth holding onto: the US dollar actually rose against most major currencies today, yet the rupee still gained. That only happens when something India-specific is at work, and here it was the oil collapse improving India's external position directly.

## Macro & Policy

**The positive side — oil, and what it pulls with it.** The crude collapse (see The Lead) did more than help the import bill. Cheaper oil lowers the expected path of inflation, and when inflation worries ease, bond yields fall. India's 10-year government bond yield slipped to about 6.79% and the shorter end fell harder (the 1-year down roughly 10 basis points). Falling yields are a direct tailwind for anything that borrows or lends, which is why the banks, the non-bank lenders (NBFCs) and real-estate stocks led the day. There is also a supportive backdrop in place from earlier this month: a Reserve Bank rule (dated 17-June) that lets banks lend to non-residents against their foreign-currency (FCNR) deposits, which makes banks' dollar funding easier. That helped sentiment toward lenders, but it is a week-old measure, not something that happened today, so it is a backdrop, not the trigger.

**The negative side — who cheaper oil hurts, and the foreign exodus.** Cheaper oil is not good for everyone. The energy and metal sectors were the only ones to close red, because a lower crude price squeezes the earnings of the oil *producers* (companies like ONGC and the refiners). And the bigger worry hasn't gone away: foreign investors sold another ₹1,843 crore of Indian shares today, taking this year's outflow to a record of roughly ₹2.2 lakh crore. The market held up only because domestic institutions bought ₹3,637 crore, comfortably absorbing the foreign selling.

**What to watch.** The Iran oil licence runs for 60 days (it lapses around 21-August), so the crude price is the live signal on whether this relief holds or reverses. And a long-discussed India-US trade deal is reported to be close, with a signing targeted before 24-July, though nothing has actually been signed yet. Treat it as optimism until there is a real agreement.

## The Connections

*How the day's events ripple into each other — the part most recaps skip.*

**Cheaper oil helps the companies that buy oil, not the ones that sell it.** The lazy read is "crude fell, so oil-linked stocks rise." But the oil *producers* fell and the energy sector was red, because a lower crude price is exactly what shrinks *their* revenue. The clean winner is the oil *user* — an airline like IndiGo, up 5%, whose biggest cost just dropped. Whenever an input gets cheaper, the money to be made is in who consumes it, not who produces it. The same logic quietly helps paint makers, tyre companies and plastics, all of which use crude-based inputs.

**IT rose even though a stronger rupee normally hurts it.** Indian IT firms earn in US dollars and spend in rupees, so when the rupee strengthens, their profits in rupee terms actually shrink. IT went up anyway. That tells you today's IT bounce was about mood, not money — hope around a US trade deal and global tech steadying after Tuesday's scare, rather than anything in the companies' own numbers. A move that runs against its own logic is a fragile one, and worth treating as a bounce rather than a turn until the July results confirm otherwise.

**The most expensive stocks fell on a green day, and that is a signal in itself.** While the index rose, the priciest corner of the market, the power-equipment names trading at extraordinary valuations, was sold hard. When investors keep the rally going but quietly dump the most expensive names, it usually means they are growing wary of stretched valuations even as they stay invested. It is the kind of shift under the surface that does not show up in the headline number but tells you where the caution is building.

**The recovery was India's own, carried by domestic money.** India bounced alongside Asia's rebound, but the buyers here were domestic — ₹3,637 crore from local funds against ₹1,843 crore that foreigners sold. That depth is a real strength; it is why India has held up while foreigners pulled out a record amount this year. But it also means the recovery rests on domestic buying continuing, so the day that buying tires is the genuine risk to watch.
