## The Lead

Indian shares jumped on Friday on reports that the United States and Iran had agreed a draft deal to wind down their conflict, with the Nifty 50 closing up 1.99% at 23,622.90 and the Sensex up 2.30% at 75,527.95. The reported terms include a 60-day ceasefire and the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz, the world's busiest oil shipping lane, which sent Brent crude to a two-month low and eased the single biggest cost India faces as a large oil importer. A second, home-grown tailwind landed the same afternoon, as May inflation came in at 3.93%, below the 4% economists expected, which calms the worry that the Reserve Bank would have to stop cutting rates. One caution sits over all of it: the deal is reported but unsigned, Iran's foreign-ministry spokesman called it "merely speculation," and the real test is whether it gets signed in Geneva this weekend.

## Global Setup

The backdrop was broadly risk-on, and oil was the hinge. Asian markets rose alongside India during the day (Hang Seng +1.93%, Taiwan TAIEX +2.36%, Shanghai +1.12%) on the same US–Iran de-escalation hope, so India traded *with* its region, not against it. The dollar was soft (DXY flat at 99.76), giving the rupee room to breathe, and gold rose 2.97% to about $4,212 even on a risk-on day, a reminder that the all-clear is not yet sounded. One thing to keep separate: last night's US close shaped this morning's mood, but anything Wall Street does *tonight* is Monday's input, not today's cause.

## Macro & Policy

The day's macro picture was unusually one-sided to the good, with one large caveat.

**On the supportive side**, two forces pulled together. The reported US–Iran draft, with its Hormuz reopening, crashed Brent toward a two-month low near $87-89; for an oil importer like India that eases the import bill, the current-account gap and fuel-driven inflation all at once. Landing the same afternoon, May CPI printed 3.93%, below the 4.0% Reuters poll, which removes some of the pressure that was building on the Reserve Bank to pause its rate cuts. The bond market noticed, with the India 10-year yield easing about 3 basis points to 6.866%.

**The caveat that cuts the other way** is the deal's status itself. The whole rally leans on a draft that has not been signed and that Iran's foreign-ministry spokesman publicly dismissed as "speculation." A reported signing in Geneva this Sunday, June 14, ahead of the G7, is the swing event. If it slips, the crude relief that powered Friday can reverse, and Monday could open with a gap the wrong way.

**Watch next week:** the Sunday June 14 Geneva signing, or its slippage, and the path of Brent, which is the cleanest live gauge of whether the de-escalation is holding.

## The Market

Friday was a broad, financials-led relief rally with almost nothing left behind: all 30 Sensex stocks closed green, and the mid- and small-caps rose even harder than the headline. The lead engine was the banks (Nifty Bank +2.97%, PSU Bank +2.71%), because lower oil and softer inflation both point toward an easier rate path, which is good for lenders. The standout sector was realty (+3.53%), the classic winner when rates look like they are heading lower, since property demand tracks home-loan costs; autos (+1.95%), energy (+1.52%) and consumption (+1.64%) joined in. The lone laggard was IT (−0.09%), because a stronger rupee *hurts* a sector that earns in dollars and spends in rupees, so the currency move worked against it while everything else rotated into domestic names. India VIX, the fear gauge, fell 5.72% to 14.72, a fear-unwind rather than a leveraged squeeze. The quiet tell: foreign investors were still net sellers (FII −₹1,082 crore), and it was domestic institutions (DII +₹5,341 crore) that funded the rally.

## The Connections

*How the day's events ripple across industries — the part most market reports skip.*

**Cheaper oil is doing more work here than the headline shows.** When Brent fell on the Hormuz-reopening story, the relief did not stay in oil stocks; it travelled. It lifted the two commercial-vehicle makers (fuel is a fleet's biggest cost), the auto-parts cohort (crude feeds plastics and input costs), and banks and realty (lower oil eases inflation, which eases the rate outlook). A single geopolitical headline rotated money across half a dozen sectors at once, which is why the rally was so broad. The catch is that the whole chain rests on a draft no one has signed.

**India outran the emerging-market pack, but on borrowed strength.** The Nifty's +1.99% beat the broad EM equity gauge (up about 0.7%) and most Asian peers. But the engine was a global risk-on wave plus India's own oil-importer relief, not anything India did alone, and the flows prove it: foreign investors were still net sellers (−₹1,082 crore) while domestic funds (+₹5,341 crore) did the buying. A rally the locals pay for is sturdier than one foreigners chase, but it also means the foreign bid that would confirm the move has not shown up yet.

**The rupee firmed, but less than it should have.** The dollar was broadly soft on Friday (the Australian dollar up 0.85%, the euro up 0.37%), so the rupee's 0.57% gain actually under-participated. The likely reason is the same foreign selling: money leaving Indian equities needs dollars to exit, which quietly caps how far the rupee can climb. One soft spot on an otherwise clean day.

## What to Watch Tomorrow

- **The Geneva signing, Sunday June 14** — the weekend's single most important event. A signing confirms Friday's move; a slip is the live risk of a Monday gap the other way. Treat the deal as unsigned until a signed text lands.
- **Brent crude over the weekend** — the cleanest real-time read on whether the de-escalation is holding. Oil near its two-month low means Friday's logic survives; a spike back means the rally front-ran a deal that didn't happen.
- **Foreign flows (NSDL/NSE daily FPI)** — Friday was domestic-funded with foreigners still selling. Watch whether FII selling turns to buying, which is what would confirm the rally rather than just cushion it.
