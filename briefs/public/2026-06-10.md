## The Lead

The June 8 stop-attacking pledge between Israel and Iran broke down overnight, with the US now reported to have struck Iran directly, and that reversal pulled the Indian market round in a full circle on Wednesday. The market opened firm, with the Sensex up about 600 points by midday on hopes the Middle-East crude premium was easing, and then gave the whole gain back as the fresh escalation re-priced oil risk back up and European markets opened weak. Brent ended higher near $92. The Nifty 50 closed almost exactly flat at 23,214 (−0.12%), but the flat headline hid the real damage: the broader market fell about 1.3%, with mid- and small-caps each down that much.

---

## Global Setup

The backdrop India traded into Wednesday was risk-off across the region. US CENTCOM is reported to have begun strikes on Iran around 2:30 AM India time — before our open, so it shaped today's session — and Asia sold off with it: Taiwan's TAIEX fell 3.31%, Hong Kong's Hang Seng 0.64%, Shanghai 0.42%, with Japan and Korea also lower. (Ignore any feed showing Korea or Japan sharply up — those are stale Tuesday prints, not today.) Crude rose on the escalation, Brent up 0.5% to about $92, the structural negative for an oil importer. Notably, gold did NOT catch the usual safe-haven bid — it fell 2.0% to $4,175 — which tells you today's fear was channelled through the oil-supply premium, not a broad flight to safety. The dollar was soft (DXY −0.18%), so the rupee firmed to ₹95.25 by tracking the weaker dollar rather than anything India-specific. Last night's US close was lower (Nasdaq −1.13%), but that came after India had shut, so it is a Thursday input, not today's driver.

## Macro & Policy

The day was geopolitics-first, but the domestic policy threads kept running underneath.

**On the supportive side**, India's capital-flow package is still transmitting. The RBI's FCNR-B deposit window remains open — the central bank is bearing the full cost of hedging fresh three-to-five-year foreign-currency deposits banks raise from non-residents through end-September — and the June 5 ordinance that scrapped the capital-gains and interest taxes foreign investors pay on Indian government bonds is still feeding through. The India 10-year yield sits near a four-week low at 6.91%, though it ticked up 4.2 basis points today with the global risk-off. The local-currency EM bond ETF where India is a constituent held flat against a red equity tape, a small sign the bond-side story is a genuine counter-current.

**On the worrying side**, the same Middle-East escalation that whipsawed equities is the live threat to Friday's inflation print. Brent crossing into the low-$90s during this episode feeds straight into fuel costs, and a Reuters poll already sees May CPI rising to 4.0% from 3.48% in April, largely on that fuel pass-through. The monsoon is the standing rural worry — it is advancing briskly (now into the North-East and through Maharashtra and Andhra) but the seasonal forecast stays a below-normal 90% of the long-period average.

**Watch this week:** India's May inflation on Friday June 12, the first read that captures the crude spike; and the RBI's Friday forex-reserves supplement, the cleanest gauge of how hard the central bank is leaning to defend the rupee.

## The Market

The flat Nifty masks a soft, narrow session. The damage was in the broad market — BSE Mid-cap and Small-cap both fell about 1.3% — while the index headline held flat only because domestic funds kept buying (DII +₹3,124 crore) against foreign sellers (FII −₹2,125 crore). The clearest green corner was fertilisers: Chambal Fertilisers rose 5.04% and Coromandel about 4%, on the monsoon advance lifting nutrient demand and firmer global prices. A report that surging prices could double next year's subsidy bill is the double-edged part: a bigger subsidy supports makers' realisations if the government funds it promptly, but it turns into a working-capital risk if those reimbursements lag, so the demand pull is the cleaner driver for today's move. Metals were the weakest patch — aluminium futures crashed 9.30%, dragging Hindalco, Hindustan Zinc and Coal India down 3% or more. IT stayed soft (Nifty IT −0.83%, now off 9% on the week and 26% this year), the structurally weakest large-cap sector with no relief today. A data note for transparency: yfinance dropped the Tuesday bar for most sector indices, so the fertiliser-up and metals-down reads above are inferred from individual stock breadth, not a clean sector-index percentage.

## The Connections

*How the day's events ripple across industries — the part most market reports skip.*

**A flat index hid a risk-off session, and that is the read for the week.** The Nifty closed flat, but mid- and small-caps fell about 1.3% — the index held only because domestic funds (DII +₹3,124 crore) absorbed the foreign selling (FII −₹2,125 crore). So the flat number is a composition story, not a calm one. The mechanism is simple: large-cap weight plus a domestic bid steadied the headline, while the broad market took the hit from the overnight escalation. For a reader who owns mid-caps, "the index was flat" is misleading today — your basket likely fell.

**The classic safe-haven trade did NOT fire, and the non-move is the signal.** On a day of fresh military escalation, you would expect gold to rally and gold-financiers with it. Instead gold fell 2.0%. The reason is that today's fear was specifically an oil-supply risk — the Strait of Hormuz threat pushing crude back up — not a broad flight to safety. Gold down while crude up is the signal that the risk is being priced through energy, not through havens. So the gold-lenders sat quiet, correctly, because the driver that would have moved them never fired.

**The early crude-relief rally evaporated, and it took the oil-sensitive names with it.** At the open, the market was pricing easing crude — good for airlines and fuel retailers, bad for upstream producers. By the close, crude had reversed back up on the escalation, so that relief trade unwound before it could stick: no sustained bid for the oil-marketing companies or aviation, and upstream stayed soft anyway. The read is that crude's intraday round-trip erased the input-cost-relief thesis within the same session, which is why none of the oil-chain bets paid off cleanly.
