## 1. The Week in a Line

India rewrote the rules for foreign bond money and the whole yield curve fell, even as foreign equity money left every single day, while a Friday oil crash on an unsigned US-Iran deal sat on top as the relief that turned the week green.

## 2. The Themes

Three threads ran the week. The consequential and quiet one: two India-specific policy moves, a June 5 tax ordinance and a new RBI deposit window, opened the door to foreign debt money and the bond curve fell at every tenor. The loud one: oil, up on a Middle-East war scare mid-week, then crashed by a Friday US-Iran de-escalation that powered the week's only real up-day. And underneath both, a split, foreigners selling Indian shares all five days while turning the first small bid for Indian government bonds.

## 3. Global Setup

The global backdrop ran in three acts and oil was the hinge through all of them, and India traded into each act rather than setting it.

The week began with a tech wreck off Wall Street that hit Asia first. A US AI-chip rout carried into Monday, and North Asia took the worst of it, Korea's KOSPI crashing 8.29% and Japan's Nikkei falling 3.85%. India sat outside that chip-hardware trade, since its tech exposure is IT services not chips, but the US weakness kept returning, and the Nasdaq's 2% Wednesday-night drop dragged Indian IT lower into Thursday.

The middle of the week belonged to oil. Iran's Sunday-night strike spiked crude, US strikes on Iran were reported around Wednesday's pre-open, and by Thursday Iran had declared the Strait of Hormuz "closed," pushing Brent near $95. For an oil importer like India that war premium capped the market's midday bounces all week. It did not trigger a broad rush to safety: gold fell 2.0% even as crude rose, so the fear ran through the oil-supply premium, not a flight to haven assets.

Then Friday flipped it. A reported US-Iran de-escalation crashed the war premium out of crude, and Asian markets rose with India. Brent ended the week near $89.25, down 4.13%, round-tripping the entire Hormuz spike, the single biggest reason the backdrop ended the week a tailwind rather than the mid-week drag.

## 4. Macro & Policy

This was the week India's own policy machinery did the work. Two India-specific levers drove the move, and both pull foreign debt money in.

The first was the FPI bond-tax ordinance from June 5, which scrapped the capital-gains and interest-income taxes foreign investors pay on Indian government bonds and opened long-dated G-secs to unlimited foreign buying through the Fully Accessible Route. The second went live on Tuesday, June 9: under the RBI's new foreign-currency deposit window, the central bank itself bears the cost of hedging fresh three-to-five-year FCNR-B deposits that banks raise from non-residents through end-September. In plain terms the RBI is subsidising the dollar-rupee hedge so banks can offer NRIs higher rates and pull dollars in, which cut their funding cost directly and is why financials led the market on Tuesday and again on Friday. Jefferies put a $50-70 billion figure on what the measures could attract, one desk's estimate rather than a settled number.

The bond market answered across the whole curve. The 10-year G-Sec ended Friday at 6.866%, and yields fell at every tenor, the belly leading with the 5-year down 12.7 basis points and the 10-year down 8.3. A soft inflation print sealed it: May CPI came in at 3.93% on Friday, below the 4.0% the Reuters poll expected, against a going-in worry of a hot crude pass-through number. That eases pressure on the Reserve Bank to pause its rate cuts, and a falling curve into a soft print is the bond market pricing an easier rate path. The standing worry stayed the monsoon, advancing now but held at a below-normal 90% of the long-period average, the food-inflation tail the RBI had flagged.

The bond-and-equity split is the week's quiet structural story. Foreign investors were net sellers of Indian equities every single session, the daily selling shrinking from ₹8,776 crore on Monday to ₹1,082 crore by Friday, and domestic funds absorbed all of it. But on the debt side the new measures showed a first sign of working: foreigners turned small net buyers of Indian government bonds in June so far, about ₹377 crore, even while pulling money out of equities. So the side built to pull debt money in and steady the rupee has begun to turn while the equity outflow has not, and the rupee told the same story, weakening 0.72% to ₹95.10 even when the dollar was soft, because money leaving Indian equities needs dollars to exit.

## 5. The Market

The week closed green but it did not feel that way for most of it. The Nifty 50 ended up 1.1% at 23,623 and the Sensex up 1.73%, yet the whole weekly gain was really one day's work. Monday opened with a broad, orderly retreat and the soft sessions that followed kept the index pinned, until Friday delivered a financials-led relief rally where all 30 Sensex stocks closed green and the Nifty rose 1.99% in that single session. Without Friday the week was red. The breadth agreed: 347 stocks fell against 320 that rose, the signature of a narrow, big-cap-led week where a handful of large financials did the lifting while the broader market stayed soft.

The sector map split cleanly along one line, which was rates. The rate-sensitive corners led, Bank Nifty up 4.25% and PSU Bank the best sector at +3.27% on the deposit-scheme funding relief and then lower oil and softer inflation, with realty green too. The losers were the global cyclicals and the dollar earners: IT was the worst sector at −4.19% on its US read, while metals fell 2.78% and energy 2.73% on the global cyclical washout, with aluminium futures crashing mid-week and dragging the metal names down.

## 6. Earnings

Q4 season is behind us, so earnings stopped being a market-wide driver and became single-stock fuel. Inox India broke out to a new high on Wednesday on its record Q4, revenue up 24.2% to ₹475 crore, and CCL Products rose 8.7% on Monday on its own strong Q4, revenue up 46% to ₹1,226 crore on 18% volume growth. On a risk-off week, proven earnings still got bid while the rest traded the macro.

## 7. Corporate News

With results over, the company news was about orders, deals and capital. Order wins led, mostly in capital goods and infrastructure: JNK India landed a large waste-gas order for ADNOC's TA'ZIZ project in Abu Dhabi and rose about 16% on Tuesday, Afcons won a ₹5,301 crore breakwater award at Vadhvan Port, and Dynacons picked up a ₹125.88 crore private-cloud contract from Central Bank of India. On capital, Zee's board approved raising at least ₹2,300 crore and paired it with India rights to 39 FIFA events through 2034. One to flag: IFCI surged to its 20% limit on Friday on talk the NSE is preparing its long-awaited IPO, since IFCI holds an indirect NSE stake a listing could unlock.

## 8. Big Movers

The single stocks worth explaining split cleanly: the risers were paid for a real catalyst, the faller was the market taking back a spike.

**Aegis Logistics, +22.6% on the week**, was the cleanest big riser, and most of it was a sustained trend rather than one wild day. Aegis handles LPG, and the supply shortfall that had run near 50% in April narrowed to about 30% in May, easing the squeeze on its core business, while JM Financial reiterated a 'Buy' and raised its target to ₹1,200 from ₹935. A target raise plus a real operating improvement can compound into a multi-day run, but with no single fresh filing to justify the size this was momentum and a brighter outlook rather than a hard one-day catalyst.

**Oil India, −13.6% on the week**, was the biggest sourced fall, and it teaches that the market does not pay you twice for the same news. Oil India had spiked on a June 5 Andaman natural-gas discovery, but the find sits in remote deep water, so turning it into production and cash is a multi-year job. Once that sank in, investors faded the excitement and the stock gave most of the spike back. A real discovery and a tradeable catalyst are not the same thing when the payoff is a decade away.

**Wipro, −9.2% on the week**, screens as a big drop but is not a fundamental break. Monday was the record date for its ₹15,000 crore buyback, which marks the quoted price down by design, the same way a dividend adjusts the price, with the rest sympathy for a weak IT sector. So a chunk of that −9% is an accounting adjustment that means little once you know the cause.

The rest rode smaller catalysts, CarTrade +19.8% on a Kotak upgrade and Caplin Point +18.4% on FY26 growth, while run-up names handed back gains, TD Power Systems −9.1% and HFCL −8.2% on profit-booking, and microfinance like Spandana −11.2% on sector nerves. A few big raw numbers, Senores +24.7%, Thomas Cook +17.0% and Suven +13.0%, had no established cause, so they stay table-only.

## 9. The Connections

*How the week's cross-currents ran between commodities, sectors and the bond market.*

Crude was the busiest wire, pulling both directions before settling cheaper. When it rose, the read was a cost hit for fuel buyers and a revenue lift for upstream producers. When it reversed, the relief travelled outward to FMCG and auto parts, where crude feeds plastics and packaging, and to commercial-vehicle makers because fuel is a fleet's biggest line item.

Cheaper oil then fed straight into the rate story. Lower crude eases the inflation outlook, an easier inflation outlook eases the rate outlook, and the bond market did the relaying, which is the oxygen that carried banks, realty and autos. The RBI deposit scheme showed the same wiring in miniature, cutting deposit-takers' funding cost at once, though it skipped the housing financiers that do not fund themselves with NRI dollars.

IT ran on a wire of its own, plugged into America rather than India. The telling part was the non-move underneath the fall. A weaker rupee normally pads an exporter's margins, because IT earns dollars and spends rupees, yet it did nothing to cushion the drop. So the market was pricing the structural worry about US tech demand, not the currency math. And across all of it ran the foreign equity bid that never came: a market the locals pay for is sturdier than one foreigners chase, so turning that selling is the flow that would lift the market broadly rather than sector by sector.

## 10. One Thing, Explained

**Why foreign money can leave the stock market and walk into the bond market at the same time.**

This week looked contradictory if you only watched stocks. Foreigners sold Indian shares every single day, yet also turned small net buyers of Indian government bonds, about ₹377 crore. The trick is that they do not treat the two as one decision. Buying a stock is a bet that a company will grow. Buying a government bond is quieter: you are lending money to the government for a fixed return, so what you care about is the interest rate, the safety, and how easily you can get your money in and out. Different question, different answer.

This week the two India-specific rules above made the bond answer "yes" while the stock answer stayed "no." Scrapping the tax and opening the no-ceiling FAR lane made the bond door wider and cheaper at once. And the government wants that debt money, because it funds the spending gap and the incoming dollars support the rupee. So even with stocks bleeding foreign money, the debt side had begun, gently, to turn.

## 11. The Pattern

This is the first weekly brief, so there is no prior week to compare against. The baseline starts here, and from next week this section will measure whether the threads below continued or broke. Four carry forward.

- **Foreign out, domestic in, every single day**, with domestic funds funding the whole green close. The selling did shrink as the week wore on, which one week cannot tell apart from a turn. Watch whether it reverses.
- **IT was the worst sector, persistently**, weakest on four of five days, and a weaker rupee did nothing to cushion it. Watch whether this is a wobble or the start of a longer slide.
- **Crude led the week.** The producer's recurrence data agrees, crude the most repeated linkage and the crude-to-FMCG wire firing three times, so the oil-driven read is measured. Watch whether oil stays the lead wire or hands off to something domestic.
- **The yield curve fell all week**, the through-line tying together the soft May CPI, the cheaper oil and the two debt measures. If it holds it is the sturdiest signal of the four.
