## The Lead

India's large-cap IT shares fell hard on Tuesday while the rest of the market held firm, dragging a flat-looking index slightly red. Nifty IT dropped 2.73% to a multi-year low, with Infosys (INFY) and Tata Consultancy Services (TCS) both hitting fresh 52-week lows. The trigger was Accenture's weak revenue guidance last week, plus a growing fear that artificial intelligence is shrinking the outsourcing work these firms get paid for. The Nifty 50 slipped only 0.34% to 23,865.75 because the IT damage was narrow: most of the market was green. The session was a single-sector re-rating, not a broad sell-off.

---

## Global Setup

The global backdrop India traded into was firm, which makes the IT fall all the more telling. Last night's Wall Street was strong (the Nasdaq closed up 1.3%, the S&P 500 up 0.7%), and Asian peers were mostly green during India's day (Hong Kong's Hang Seng +1.57%, Shanghai +1.16%, Taiwan +0.96%). The dollar was a touch firmer, gold sat at $4,044, and Brent held around $73. Emerging-market equity funds rose (the EEM exchange-traded fund +1.4%), so there was no money fleeing emerging markets. The clean read for India: on a day the world was buying risk and even US tech rallied, Indian IT still fell. That tells you the IT weakness was about Indian IT's own demand worries, not a global mood.

## Macro & Policy

The day's macro story was quiet on fresh action but supportive underneath.

**The positive side — cheap oil is now a structural tailwind.** Brent closed the June quarter down about 30%, its largest quarterly drop since 2020, with crude back to where it sat before the late-February Gulf scare. The Strait-of-Hormuz de-escalation held, and the oil that had been bottled up in the Persian Gulf flooded an already well-supplied market. For an economy that imports roughly 85% of its oil, that is a durable relief: cheaper imports, softer inflation, and a lighter import bill. It supports oil-marketing companies, paint-makers, airlines and tyre-makers, and it eases the price-rise worry the Reserve Bank watches.

**The negative side — the monsoon and a no-relief US rate backdrop.** June closed the driest in over a century, with a rainfall deficit near 42-46% and some states (Madhya Pradesh, Maharashtra, Gujarat) far short. That is a direct risk to the rural economy and food prices, though the weather office has flagged a Bay-of-Bengal system around 3 July that could finally revive the rains. Separately, US bond yields ticked up again (the 10-year to 4.41%, the 2-year to 3.73%), confirming there is no US rate cut coming soon. That "higher-for-longer" backdrop is one of the three forces weighing on Indian IT, which earns in dollars and depends on US clients spending freely.

**What to watch.** The India-US interim trade deal is still unsigned, with the temporary 10% US tariff on Indian goods set to expire on 24 July. India's stance has hardened: Commerce Minister Piyush Goyal said last week India will not sign until the terms give it a genuine edge over rival exporters.

## The Market

The headline barely moved (Nifty −0.34%, Sensex −0.33%, Bank Nifty −0.32%), but underneath sat a clean two-speed tape. The one heavy weight was IT (−2.73%), and it alone explains the index's small loss because IT is about 12-13% of the Nifty by weight. Everything else leaned green: Realty led (+1.31%), Consumer Durables next (+1.16%), with Pharma (+0.39%), Auto (+0.24%), Consumption (+0.20%), Oil & Gas (+0.17%) and Healthcare (+0.16%) all firm; the soft corners outside IT were mild (Media −0.77%, PSU Bank −0.72%, FMCG −0.68%). Crucially, the broad market was positive: midcaps rose about 0.4% and smallcaps about 1%, with advancing stocks outnumbering decliners. This was not risk-off; money moved out of IT and into domestic-facing sectors.

The most informative thing was a *non*-move in IT's favour that didn't help. A weaker rupee and a green Nasdaq both usually lift dollar-earning IT exporters, yet IT fell anyway. When the two things that should have supported a sector can't, the weakness is fundamental, not technical. On flows, foreign investors sold ₹2,557 crore on quarter-end book-squaring while domestic funds bought ₹6,842 crore, more than mopping it up. The fear gauge (India VIX) sat flat at 13.6, so despite this being both monthly options expiry and quarter-end, there was no panic and no expiry squeeze. The mechanical overlay was absorbed, not disruptive.

## Corporate News

A quiet day for company-specific news, with no material SEBI, RBI or tribunal action, so there is no Regulatory section.

- **The Delhi EV Policy 2026-2030** was the one policy event with clear winners and losers across the market. The Delhi Cabinet approved a ₹15,000 crore EV push, effective 1 July and valid to March 2030, targeting 95% electric-vehicle registrations in the city by 2027, with subsidies on electric two-wheelers and a halt on new petrol and CNG two-wheeler registrations from April 2028. The market split the auto pack cleanly along it (see The Connections and Big Movers).
- **The Finance Ministry's Monthly Economic Review**, out today, said economic activity "remained resilient" in the early months of 2026-27, citing manufacturing activity, e-way bills, electricity use and auto sales. Routine commentary rather than a fresh policy action, but a steady-state read on the domestic economy that fits the day's green domestic breadth.

## The Connections

*How Tuesday's session rippled across Indian sectors — the part most recaps skip.*

**Indian IT fell despite everything that should have lifted it, which is the whole point.** On a normal day, two things support dollar-earning IT exporters: a weaker rupee (more rupees per dollar earned) and a rising US tech market (the demand proxy). Tuesday delivered both — the rupee softened and the Nasdaq rose 1.3% — and Indian IT still dropped to a multi-year low. When a sector ignores its usual tailwinds, the move is fundamental, not noise. The fear here is structural: Accenture's weak guidance plus growing evidence that artificial intelligence is doing the routine work outsourcing firms used to bill for. The first real test of whether this is panic or a genuine re-pricing of these firms' worth comes on 9 July, when TCS reports.

**One EV policy picked winners and losers inside the auto pack on the same day.** Delhi's EV Policy 2026-2030 subsidises electric two-wheelers and will bar new petrol and CNG two-wheeler registrations from 2028. That is a straight demand tailwind for electric-scooter makers (Ola Electric +8.4%, Ather +5.2% to a record) and a straight threat to legacy petrol two-wheelers, which is why Eicher Motors, the maker of Royal Enfield, fell about 4.75% the same session. The auto index looked flat (+0.24%), but that flatness hides a clean split: policy is repricing who is on the right side of the electric shift, not lifting or sinking the whole sector.

**Quarter-end was a test the domestic-money story passed.** This was both monthly options expiry and the last day of the June quarter, the kind of session that can see foreign funds exit and mechanical squeezes distort the day's prices. Foreign investors did sell (₹2,557 crore), but domestic institutions bought far more (₹6,842 crore), the fear gauge stayed flat, and the broad market closed green. The same pattern that has run for months — domestic money absorbing foreign selling — held cleanly through the year's most flow-heavy session. The read: India's own savings, flowing in through monthly investment plans, are now deep enough to steady the market even on a quarter-end exit.

## What to Watch Tomorrow

- **Wednesday 1-July — June auto sales (SIAM/OEM dispatches) and the final manufacturing PMI.** The first hard demand read of the month; the auto numbers matter more than usual after the Delhi EV-policy split, and the June flash PMI already eased to a three-month low of 54.5.
- **Around 3-July — the monsoon relief window.** A Bay-of-Bengal system is flagged as the trigger for July rains to catch up after the driest June in over a century; rural demand and food prices ride on it.
- **Friday 3-July — US jobs data (non-farm payrolls) and the RBI Weekly Statistical Supplement.** The US number drives the global rate read that is now part of the IT story; the RBI supplement shows the forex-reserves draw.
- **Wednesday 9-July — TCS Q1 FY27 results**, the first hard data point on whether today's IT sell-off is justified. Infosys follows on 23 July.
- **Thursday 24-July — the India-US trade-deal deadline**, when the temporary 10% US tariff on Indian goods expires. Still unsigned.
- **Thursday 31-July — the US pharma tariff (Section 232) takes effect for named firms** (29 September for all others). Indian generic exporters are exempt for now, so the pharma overhang is a late-July watch, not an imminent one.
