Good evening. This is India Markets Brief from toroIQ. Your read on today's session.

Indian IT cracked today, and the shock was imported. Accenture, the global bellwether for the outsourcing and software-services business that Infosys and TCS live in, cut its full-year revenue forecast and missed its quarter. Its New York shares fell about eighteen percent, the worst single day in the company's history. That broke before India opened, so it hit our IT names the same session. The Nifty IT index closed down three point six five percent, after sinking roughly six percent during the day. Infosys was the worst of the lot, down eight point two percent to a near five-year low. Here is the part that matters for the next few months. Accenture did not say AI demand is weak. It said companies are moving their tech budgets into AI rather than growing them. That is a worry about the size of the whole demand pie, not a one-company stumble.

Here is where the market closed today. The Nifty fifty finished at twenty-four thousand and thirteen, down about two-thirds of a percent, snapping a five-day winning streak. The Sensex fell six hundred and seven points to seventy-six thousand eight hundred and three. But this was a single-factor day. Strip out IT and the market was essentially flat. Fourteen of the fifteen Nifty sectors closed within about one percent of unchanged, and only IT moved with any real force. Underneath that flat headline, the money that left IT rotated into domestic, rate-sensitive names. Non-banking lenders ran, hotels bid, and the power-grid and engineering names lifted. The Leela was up eight percent, Piramal Finance almost eight, Tata Capital over six.

Now the day's puzzle, and it is worth slowing down on. Foreign investors were net buyers of about four thousand eight hundred and fifty-nine crore rupees of Indian shares, on a down day, while domestic institutions were net sellers. That sounds like a contradiction, and it isn't. Foreigners did two things at once. They bought the non-IT large-caps that were rotating up, and at the same time they sold Nifty futures as a hedge against a further fall. The index dropped anyway, because the selling in the heavy IT names, done by everyone, outweighed the buying elsewhere. Net cash buying plus a futures short is a cautious, hedged stance, not conviction.

The other story today was supply. Reliance said at its annual meeting that Jio Platforms has filed for India's largest-ever IPO, about thirty-eight thousand crore rupees, all new shares with no promoter selling, most of it earmarked to repay Jio's overseas debt. That lands just three days after the National Stock Exchange's own thirty thousand crore filing. Two record issues in a single week point to a heavy supply pipeline for the second half, and a lot of new paper competes for the same pool of money. The one stock that moved on it was New India Assurance, up thirteen percent, because it is a selling shareholder in the NSE offer.

Now, what to watch, and the biggest item landed over the weekend. Iran declared the Strait of Hormuz closed on Saturday, but it is not closed. The US Navy says shipping traffic through the strait actually rose, and Brent crude is still near eighty dollars, when a real closure would push it past a hundred. So treat it as rhetoric ahead of the US-Iran talks that opened in Switzerland on Sunday, not a supply shock. The number that tells the truth here is Brent. For a country that imports most of its oil, that is the single biggest thing on the board.

On the home front, El Niño has been formally flagged and first-half-of-June rainfall is forty-one percent below normal, with a revival expected, so the cumulative number is the swing variable for the farm and agri-input names. And markets are shut Friday the twenty-sixth for Muharram, so next week runs only four sessions.

That's your brief. Before I sign off: this has been general market commentary, not investment advice. For investment advice tailored to your situation, consult a SEBI-registered Investment Adviser. Markets are risky; you may lose money; act with care. See you tomorrow.
