Good evening. This is India Markets Brief from toroIQ. Your read on today's session.

The deal that markets only hoped for on Friday got signed over the weekend, and that single event powered today's session. The United States and Iran signed a fourteen-point agreement on Sunday to end their roughly hundred-and-seven-day war. The agreement reopens the Strait of Hormuz, the world's busiest oil shipping lane, and that sent crude tumbling. For a country like India, which imports about eighty-five percent of its oil, cheaper crude is a direct relief, and the Nifty rose on it.

Here is where the market closed today. The Nifty fifty finished at twenty-three thousand eight hundred and fifty-four, up almost one percent, with risers beating fallers about four to one. This was a broad rally, not a top-heavy one. The wider Nifty hundred actually rose more than the giants. Real estate led every sector, up almost four percent, because lower oil cools inflation, which points to lower interest rates, and property demand tracks home-loan costs. Autos followed, up two and a half percent, helped by cheaper fuel and easier financing. The one sector that fell was pharma, down about half a percent, as defensive stocks were sold to fund the rate-sensitive names. The fear gauge, India VIX, eased to fourteen point three.

There was a second piece of good news. India's May inflation, released Friday, came in at three point nine three percent, comfortably inside the Reserve Bank's target band. Cheaper oil and tame inflation together keep the door open for more rate cuts, and that is exactly what today's rally was pricing.

Now the one honest caveat. The deal is signed but not yet done. Clearing the mines from the Strait of Hormuz is assessed at forty to fifty days, and about six hundred ships are still stuck in the Gulf waiting on it. That is why crude fell on the signing but still sits about sixteen dollars above where it was before the war. The relief is real, but the last leg of it is not yet banked.

Now the movers. The sharpest was Aarti Industries, a specialty-chemicals maker, up about thirteen percent on a run of long-dated contract wins, with cheaper crude an added feedstock relief. The cleanest trade-deal story was Balkrishna Industries, India's largest off-highway-tyre exporter, up about nine percent. United States tariffs had halted its American shipments, and the weekend de-escalation directly reverses that overhang. And Schneider Electric Infrastructure rose nearly ten percent after Goldman Sachs upgraded it to Buy.

Here is what struck me most about today. The fall in oil did not stay in oil stocks. In fact the oil companies themselves barely moved. For the state oil marketers, cheaper crude cuts both ways, since lower input cost is offset by weaker earnings at the upstream producers, so the two roughly cancel. Instead the relief travelled. It eased the inflation outlook, which eased the expected rate path, which lifted property, autos and consumer names together. One geopolitical headline rotated money across half a dozen sectors at once, and that is why the rally was so broad.

Now, what to watch this week. The biggest scheduled event is the United States Federal Reserve, whose rate decision and projections land Wednesday night Indian time, and that sets the global rate mood India trades inside. Then on Friday, the formal signing ceremony in Geneva, though the real-time gauge of whether this relief holds is simply the price of crude.

That's your brief. Before I sign off: this has been general market commentary, not investment advice. For investment advice tailored to your situation, consult a SEBI-registered Investment Adviser. Markets are risky; you may lose money; act with care. See you tomorrow.
