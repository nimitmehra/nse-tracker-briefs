Good evening. This is India Markets Brief from toroIQ. Your read on today's session.

The most important thing that happened this weekend was not on the stock screen. It was at the source of the petrol pump. The Iran crisis that had markets nervous on Friday resolved. The Strait of Hormuz, the world's most important oil chokepoint, reopened. The United States lifted its naval blockade, and over the weekend the US and Iran, meeting in Switzerland, agreed a sixty-day roadmap toward a final deal, including a commitment to keep Hormuz open and a waiver letting Iran sell its oil again. And so Brent crude fell about eleven percent over the week, to roughly seventy-eight dollars a barrel.

Here is why that matters for every Indian, not just investors. India buys about eighty-five percent of its oil from abroad. When a war scare threatens the world's biggest oil chokepoint, the price spikes, and everything imported gets costlier: fuel, transport, the rupee, inflation. When that scare lifts, it all eases. A lighter import bill, softer inflation, and support for the rupee, all at once. That is the single biggest tailwind an oil-importing economy can get, and it is far more consequential than anything that happened on the index today.

Now, the tape. On that relief, the market bounced back from Friday's IT-driven fall. The Nifty fifty added a third of a percent to reclaim twenty-four thousand one hundred. But be honest about the size of it. This was a shallow rebound, not a full recovery. The index is up only about one percent on the week, and the IT sector, which crashed on Friday, only partly recovered. But the biggest moves of the day were not in the index leaders. They were small companies on hard, specific news. Kirloskar Oil Engines locked a twenty percent upper circuit on an order to supply generators that power AI data centres. And the chemicals maker NOCIL also jumped twenty percent after the government imposed a five-year anti-dumping duty on cheap imports from China. The day's real money chased specific catalysts, with artificial-intelligence data-centre demand the standout theme. Cipla led the index itself, up almost five percent, on a fresh buy call from the brokerage Citi.

Now the day's catch, and it is worth slowing down on. Foreign investors did buy about four thousand eight hundred crore rupees of shares today. It is tempting to read that as foreigners coming back. But a big chunk of it was mechanical. A scheduled reshuffle of the FTSE India index took effect today, which forces index funds to buy certain stocks regardless of the news. So the honest read is, a calendar event plus a genuine oil relief lifted a thin market, not a flood of conviction money returning. The bigger picture is still a record two-point-two lakh crore rupees of foreign money that has left India this year.

The other story today was supply. Reliance rallied as it filed papers for the Jio IPO, India's largest ever, an all-fresh issue of about thirty-eight thousand crore rupees, most of it to repay Jio's debt.

Now, what to watch. The oil relief is real, but it is tied to a sixty-day deal that can still slip. President Trump threatened fresh strikes even during the talks, so treat cheaper oil as a window, not a permanent gift, and watch the crude price as the truth-meter. At home, watch the monsoon. June has been the driest in over a century, a forty-six percent rain deficit, but the rains have just revived, and whether they catch up in July is the swing factor for farm incomes. And a calendar note: markets are shut Friday for Muharram, so this is a four-session week.
