Good evening. This is India Markets Brief from toroIQ. Your read on today's session.

Today the June-quarter results season did its real work. It started separating the strong companies from the weak ones, and that pulled Indian shares up even as most of Asia fell hard. A record quarter from Federal Bank lifted the private banks, and strong software results lifted technology. Those were the day's two engines. But one caveat runs under the whole session, and I will come back to it. This was home-grown money, not a foreign vote of confidence.

Here is where the market closed today. The Nifty fifty rose just over one percent, to twenty-four thousand three hundred and thirty-four. The banks did the heaviest lifting, up one point six percent, after Federal Bank's record quarter set the private-bank tone. Technology added one point eight percent, carrying Thursday night's software results into today. The one weak corner was pharma, down one point four percent, as money rotated out of the defensive names into the day's growth stories.

Now the part that makes today unusual. Right through Indian trading hours, the rest of Asia was falling hard. Japan's Nikkei dropped four percent. Korea's market fell three and a half. India rose over one percent anyway. So this was not a global tide lifting everyone. India went the other way, on the strength of its own results.

Now that caveat about the money. All month the story has been that foreign investors are returning to India, and over July that is genuinely true. But today itself does not prove it. Foreign investors were small net sellers. It was Indian institutions, buying just over a thousand crore rupees, that carried the market up. Do not read today's rise as foreign conviction.

Now the movers. The biggest gainer was eClerx Services, a back-office and data-analytics firm, up more than twelve percent. It reported a strong quarter, with profit up twenty-six percent, and flagged better demand into the second half. On top of a hot one-month run, Nomura kept its buy call and raised its price target.

The clean fall was CEAT, the tyre-maker, down more than seven percent. Its revenue actually grew twenty-two percent, but its profit collapsed ninety-six percent, to almost nothing. Costlier natural rubber, crude-linked inputs and a currency loss wiped out the margin. This was a genuine margin miss, not profit-taking.

The third was HFCL [SAY: H-F-C-L], an optical-fibre and network-gear maker, down five percent and locked at its lower price limit. There was no fresh bad news. The stock had simply run up more than two hundred percent this year, and today it gave some of that back.

Here is the one thing that struck me most about today. For weeks the market traded a cheap-oil-helps-India story. That has quietly reversed. Brent crude is back near eighty-six dollars, up about nine percent over the month. You do not see it yet in the oil-refiner stocks, because a single day's move is too small to shift them. But you see it clearly in a company that buys oil-linked inputs. That is exactly what happened to CEAT today. The oil premium is back, and now it shows up as a margin line, not as relief. Watch the whole tyre and auto-parts cohort.

Now, what to watch, and it is two clocks. The nearer worry is prices. India's June retail inflation ran hot at four point three eight percent, the highest in over a year, as food prices jumped. That narrows the Reserve Bank's room to cut rates in early August. The bigger clock is trade. A temporary ten percent United States tariff on Indian goods expires on the twenty-fourth of July, and the interim deal is stuck on the last piece of legal text. That deadline is the market's largest scheduled event, now one week out.
