Good evening. This is India Markets Brief from toroIQ. Your read on today's session.

Today India's biggest private banks let the market down on the one number that matters most for a lender, its margin on loans. HDFC Bank [SAY: H-D-F-C Bank] and Axis Bank each fell about five percent after their June-quarter results, and that single earnings theme pulled the whole market lower. This was a home-grown bank story.

Here is where the market closed today. The Nifty fifty slipped about four tenths of a percent, to twenty-four thousand two hundred and thirty-eight. The Bank Nifty fell nearly one percent. But the fall was shallow, because money did not leave the market. It rotated inside it. The day's clear winner was the state-owned banks, and that index jumped almost two point eight percent as government lenders reported strong quarters. Pharma rose one point four percent, and energy, media and metals all closed higher. India's fear gauge actually fell, to just under thirteen. That tells you this was an orderly, stock-specific sell-off, not a panic.

And here is the unusual part. Across the region, emerging markets were higher today, yet India slipped anyway. So this was not foreign money fleeing. It was India going its own way, down on its own bank earnings. It is the exact mirror of Friday, when India rose on good results while Asia fell.

Two things frame the week from here. On the worrying side, the oil premium is back. Over the weekend the standoff between the United States and Iran around the Strait of Hormuz flared up again, the narrow lane that carries much of the world's oil. Brent crude spiked toward ninety-one dollars before easing back to about eighty-eight, now roughly thirty percent above its early-July lows. For an oil importer like India, that revives the inflation worry that cheap oil had eased just weeks ago. On the supportive side, domestic institutions bought more than thirteen hundred crore rupees today and fully absorbed the foreign selling, and Iran signalled it could reopen talks, which pared crude back off its high.

Now the movers. The cleanest gain of the day was D P Abhushan [SAY: D-P Ah-BHOO-shun], a jewellery retailer, up more than seven percent. Its June-quarter revenue rose fifty-eight percent and profit seventy-seven percent, and it opened two new showrooms, with the board approving the result during the session.

The falls were the two private banks at the centre of the day. HDFC Bank dropped about five percent. Its margin fell to a record low of three point two six percent, and its lending income missed expectations. Axis Bank fell about five and a half percent. Its headline profit jumped twenty-three percent, but that was flattered by a one-off write-back of past provisions, while the underlying margin still slipped. The market saw through the headline and sold both on the margin worry.

And the mirror of that trade was Punjab National Bank, up more than five and a half percent. This state-run lender's quarterly profit more than tripled, up two hundred and thirteen percent.

Here is what struck me most about today. This margin squeeze is not a two-stock accident. It is a signal for the whole large private-bank pack. As the Reserve Bank has cut interest rates, banks' loan earnings reset lower faster than their deposit costs, so the spread narrows across every big private lender, not just these two. That is why the market treated the results as a cohort warning, and rotated into the cheaper state banks instead. The counter-view from several brokers is that cheaper funding should ease the pressure ahead. Whether that plays out is the read to track.

Now, what to watch. On Wednesday the twenty-third, Infosys reports, the big technology verdict of the season. And Thursday the twenty-fourth brings the United States tariff deadline, when a temporary tariff on Indian goods expires with the trade deal still unsigned. That is the market's largest scheduled event, now four days out.
