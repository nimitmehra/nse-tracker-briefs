Good evening. This is India Markets Brief from toroIQ. Your read on today's session.

Today the market ran a full circle, and the round trip is the whole story. The June eighth pledge between Israel and Iran to stop attacking each other broke down overnight, with the United States now reported to have struck Iran directly. India opened firm anyway, with the Sensex up about six hundred points by midday, on hope the oil-war premium was easing. Then the market gave the entire gain back as the fresh escalation pushed oil risk straight back up. The Nifty closed almost exactly flat.

Here is where the market closed today. The Nifty fifty finished at twenty-three thousand two hundred and fifteen, down a flat zero point one two percent. But the flat headline hid the real damage. The broader market fell about one and a third percent, with mid-caps and small-caps each down that much. So if you own mid-caps, the index was flat but your basket likely fell. The one green corner was fertilisers, with Chambal up five percent on a report that surging global prices could double next year's subsidy bill. Metals were the weakest patch, as aluminium futures fell sharply and dragged Hindalco and Hindustan Zinc down three percent or more.

And here is the more telling part. The index held flat only because domestic funds kept buying. They bought about three thousand crore rupees while foreign investors sold about two thousand crore. So the flat number is not a calm session, it is domestic money absorbing a foreign exit on a soft, narrow day.

Now the macro frame. The same Middle-East escalation that whipsawed shares is the live threat to Friday's inflation print. Brent crude ended higher near ninety-two dollars, and that feeds straight into fuel costs. A Reuters poll already sees May inflation rising to four percent, from about three and a half in April, mostly on fuel. On the supportive side, India's bond story is still a counter-current. After last week's tax change for foreign bond buyers, the ten-year yield sits near a four-week low at six point nine percent, even with today's global risk-off.

Now the movers. The cleanest winner was Inox India, up twelve percent. The cryogenic-equipment maker posted a record quarter and won about three hundred and twenty crore rupees of fresh orders since April, including a large cryogenic-storage order from a global private space-exploration company. It hit a new high on heavy volume, even on a weak day for the broader market. The second was CarTrade Tech, also up twelve percent, after the brokerage InCred started coverage with a Buy and a target near three thousand rupees, on top of strong used-car platform growth. The third was a fall. Oil India dropped ten percent, as the market faded its recent natural-gas discovery in the Andaman waters. The find is real, but it sits in remote deep water, so any production is a multi-year story.

Here is what struck me about today. On a day of fresh military escalation, you would expect gold to rally as the safe haven. Instead gold fell two percent. The reason is that today's fear was specifically an oil-supply risk, the threat to the narrow shipping lane that carries much of the world's oil, not a broad flight to safety. Gold falling while crude rose is the signal that the fear was being priced through energy, not through havens.

Now, what to watch, and it is Friday. India's May inflation figure lands, the first read that captures this crude spike, with fuel the swing factor. The same day brings the Reserve Bank's weekly reserves figure, the cleanest sign of how hard it is defending the rupee. And the oil price stays the live signal. Any further escalation overnight is a Thursday input, and a re-spike in crude would tighten Friday's inflation read further.

That's your brief. Before I sign off: this has been general market commentary, not investment advice. For investment advice tailored to your situation, consult a SEBI-registered Investment Adviser. Markets are risky; you may lose money; act with care. See you tomorrow.
