# Canonical Voice Exemplar — Podcast Script (EVENING model)

**This is the bar.** Read it FIRST every run, before composing. Your script should sound like this one. If a sentence feels like a desk note, an analyst's shorthand, or something a listener would have to ask about — you've drifted. Re-read this.

Source: the 2026-06-04 episode (Thursday's session), an **evening recap published post-close**, after a full principal line-edit. Established 2026-06-05; re-framed to the evening model 2026-06-06.

---

## Why this is the bar — the seven things it gets right

Each maps to a real failure the first draft made and this version fixed. These are the *judgments* to imitate, not rules to memorise.

1. **Every sentence passes the naive-listener test.** Read each line as if speaking to a smart friend who knows nothing about markets. If they could ask "what does that mean?" — it fails. No desk-shorthand ("producer's cut", "flow-math beat currency-math"). Every line is plain cause → effect → so-what.
2. **Names are placed the moment they appear.** When the media move is mentioned, Zee Entertainment is named *in the same breath*. A listener hears linearly and cannot scan ahead.
3. **Time-words are anchored to the EVENING listener.** This is an evening recap. The covered session is **"today"**; scheduled events that land the next session (RBI, GDP) are **"tomorrow"** — never "today" for a not-yet-happened event, never "yesterday" for the close just covered. (Older morning drafts said the opposite — under the morning model the session was "yesterday" and events were "today". The evening model inverts this.)
4. **Causal claims are stated in full, in plain words, ONCE.** The war-risk point is a complete chain — strikes near Hormuz → war premium up → so even though oil fell, the relief didn't pass through. No oblique metaphor; made one time.
5. **Each mover is what-it-does / what-happened / why-it-matters, in 3 sentences.**
6. **No meta-commentary.** The listener gets the story, not the analyst's view of the story.
7. **TTS-clean.** Numbers spelled out, no ₹ or % symbols, no em-dashes, sentences ≤25 words. Within the 500–700 word band.

## The exemplar (body as narrated)

Good evening. This is India Markets Brief from toroIQ. Your read on today's session.

Today was almost a non-event, and that was the whole story. The Nifty closed up just ten points, and the fear gauge actually fell. The market sat on its hands, because everything that matters lands tomorrow morning, here in India.

Here is where the market closed today. The Nifty fifty finished at twenty-three thousand four hundred and sixteen, up a flat zero point zero five percent. The Sensex and the Bank Nifty barely moved. The one real sector move was Nifty Media, up about two percent, and that was almost entirely one stock, Zee Entertainment, on a sports-rights win I will come to in a moment. The fear gauge, India VIX, fell to fifteen point nine, down about two and a half percent. That is unusual right before a major event. Normally fear rises into a big decision; today it fell.

Now the two things hanging over the market. On the worrying side, the conflict between the United States and Iran escalated again this week, with fresh military strikes near the Strait of Hormuz, the narrow lane that carries much of the world's oil. That pushed the war-risk premium back up. Here is the key part. Crude oil actually fell almost three percent today, which would normally help an oil importer like India. But the market felt no relief, because if the conflict widens, oil could spike straight back up. That fear is the reason a falling oil price did not lift Indian stocks. On the supportive side, the monsoon reached Kerala, only three days late, which is early relief for rural demand.

Now the movers. The biggest gainer was PhysicsWallah, the education company, up almost sixteen percent. The week before, the stock had fallen about thirteen percent, after the company said it would fund student loans through its own in-house finance arm, which investors did not like. Today it reversed that decision and said it will use outside regulated lenders instead. That one reversal won back the entire week's loss in a single session.

The second mover was Zee Entertainment, up ten and a half percent, the stock that lifted the whole media index. Zee won the India broadcast rights to football's FIFA World Cup, for both twenty twenty-six and twenty thirty, plus thirty-seven other FIFA events. This is a multi-year content win for Zee itself, not a sign the whole media sector is turning.

The third was Anant Raj, a real-estate and data-centre developer, up about seven and a half percent. It signed a twenty-five thousand crore rupee agreement with the Haryana state government to build large data-centre and cloud infrastructure. Money keeps flowing toward companies with concrete, government-backed building plans in that space.

Here is the one thing that struck me most about today. A weaker rupee normally helps Indian IT companies, because they earn in dollars and spend in rupees. Today it did not help at all. The rupee is falling because foreign investors are pulling money out of India, and those same investors are selling Indian IT shares on the way out. So the selling pressure outweighed the currency benefit. When the rupee and IT fall together like that, it is foreign money leaving, not a currency story.

Now, what to watch — and it is all tomorrow morning, here in India. At ten, the Reserve Bank of India announces its interest-rate decision. The market expects no change, a hold at five point two five percent, so the Governor's language will matter more than the number. India's growth figure, the fourth-quarter GDP, comes out in the same window. Governor Sanjay Malhotra speaks to the press at noon. And United States jobs data follows at six pm Indian Standard Time. After a quiet today, tomorrow is the session that sets the tone.

That's your brief. Before I sign off: this has been general market commentary, not investment advice. For investment advice tailored to your situation, consult a SEBI-registered Investment Adviser. Markets are risky; you may lose money; act with care. See you tomorrow.
