Good evening. This is India Markets Brief from toroIQ. Your read on today's session.

India's defence stocks re-rated hard today, and it came from a single number. The Ministry of Defence reported that India's home-made defence production hit a record this year, one point seven eight lakh crore rupees, up about sixteen percent and more than double the level of five years ago. That is not a fresh order. It is a production figure. But it turned the make-in-India defence story from a slogan into a number, and it lifted the whole listed group. Paras Defence closed up about six percent at an all-time high. MTAR Tech, Data Patterns, Astra Microwave, HAL and BEL all rose with it.

Here is where the market closed today. The Nifty fifty finished at twenty-four thousand and eighty-six, up about half a percent. That cleared twenty-four thousand for the first time in this rally, on a fourth straight up-day. The Sensex and the Bank Nifty rose about the same. And the buying was broad. Midcaps and smallcaps both did better than the headline, so this was not a narrow move. The shape was cyclical and pre-Fed. With tonight's United States rate decision coming, money rotated into the economy-sensitive corners. Public-sector banks led, and metals, power and consumer-durables names were all up more than one percent.

Now the two things hanging over the market. On the supportive side, oil stayed cheap. Brent ticked up over one percent back above eighty dollars, but it is still down about forty percent from its March peak. For a country that imports most of its oil, that sustained fall keeps easing inflation and the import bill. On the worrying side, two questions are still open. The US-Iran deal signs formally in Geneva on Friday, but the physical clearing of mines from the Strait of Hormuz could take anywhere from forty days to six months. That is why Brent has settled at eighty, not collapsed. And the monsoon read softened. Early-June rainfall ran about a quarter below normal, with a dry-spell risk later in the summer.

Now the movers. The other big gainer was Trent, the Tata retailer, up about seven percent. This was not a fresh result. It was the market finally re-rating its strong fourth-quarter numbers from late April, when revenue rose about twenty percent and profit thirty. The stock has now rebounded about forty percent off its March low.

The standout fall, on a day when almost everything else rose, was Tata Motors' passenger-vehicle arm, down about eight percent. The trigger came from Jaguar Land Rover's Investor Day, where the guidance for next year disappointed. JLR pointed to a thin profit margin of about four percent and cash flow barely at breakeven. Because JLR is roughly forty-five percent of the value of that entity, the weak outlook dragged the whole stock down.

Here is what struck me most about today. The foreign buyers came back for a second day, but only just. Foreigners bought about one hundred crore rupees of Indian shares. Domestic institutions bought about fifteen hundred crore, fifteen times as much. So the move above twenty-four thousand is real, but it is still being carried almost entirely by domestic money. The foreign buy-turn has held, but it has not yet firmed. That is the single thing this rally still lacks, and it is why the next day or two of flow data matter more than the index level.

Now, what to watch. It is all overnight and into the rest of the week. The United States Federal Reserve announces its rate decision late tonight, after our close, so it sets the mood for Thursday, not today. It is the first decision under the new Chair, Kevin Warsh, and a hold is almost fully expected, so the language will matter more than the number. Then on Friday, the formal signing of the US-Iran deal in Geneva.

That's your brief. Before I sign off: this has been general market commentary, not investment advice. For investment advice tailored to your situation, consult a SEBI-registered Investment Adviser. Markets are risky; you may lose money; act with care. See you tomorrow.
