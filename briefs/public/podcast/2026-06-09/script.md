Good evening. This is India Markets Brief from toroIQ. Your read on today's session.

India closed green today, but only just, and the reason was made entirely at home. The Reserve Bank of India opened a new window today that pulls foreign-currency deposits into Indian banks. Under it, the central bank itself pays the full cost of hedging fresh three-to-five-year deposits that banks raise from non-residents. That cuts what banks pay for funding, so bank stocks jumped and dragged the whole index up. The Nifty rose half a percent on the back of banks alone.

Here is where the market closed today. The Nifty fifty finished at twenty-three thousand two hundred and forty-two, up zero point five two percent, and almost all of that lift came from financials. The public-sector bank index led, up three point six percent, with every one of its twelve members green, and the broader Bank Nifty rose two percent. The one red sector was information technology, down half a percent. The fear gauge, India VIX, fell more than eight percent to fifteen point six, the look of a relief session, not a conviction breakout.

Now put India against Asia, because that is the gap. Today the rest of Asia ripped on chip stocks. Korea surged more than eight percent and tripped a circuit-breaker, Taiwan rose almost three and Japan over two, all on memory-chip optimism. India barely owns that trade. Its tech weight is software services, not chips, so India sat out the rally and had to make its own gain. And the flows confirm it. Foreign investors were net sellers of four thousand five hundred crore rupees while domestic funds bought six thousand crore, and the rupee weakened even though the dollar was soft. So today's green was a home-made policy bid, not foreign money coming back.

Now the macro frame. Today's deposit window sits on top of last week's bond-tax ordinance, which scrapped the taxes foreign investors pay on Indian government bonds. Together they pushed the ten-year yield to a four-week low of six point nine percent. But the rupee is still slipping and foreign money is still leaving, so this policy is a response to pressure that has not yet turned. The monsoon is the standing food worry, forecast below normal at about ninety percent of average.

Now the movers. The cleanest winner was JNK India, up almost sixteen percent. The company makes heating equipment, and it won a large order from an Emirates engineering firm for an Abu Dhabi gas project, taking the stock up about ninety percent this year. The second story was defence. Data Patterns rose ten percent and Paras Defence almost eight, both at new highs, as twenty-five of twenty-seven tracked defence names rose on strong order-book outlooks. This is the cohort the market is paying up for. The third was L and T Technology Services, the engineering arm of Larsen and Toubro, up eight percent against a red IT sector, on the sale of one of its units. A stock-specific win, not a sector turn.

Here is what struck me about today. One policy lever moved an entire sector at once, with thirteen of fourteen big lenders green. But the relief is specific. Affordable-housing financiers, which do not fund themselves with non-resident dollars, sat out, one of them basically flat while banks ran. A funding subsidy helps the lenders that use that funding. Do not assume it spreads to all of them.

Now, what to watch, and it is Friday. India's May inflation figure lands, the first read after this policy package, and the food part is the one to watch given the weak monsoon. The same day brings the Reserve Bank's weekly reserves figure, the cleanest sign of how hard it is defending the rupee. One timing note. After India closed today, United States markets were firm and US yields eased. That is a Wednesday input for tomorrow's open. It did not lift today's close.

That's your brief. Before I sign off: this has been general market commentary, not investment advice. For investment advice tailored to your situation, consult a SEBI-registered Investment Adviser. Markets are risky; you may lose money; act with care. See you tomorrow.
