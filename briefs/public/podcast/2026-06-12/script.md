Good evening. This is India Markets Brief from toroIQ. Your read on today's session.

Today was a big, broad relief rally, but it leaned on a deal that nobody has actually signed yet. The Nifty jumped almost two percent on reports the United States and Iran had agreed a draft to wind down their conflict. The reported terms reopen the Strait of Hormuz, the world's busiest oil lane, and that sent crude tumbling. For a country that imports most of its oil, like India, cheaper crude is a direct relief.

Here is where the market closed today. The Nifty fifty finished at twenty-three thousand six hundred and twenty-three, up one point nine nine percent. The Sensex rose two point three percent. This was as broad as it gets. All thirty Sensex stocks closed green, and the smaller companies rose even harder than the headline. The engine was the banks, up almost three percent, because lower oil and softer inflation both point to an easier path for interest rates, which helps lenders. Real estate led all sectors, up three and a half percent, for the same rate-cut logic, since home demand tracks loan costs. The one corner that did not rise was IT, and I will come back to why.

There was a second, home-grown piece of good news the same afternoon. India's May inflation came in at three point nine three percent, just below the four percent economists expected. That eases the worry that the Reserve Bank would have to stop cutting rates. Softer inflation plus cheaper oil is exactly the mix that lifts banks and property.

Now the one caution that sits over everything. The deal is only reported, not signed. Iran's foreign ministry publicly called it, quote, merely speculation. The real test is a reported signing in Geneva this Sunday, the fourteenth of June. If it happens, today's move is confirmed. If it slips, the crude relief that powered today can reverse, and Monday could open with a gap the wrong way. So treat today's rally as resting on a draft, not a done deal.

Here is the quiet tell underneath it. Foreign investors were actually still net sellers today. It was domestic funds that paid for this rally. A rally the locals fund is sturdier than one foreigners chase, but it also means the foreign buying that would confirm the move has not shown up yet.

Now the movers. The sharpest was IFCI, a state-backed financier, up its full twenty percent limit on talk that the National Stock Exchange is preparing its long-awaited listing. IFCI holds an indirect stake in the exchange, so a listing could unlock that hidden value. The other clean story was the two big truck makers, Tata Motors Commercial Vehicles and Ashok Leyland, both up about ten percent. Fuel is a fleet's single biggest cost, so cheaper crude and easier truck-loan rates rotated straight into them.

And that is what struck me most about today. The fall in oil did not stay in oil stocks. It travelled. It lifted the truck makers because fuel is their biggest cost. It lifted the banks and property because cheaper oil cools inflation, which points to lower rates. One single headline rotated money across half a dozen sectors at once, and that is why the rally was so broad. The only loser, IT, sits on the other side of the same coin. A stronger rupee hurts companies that earn in dollars and spend in rupees, so the very move that helped everyone else worked against them. The whole chain, the good and the bad, rests on that unsigned draft.

Now, what to watch, and the big one is this weekend. The reported signing in Geneva on Sunday is the single most important event. A signed text confirms today; a slip is the live risk of a Monday gap the other way. Alongside it, watch the price of crude over the weekend, the cleanest real-time sign of whether the calm is holding.

That's your brief. Before I sign off: this has been general market commentary, not investment advice. For investment advice tailored to your situation, consult a SEBI-registered Investment Adviser. Markets are risky; you may lose money; act with care. See you tomorrow.
