Good evening. This is India Markets Brief from toroIQ. Your read on today's session.

After the close today, Tata Consultancy Services [SAY: T-C-S], India's largest technology company, opened the earnings season with a soft-but-steady result. Revenue rose almost fourteen percent from a year ago. But strip out the weaker rupee, which flatters the headline, and underlying growth was just four-tenths of one percent, close to flat. Because it filed after the market shut, the share reaction comes tomorrow. But the tone for the whole sector is already set.

Here is where the market closed today. The Nifty fifty added zero point three four percent to twenty-three thousand nine hundred and sixty-three, a relief bounce after Wednesday's oil shock. The banks did the heavy lifting, up zero point nine percent, because they had taken the worst of Wednesday's selling and repaired the most today. The one red corner was technology, down about a third of a percent, as investors trimmed it ahead of the TCS result. And the fear gauge, India VIX, collapsed almost nine percent to thirteen point four. That is the clearest sign the panic came off.

India did not move alone today. Emerging-market shares were firm across the board, the main gauges up around one percent, and India simply traded alongside them. This was shared relief, not an India-specific story.

Now the force under the day, and it is the same one that broke Wednesday: Iran. On the supportive side, the conflict partly cooled during the session. President Trump said on the record that he does not think America and Iran go back to full war, and the strikes that did land hit two islands, not Tehran or nuclear sites. That drained the panic. Crude oil stopped rising, the rupee firmed a touch, and the fear gauge fell hard. On the worrying side, nothing is actually settled. This is rhetoric, not a signed ceasefire. Oil is still up about nine percent on the week, gold stayed firm, and a United States tariff deadline on Indian goods still lands on the twenty-fourth of July.

Now the movers. The biggest gainer in the whole market was Kalyan Jewellers, up more than eighteen percent. Kalyan had posted a strong quarterly update on Monday, with sales up thirty-eight percent, but the stock fell nine percent that day on profit-booking. Today reversed that overshoot, helped by a broad jewellery bid as gold firmed. The update is the floor; the size of the jump is a snap-back.

The biggest faller was Dr Reddy's Laboratories, down almost six percent, on a clean, company-specific cause. The company told the exchange that certain batches of semaglutide [SAY: sem-a-GLOO-tide], the blockbuster weight-loss and diabetes drug, failed a quality specification because of an ingredient problem, so commercial supply will be delayed. Dr Reddy's was first to win generic-semaglutide approval in India and Canada, so this dents a flagship product just as it launches. A real setback, not a passing wobble.

The third was Swiggy, the food-delivery company, up almost seven and a half percent. Its foreign ownership fell below fifty percent, pushing domestic ownership over half for the first time, a step toward letting its quick-commerce arm hold its own stock like rival Blinkit. Worth knowing, the analysts are split; JM Financial still rates it Reduce.

Here is the thread that struck me about today. The same unresolved Iran risk that kept oil from crashing also kept gold firm, up about one percent. And firm gold is a direct tailwind for jewellery retailers, whose inventory is worth more and whose sales value rises with the metal. That is the chain running under the day's loudest winners, Kalyan and the smaller jewellers all moving up together. Geopolitics to gold to jewellery. Watch whether it holds if the tension actually eases and gold gives back that premium.

Now, what to watch. Tomorrow brings the first real verdict on TCS, the first session the market can trade those numbers. And in the evening, the Reserve Bank publishes its weekly figures, where the forex-reserves line matters most.
