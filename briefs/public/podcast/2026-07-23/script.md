Good evening. This is India Markets Brief from toroIQ. Your read on today's session.

Today Indian shares fell for a fourth straight day, and the culprit was the same one that has driven the whole slide: oil. Brent crude crossed one hundred dollars a barrel, up about seven percent, after fresh attacks on tankers near the Strait of Hormuz and in the Red Sea. For a country that imports most of its oil, that lands straight on the bill and the rupee. But this was an orderly fall, not a panic. The fear gauge barely moved.

Here is where the market closed today. The Nifty fifty finished at twenty-three thousand eight hundred and sixty-nine, down about half a percent. The fall was broad but shallow, with more stocks falling than rising, and the mid and small caps off about one percent. The one striking non-move was technology. The IT index closed flat, even though a rupee near its weakest ever normally helps software exporters who earn in dollars. Two things cancelled that help. An overnight global tech sell-off, and traders sitting on their hands ahead of Infosys, whose results were due after the close.

And here is the part worth holding on to. India fell, but it fell far less than the rest of the world, even as American and Asian markets were sold hard. The reason was homegrown, not a global wave washing over everyone equally.

Now the two forces that framed the day. On the worrying side, oil got worse, not better. Beyond the Strait of Hormuz, militants struck two Saudi tankers in the Red Sea, opening a second supply front, and a major pipeline halted its flows. A sustained one hundred dollar oil feeds inflation and narrows the Reserve Bank of India's room to cut interest rates later this year. On the supportive side, the one real cushion was domestic money. Foreign investors sold about three thousand crore rupees of Indian shares, but domestic funds bought almost exactly the same amount, share for share, soaking up the entire foreign exit.

Now the movers. The biggest gainer was Tanla Platforms, a messaging-technology company, up about ten percent. It posted a clean quarter, profit up twenty percent, and the market rewarded the beat on a red day. The biggest fall was C-I-E Automotive India [SAY: C-I-E Automotive India], an auto-parts maker, down about eleven percent. Revenue grew, but profit margins missed, and the market punished the squeeze. And a momentum name gave back. H-F-C-L [SAY: H-F-C-L], a telecom-gear company, fell five percent, cooling off after running up more than ninety percent in three months.

Here is what struck me most about today. One oil shock hit this market in three completely separate places. First, it pushed the state fuel retailers into losses. The price they pay for crude jumped, but the pump price they charge did not, and one of them posted its first loss in about fourteen quarters. Second, it blew up the fuel bill at IndiGo [SAY: IndiGo], the airline, tipping it into a quarterly loss. And third, it pressed the rupee toward its weakest-ever level, which quietly raises the cost of everything India imports. Same trigger, three different wounds. That is why, for India, oil is never just a number on a screen.

Now, what to watch, and it all lands tomorrow, Friday. The big one is Infosys. It reported after today's close.
