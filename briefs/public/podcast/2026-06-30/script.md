Good evening. This is India Markets Brief from toroIQ. Your read on today's session.

Here is the headline. The index looked almost flat today, but that flatness was hiding a sharp fall in one corner of the market. The Nifty fifty slipped just a third of a percent, yet underneath, India's big technology shares were sold hard. Infosys and Tata Consultancy Services both fell to a fresh one-year low. This was not a broad selloff. Almost everything outside of technology closed green. It was one sector being re-priced on its own, while the rest of the market held up.

Here is where the market closed today. The Nifty fifty finished at twenty-three thousand eight hundred and sixty-five, down zero point three four percent. The Sensex and the Bank Nifty fell by about the same small amount. But the one heavy weight was the technology sector, down two point seven three percent to a multi-year low. Everything else leaned green. Realty led, up about one point three percent, and the broader market was positive, with the midcaps and the smallcaps both higher. So this was not fear. Money simply moved out of technology and into the home-facing parts of the market.

Now the two threads that shaped the day. On the supportive side, cheap oil has become a real tailwind. Brent crude closed the June quarter down about thirty percent, its biggest quarterly drop since twenty twenty. For a country that imports most of its oil, that means softer inflation and a lighter import bill. On the worrying side is the weather. This June was the driest in over a hundred years, and that is a direct risk to the rural economy and to food prices. The weather office has flagged a system over the Bay of Bengal around the third of July that could finally bring the rains.

Now the movers, and one policy ran through most of them. Delhi approved a new electric-vehicle policy, effective tomorrow, with subsidies on electric two-wheelers and a plan to bar new petrol two-wheelers from twenty twenty-eight. So the electric-scooter makers jumped. Ola Electric rose about eight percent and Ather Energy [SAY: AH-ther] about five percent, to a record high. But the same policy was a threat to old petrol bikes, so Eicher Motors, the maker of Royal Enfield, fell about five percent. One policy, picking winners and losers inside the same auto pack. Maruti Suzuki, meanwhile, rose about five percent on an upgrade from the brokerage Jefferies [SAY: JEFF-reez].

Here is the one thing that struck me most about today. On a normal day, two things lift India's technology exporters. A weaker rupee helps, because they earn in dollars and spend in rupees. And a rising American tech market helps, because it signals their clients are spending. Today the market delivered both. The rupee softened, and the US Nasdaq had closed up more than one percent overnight. And Indian technology fell anyway. When a sector ignores both of its usual tailwinds, the weakness is fundamental, not noise. The real fear is that artificial intelligence is starting to do the routine work these firms used to bill for. That worry, not the global mood, is what sold them today.

One more read, because today was quarter-end. This was both monthly options expiry and the last day of the June quarter, the kind of session where foreign funds exit and prices can get distorted. Foreign investors did sell, about two thousand five hundred crore rupees. But domestic funds bought far more, almost seven thousand crore. So this was book-squaring, not capital flight. India's own savings absorbed the foreign exit cleanly, even on the year's most flow-heavy day.

Now, what to watch. The earnings season opens in the middle of July. Tata Consultancy Services reports first on the ninth, and Infosys follows on the twenty-third, and after today, those two results are the real test of whether this technology selloff is justified. Closer in, tomorrow brings the June auto sales numbers and the final manufacturing survey. And around the third of July, watch the monsoon for the rains to finally catch up.
