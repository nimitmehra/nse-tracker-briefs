Good evening. This is India Markets Brief from toroIQ. Your read on today's session.

Today Indian shares fell for a third day running, and this time the fear finally showed up. The Nifty fifty slipped back below twenty-four thousand, and the market's fear gauge rose for the first time on a down day in this slide. The reason was oil. A fresh flare-up between the United States and Iran pushed crude sharply higher, and for a country that imports most of its oil, that lands straight on the bill.

Here is where the market closed today. The Nifty fifty finished at twenty-three thousand nine hundred and ninety-six, down about eight tenths of a percent. The damage was broad. Fourteen of the sixteen sectors closed red. Financials led the way down, with the Bank Nifty off about one and a quarter percent, its worst day of this slide. And here is the surprise. The technology index fell about one and a half percent, even though a weak rupee normally helps software exporters who earn in dollars and spend in rupees. On a genuine risk-off day, that currency help simply got ignored. Only two corners held green, consumer staples and autos, and both on their own earnings.

The real signal was the fear gauge, India VIX. It rose five and a half percent, to thirteen point three. That is its first climb on a down day this week, which tells you the selling turned from an orderly drift into something more nervous, even if the level is still low.

And here is the unusual part. Overnight, American markets rose, and emerging markets across the region gained nearly three percent, yet India fell anyway. So this was not foreign money fleeing the whole region. It was India going its own way, dragged down by an oil-import bill its neighbours do not carry.

Two forces framed the day. On the worrying side, the oil premium got worse. Brent crude pushed toward ninety-five dollars a barrel, its highest since early June, after an eleventh night of United States and Iran strikes and an attack on a tanker in the Strait of Hormuz, the narrow lane that carries about a fifth of the world's seaborne oil. For India, costlier crude revives the inflation worry, widens the trade gap, and pressures the rupee. On the supportive side, foreign investors were actually net buyers on Tuesday, putting in about sixteen hundred and fifty crore rupees while domestic funds sold. It is only one day and it may not last against the oil headwind, but it is worth watching.

Now the movers. The cleanest gain of the day was Data Patterns, a defence-electronics company, up nearly thirteen percent. It emerged as the lowest bidder for a project worth about thirteen hundred crore rupees from Hindustan Aeronautics, India's state aircraft maker. One caveat. Lowest-bidder status is a bid position, not yet a signed contract, so it is one notch below a confirmed order. Even so, the stock closed near its year high against a falling market.

The biggest fall was Bandhan Bank, down about seventeen percent, and it is a lesson in itself. The bank reported a strong quarter, with profit up thirty-five percent and bad loans easing. But management cut its forward profitability guidance, the number that tells you how much the bank expects to earn on its assets in the year ahead, blaming rising deposit costs. The market sold the weaker outlook hard, and looked straight past the good headline.

And the mirror of that was M and M Financial Services [SAY: M-and-M Financial Services], up about eight percent. It posted a seventy-five percent jump in profit and its best asset quality in eight years. Two lenders, opposite reactions, on the very same day.

Here is what struck me most about today. These two results are the clearest lesson of the season on how the market reads a bank. Bandhan grew its profit by more than a third and fell seventeen percent. M and M Financial grew its profit by three quarters and rose eight. The difference was not last quarter's number at all. It was the outlook. A bank's forward guidance moves its stock more than the profit it just reported, because the market is paying for next year, not last quarter. And Bandhan's cut is a warning the market may now read across the whole small-lending pack.

Now, what to watch. Tomorrow, Thursday the twenty-third, Infosys reports its June-quarter results, the big technology verdict of the season. After a soft read from the sector so far, the whole technology pack will trade off it. And on Friday, the United States tariff deadline on Indian goods, still unsigned.
