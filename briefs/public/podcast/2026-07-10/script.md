Good evening. This is India Markets Brief from toroIQ. Your read on today's session.

Indian shares rose for a second day running today, and the reason was earnings. The June-quarter results season opened well. Tata Consultancy Services [SAY: T-C-S], the country's largest software company, had reported strong numbers the night before, and that lifted the whole technology sector today. State-run banks then beat too, led by Indian Bank. Money flowed back into technology, banks and property. But one caveat runs under the whole day, and I will come back to it. This was home-grown money, not foreign.

Here is where the market closed today. The Nifty fifty added just over one percent, to twenty-four thousand two hundred and seven. The banks did real work, up one point four percent, on that run of strong state-lender results. Technology went from Thursday's worst corner to today's rally leader, the IT index up almost two percent on the read from TCS. And property was the single best sector, the realty index up three and a half percent. Nearly twice as many stocks rose as fell. The fear gauge, India VIX, fell another eight percent to twelve point two five, close to a one-year low.

Now that caveat about the money. All week the story has been that foreign investors are coming back to India after a long exodus. Over several weeks that is true. But today itself does not support it. Foreign investors were small net sellers. It was Indian institutions, buying more than two thousand crore rupees, that carried the market up. So do not read today's rise as proof foreign money drove it.

Now the two forces under the day. On the supportive side, the Reserve Bank's weekly figures, out around five this evening, showed India's foreign-exchange reserves rose more than seven billion dollars, to about six hundred and seventy-four billion. That is reserve building, not the central bank spending money to defend the rupee. On the worrying side, the weather turned. The weather office now expects below-normal rain across much of the country this month. A weaker monsoon threatens rural demand and food prices, so watch consumer and farm-input names.

Now the movers. The biggest single jump, Godrej Industries up sixteen percent, had no clean cause I could find, so I will not invent one. The clean stories were elsewhere. Indian Bank rose almost ten percent after a strong first quarter. Profit was up ten percent, and bad loans nearly halved, from three percent of loans to under two. It opened the state-bank season, and Bank of Maharashtra beat on the same day, turning one result into a pattern.

The technology bounce showed up most in the mid-caps. Zensar Technologies jumped almost fourteen percent, riding the TCS read plus heavy short-covering on more than fifty times its usual volume. The large software names moved far less.

The third was Greaves Cotton, up eleven percent. Its electric-vehicle arm raised about three hundred and thirty crore rupees through a rights issue that was fully subscribed. That funds the EV business without leaning on the parent company.

Here is the one thing that struck me about today. When fear drains out of a market, money flows first into the corners most sensitive to interest rates and sentiment. That is why property led. The odd part is this. Property rose even as the United States ten-year bond yield ticked up, which normally hurts rate-sensitive real estate. Today a domestic-demand and earnings story simply overwhelmed that global signal. Watch whether it holds if US yields keep climbing into next week's inflation reading.

Now, what to watch, and it is two clocks. The nearer one is the monsoon, whether the below-normal call actually verifies through the end of July. The bigger one is trade. A temporary ten percent United States tariff on Indian goods expires on the twenty-fourth of July, and while both sides call the talks constructive, nothing is signed. That deadline is the market's largest unresolved event.
