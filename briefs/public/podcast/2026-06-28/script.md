Good morning. This is India Markets Brief from toroIQ. Your read on what you missed over the long weekend, and what to watch at today's open.

Here is the one thing to understand before the market opens. On Thursday, the last day India traded, the market turned cheaper oil into its single biggest trade. The Nifty barely moved, but underneath it the car-makers and the airlines ran hard, on the simple logic that cheaper fuel lifts demand and cuts running costs. Then, over the long weekend while India was shut, that exact trade came under threat. So the open is not calm. It is nervous.

Here is what changed while we were closed. The ceasefire between the United States and Iran collapsed into open conflict at the Strait of Hormuz, the narrow lane that carries much of the world's oil. A tanker carrying more than two million barrels of crude, the Kiku [SAY: KEE-koo], was hit. The United States struck Iran. And Iran fired back at American bases in Kuwait and Bahrain. People are already calling it the twenty twenty-six Strait of Hormuz crisis. The cheap oil that powered Thursday's rally now sits on top of a live war at the world's most important oil chokepoint.

Now think about what that does to Thursday's winners. The auto pack and the airlines, names like Maruti, Mahindra, and IndiGo [SAY: IN-dee-go], all rose on the idea that fuel was getting cheaper. If oil spikes at the open, that idea reverses, and the very stocks that led on Thursday become the ones that hurt. For a country that imports about eighty-five percent of its oil, a Hormuz disruption is the worst kind of news. It lifts the import bill, it feeds inflation, and it pressures the rupee. The relief of the past few weeks could flip into a fresh worry in a single session.

And there is a second blow landing at the same time. Over the weekend, technology shares sold off worldwide. The United States Nasdaq fell for a fifth straight session, and the big Asian memory-chip makers, Samsung and SK Hynix [SAY: S-K Hynix], dropped hard, on a rethink of how the artificial-intelligence boom is being funded. The way that reaches India is through mood. When global tech falls, Indian IT services tend to fall with it, even when their own order books have not changed.
