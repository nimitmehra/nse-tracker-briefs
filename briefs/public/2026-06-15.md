## The Lead

The United States and Iran signed a 14-point deal over the weekend to end their roughly 107-day war, and that single event powered Monday's Indian session. The agreement, signed Sunday, reopens the Strait of Hormuz, the world's busiest oil shipping lane, which sent Brent crude down 5.27% to $82.73 a barrel. Cheaper oil matters directly to India, which imports about 85% of its crude, because it eases the import bill, the current-account gap and fuel-driven inflation all at once. That, in turn, widens the room the Reserve Bank has to keep cutting rates, so the money rotated into rate-sensitive sectors and lifted the Nifty 50 to a 0.98% gain at 23,853.90, with risers beating fallers roughly four to one. One caution sits over it: the deal is signed but not yet implemented, with Hormuz mine-clearance assessed at 40 to 50 days before tankers transit confidently.

## Global Setup

The backdrop was broadly risk-on, and the peace deal was the hinge for everyone. Last night's Wall Street close that shaped this morning's mood was firm, and Asia, which trades during Indian hours, was strong (KOSPI +4.63%, Nikkei +2.81%, Hang Seng +1.93% on the prior session). The dollar was barely soft (DXY −0.19%), so the rupee's much larger 1.11% jump was an India-specific bid off the crude story, not a weak-dollar move. Gold rose 3.76% to about $4,373 even on a risk-on day, a reminder the all-clear is not fully sounded while implementation lags. The read for India: it rode the same global relief wave as everyone, but its heavy oil-import bill gave it an extra, home-grown tailwind on top. Wall Street's gains *tonight* (S&P +1.87%, Nasdaq +3.03%) are tomorrow's input, not today's cause.

## Macro & Policy

The day's macro picture leaned strongly to the good, with one honest caveat.

**On the supportive side**, the signed US–Iran deal crashed Brent to a three-month low and lifted the rupee about 40 paise to 94.70, the textbook read-through of falling oil for a big importer. May inflation, released Friday at 3.93%, sits comfortably inside the Reserve Bank's 2–6% band; it is the fifth straight monthly rise and the highest in the rebased series, but "at target" is the right frame, not an inflation scare. Together, cheaper oil and tame inflation keep the door open for further rate cuts, which is what the rate-sensitive rally was pricing.

**The caveat that cuts the other way** is that the deal is signed but not done. Hormuz mine-clearance is assessed at 40 to 50 days, and about 600 ships, including some 250 oil and gas tankers, remain stuck in the Gulf waiting on it; shipowners want security assurances before they sail. That is why Brent fell on the *signing* but still holds roughly $16 above its pre-conflict $67 level. The relief is real, but the last leg of it is not yet banked.

**Watch next week:** the formal Geneva signing ceremony on Friday June 19, the path of Brent as the live gauge of whether the de-escalation is holding, and the US Federal Reserve's rate decision and projections on June 16–17, which set the global rate mood India trades inside.

## The Market

Monday was a broad, rate-sensitive relief rally rather than a top-heavy one: the Nifty 100 (+1.24%) outran the Nifty 50 (+0.98%), so the strength sat in the wider market, not just the giants. Realty led every sector (+3.96%), the classic winner when rates look like they are heading lower, because property demand tracks home-loan costs; autos followed (+2.60%), helped by cheaper crude lowering input and running costs, with consumption (+1.73%) and metals (+1.78%) joining in. Banks lagged the broad tape (Nifty Bank +0.68%) even though they are up 5.67% on the month, as the higher-beta money chased mid-caps over large-cap lenders. The lone red sector was pharma (−0.66%): defensives were sold to fund the rate-sensitive names, a rotation rather than any distress. The rupee's 1.11% climb outran a flat dollar, an India-specific bid on the oil story, and was not a visible Reserve Bank intervention, since the latest weekly reserves data showed no large drawdown. India VIX, the fear gauge, eased 2.5% to 14.35 as the geopolitical tail compressed.

## The Connections

*How the day's events ripple across industries — the part most market reports skip.*

**Cheaper oil did more work here than the headline shows.** When Brent fell on the Hormuz-reopening story, the relief did not stay in oil stocks; it travelled. It eased the inflation outlook, which eased the expected rate path, which lifted the rate-sensitive cohorts together: realty (lower home-loan costs), autos (cheaper running costs plus easier financing), and consumer names (lower input costs). One geopolitical headline rotated money across half a dozen sectors at once, which is why the rally was so broad. The catch is that the last leg of the oil relief is gated on mine-clearance that is still weeks out.

**The oil companies themselves did NOT rise the way cheaper crude suggests they should have.** Energy was nearly flat (Nifty Energy +0.64%), well below the broad tape. For the state oil marketers the crude-down read cuts both ways: lower input cost is offset by marketing-margins normalising and by weaker realisations at the upstream producers like ONGC. The two roughly cancel, so the textbook "cheap oil lifts oil stocks" reaction simply did not appear. The non-move is the signal: the relief lands on importers' inflation math, not on the oil cohort's profits.

**IT held its ground despite a currency that should have hurt it.** A stronger rupee normally hurts IT, which earns in dollars and spends in rupees, yet Nifty IT closed +0.98%, in line with the market. The likely reason is an offset: global technology was strong overnight, supplying a bid that roughly cancelled the currency headwind. So IT neither led nor lagged — a tug-of-war that ended in a draw, worth knowing because the currency drag is still there underneath.
