## The Lead

Foreign investors looked to have turned buyers of Indian shares on Tuesday after selling for 13 sessions in a row. That is the first sign the money leaving India through May and early June may be coming back, and Business Standard's recap led with it, alongside a firmer rupee and a calmer fear gauge. The turn is directional, not a confirmed number: one futures-data aggregator still showed foreign cash selling of about ₹749 crore. So treat the *direction* as the news and wait for the official end-of-day figure. What pulled the money back is the same thing driving the last three sessions: cheaper oil. Brent fell another 5.19% to $78.85, easing India's import bill, and the Nifty 50 added 0.57% to 23,989.15 with risers and no qualifying fallers.

## Macro & Policy

The macro picture stayed supportive at the surface but added two cautions underneath.

**On the supportive side**, Brent extended its fall to $78.85, now down about 28% on the month and decisively below $80. For a country that imports roughly 85% of its crude, that keeps easing the import bill, the current-account gap and fuel-driven inflation together, which is what kept the rate-sensitive rally going. The rupee firmed again to 94.55 and the India VIX, the market's fear gauge, dropped 6.88% to 13.36, back toward where it sat before the war.

**The cautions that cut the other way** are two. First, the Strait of Hormuz is taking longer to reopen than first thought. Estimates for clearing the naval mines now range from about 40 days up to as much as six months on the Pentagon's internal projection, and there is an unresolved dispute over whether Iran can charge "service fees" on ships passing through. That is why Brent, even after the fall, still holds a premium over its pre-conflict level near $67 — the easy part of the oil relief may already be in the price. Second, the weather office has shifted its monsoon outlook to below-normal, now projecting about 90% of the long-period average on an emerging El Niño, with dry-spell risk over central and northwest farming belts in August–September. The near-term progress is still healthy, but the season-outlook downgrade is a fresh worry for rural demand and food prices later in the year — a clear change from yesterday's more favourable read.

**Watch:** the US Federal Reserve decision lands overnight tonight (June 16–17), after the Indian close, so it is Thursday's input, not today's; the G7 summit's final day is Wednesday; the formal Geneva signing of the US–Iran deal is Friday June 19.

## The Market

It was a third straight up-session, broad but milder than Monday: the Nifty 50 added 0.57% to 23,989.15, the Sensex 0.71%, while Bank Nifty (+0.17%) was nearly flat after a strong week. The same-day sector feed was unavailable this run, so the cleanest read of the rotation is the one-week trend, which the day's stock moves confirmed. Realty was the standout (+9.20% on the week), the most rate-sensitive corner and the natural winner when cheaper oil points to lower rates; it drove much of today's breadth, with Brigade, Aditya Birla Real Estate and Ashiana among the biggest gainers. FMCG (+3.21%) and autos (+4.71%) carried the consumption read, helped by softer crude on input costs. Metals were the lone corner working against the day: Hindalco fell 3.11%, the biggest Nifty 50 loser, as the Hormuz peace removed the war-premium that had supported aluminium (more in The Connections). The rupee's 0.59% climb against a flat dollar was an India-specific bid on the oil story, and the VIX easing to 13.36 says the geopolitical fear is still draining out.

## The Connections

*How the day's events ripple across industries — the part most market reports skip.*

**Peace is not uniformly good news — it redistributes.** The clearest lesson of the session is two-sided. The same Hormuz reopening that crashed crude and helped India's oil-importers also removed the war-premium that had propped up aluminium. LME aluminium fell about 4.4% to its lowest since March, and Hindalco dropped 3.11% to lead the Nifty 50 down, with NALCO and Vedanta alongside it. So when you hear "peace deal lifts markets," remember it helps the buyers of oil and pressures the sellers of war-premium commodities at the same time. Copper held firm, so this is aluminium-specific, not a broad metals collapse.

**The flow-turn matters more than any single sector move.** Cheaper oil is now a three-day-old story; the genuinely new thing today is that foreign money looked to stop leaving. Through May and early June, foreign investors were net sellers, which is part of why the Nifty is still down about 8% for the year despite the recent bounce. If today's turn holds for a second and third day, it changes the rally from "domestic funds cushioning the fall" to "foreign money actively returning" — the difference between a relief bounce and a sustained move. That is why the next two days of flow data are the thing to watch.

**A stronger rupee should have hurt IT — one deal overrode it.** A firmer rupee normally pressures IT firms, which earn dollars and spend rupees. Yet IT did not soften today, because HCLTech's Sarvam AI deal supplied a stock-specific bid that pulled the wider IT/AI cohort up with it. The currency drag is still there underneath; it was simply overwhelmed by one company's news. Worth knowing, because the next time there is no such catalyst, the rupee headwind will show through.
