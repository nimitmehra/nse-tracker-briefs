## What Happened

### The US-Iran deal got signed — Friday's risk resolved bullishly

Friday's relief rally rested on an unsigned draft, and the live worry was a weekend signing slip that would gap the war-premium back in on Monday. Instead the deal got done. A senior US official confirmed on Sunday that President Trump, Vice-President Vance and Iran's Ghalibaf had digitally signed the 14-point memorandum — a 60-day ceasefire, a permanent halt to operations, and the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz, with a formal Geneva ceremony set for Friday 19-June. That overnight resolution is what drove today's session, so Monday read as a clean bullish continuation rather than fresh news. The signing itself is now confirmed across multiple Western wires plus an on-record US briefing; only the implementation — mine-clearance and idled-field restarts — still sits in the "reported, pending execution" box. For the principal, the macro overhang that defined the prior week is gone.

### Crude below $83 is the real prize for an oil-importing economy

The mechanism under the move is oil. Brent fell another ~5% to $82.73, its lowest since March, as the signed deal and the Hormuz reopening unwound the last of the war-premium. For an economy that imports about 85% of its crude, that one variable eases the import bill, the rupee, the subsidy math and input costs all at once. With May inflation already back at target (3.93%), it is a clean disinflation set-up that widens the runway for an eventual rate cut. That is exactly why the rate-sensitive cohort led: realty up 3.96%, autos up 2.60%, financials firm, while defensive pharma rotated out at −0.66%. The rupee firmed about 40 paise to 94.70 and outran a barely-softer dollar — an India-specific bid, not a pure dollar story. The one caution is that the easy leg of the crude fall may be largely done; a signed deal is not the same as physical barrels flowing.

### The rally was domestic-led, and the flow turn is still unconfirmed

The breadth was real — the broader Nifty 100 (+1.24%) outpaced the Nifty 50, so this was not a top-heavy, large-cap-only move. But the foreign-money question stays open. Today's provisional flow split was not yet published at our write-time, and the last hard reading (12-June: foreigners net sellers at −₹1,082 crore, domestics net buyers at +₹5,341 crore) still describes the established regime — domestic institutions absorbing while foreigners trim. So the relief rally is best read as domestic-led and consistent with reduced foreign selling pressure, not as a confirmed foreign re-engagement. We are not asserting a flow-turn without the print.
