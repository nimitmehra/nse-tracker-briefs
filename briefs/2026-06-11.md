## What Happened

### A shallow IT-led drift, but the weakness was regional, not India's

Thursday's Nifty 50 slipped just 0.23% to 23,161.60, and the shape mattered more than the number. The drag was one sector: Nifty IT fell 1.62%, its fourth down-day in a row, tracking an overnight US-tech rout that knocked the Nasdaq about 2% with Nvidia and Broadcom sharply lower. That is global beta, not an India problem — the Indian IT pack moved with the US tape, not against it. Underneath, banks held up (+0.14%), pharma firmed (+0.61%) and a media pop cushioned the index. Critically, the softness was region-wide: the Nikkei fell 0.5%, the Kospi and Shanghai each about 0.2%, so India's −0.23% was actually milder than its peers. For the principal the read is a calm global-beta down-day, not a fresh leg of the correction — the market absorbed it in an orderly way, with India VIX flat at 15.6 and no fear bid.

### West Asia escalated for real — but the Hormuz "closure" is contested, not effective

Yesterday's "the calm is a reprieve" framing did not hold. The April ceasefire is now in active breach: US and Iran traded strikes for a second straight day, the US disabled an Iranian oil-carrying vessel in the Gulf of Oman, and President Trump threatened to seize Iran's main oil-export hub at Kharg Island. That much is confirmed across several independent wires. But the loudest headline — Iran's Revolutionary Guard declaring the Strait of Hormuz "closed to all vessels" — is a contested claim, not a fact. US Central Command says commercial ships are still transiting, and the market is voting with the tape, since Brent sits at only about $94, firm but not spiking. For an economy that imports roughly 85% of its oil, this re-introduces a war-premium risk that had been priced out, so crude stays the single most thesis-load-bearing variable in the book — but Thursday is incident-level escalation with contained market impact, not a supply shock yet.

### The domestic cushion: a foreign bond bid and an RBI-supported rupee

The offset to the global gloom came from India's own rates and currency. The 10Y government bond yield rallied to about a two-month low as foreign investors bought roughly ₹10,000 crore of Indian debt, helped by a tax exemption on eligible debt-investment gains and the central bank widening the range of securities open to overseas buyers. The RBI also looks to have stepped in again to support the rupee. So while foreign money keeps leaving equities — a record selling streak now stretching nearly a year — the same foreigners are buying bonds, which softens the blow and keeps the rate-sensitive parts of the book (housing finance, EPC) on a benign footing. One mechanical note: Thursday was weekly derivatives expiry, which amplified the second-half profit-booking near resistance — part of the fade was positioning, not headline.
