## What Happened

### Crude below $80 keeps doing the work — both sides now visible

The story has not changed since Monday, only deepened. Brent fell another 5.19% to $78.85, decisively below $80 and down nearly 28% on the month, as the US–Iran deal and the coming Strait of Hormuz reopening drained the last of the war-premium. For an economy that imports about 85% of its crude that single variable eases the import bill, the rupee, fuel inflation and subsidy math together — and it showed, with the rupee firming a second session to 94.55 on an India-specific bid and fear bleeding out as India VIX fell to 13.36. But today made the trade's two-sidedness plain: the same Hormuz peace that helps oil-importers removed a supply premium that had propped up aluminium, so London aluminium fell about 4.4% to a March low. There is no metals exposure in the book, so the read for the principal is clean — the crude tailwind is real and one-directional for the holdings, but the easy leg is largely spent. The next leg of crude downside is gated on physical barrels, not headlines.

### IT firmed on one deal, not a sector re-rating

The day's cleanest single-stock catalyst sat outside the book. HCL Technologies rose 3.55% after announcing a ₹1,427 crore (about $150 million) strategic minority stake of 10.46% in Sarvam AI, leading the startup's Series B at a $1.5 billion valuation and buying access to its sovereign-AI and Indic-language model stack. That lifted the IT and AI sentiment cluster — Coforge up 4.44%, Persistent up 2.58% — and steadied Infosys, which firmed 0.77% but still sits near its 52-week low after a 30% drawdown from the high. The honest read is that this is concentrated, catalyst-driven leadership in one name, not a broad IT re-rating: the IT index was flat on the week. For Infosys the day's modest Valmet IT-transformation deal-win is thesis-consistent but incremental, feeding the large-deal engine rather than re-rating the stock. The read-through is a watch-item, not a deployment trigger.

### The rally stayed domestic-led — and one wire's "inflows" framing is contradicted

Breadth held — 45 of the Tier-2 names that moved cleared the bar to the upside — but the foreign-money question is still open and, today, slightly muddied. The provisional flow read had foreigners net sellers of about ₹749 crore in the cash segment, with the same-day domestic provisional not cleanly published at our write-time. That matters because at least one Tier-1 wrap framed the third straight gain as helped by "a resurgence in foreign fund inflows" — which directly conflicts with the provisional sell print. The honest reading is unchanged from Monday: the regime is domestic-led and foreign-trimming, not a confirmed foreign re-engagement. We are not asserting a flow-turn the cash tape contradicts. One genuinely next-session input sits overnight: the US Federal Reserve decision and dot-plot land late on the 17th IST, after today's close, so nothing in today's session traces to it — it is Wednesday's input, with the Geneva signing ceremony on Friday the 19th.

The mechanism is the crude-to-input edge: cheaper oil eased the packaging input line (Varun Beverages held its high) and the FMCG margin read (Hindustan Unilever +2.03%, Tata Consumer +2.77%), while the same peace unwound aluminium's war-premium and sent the metals complex (Hindalco −3.11%, the biggest Nifty 50 loser) the other way. The mechanism is the rate-sensitive cohort edge: realty led the week (+9.20%) and consumption firmed, the most leveraged beneficiaries of the disinflation set-up. Tomorrow's read: this holds only as long as the disinflation set-up holds — and the Fed decision overnight is the next input the rupee and rate-sensitive cohort will trade on Wednesday, not today's cause.
