## What Happened

### Imported risk-off, not an India problem

Monday fell on two shocks that both came from offshore. The prior US session saw the Nasdaq drop about 4.18% — roughly $2 trillion erased — as the AI-megacap trade unwound on bubble and Fed-hike fears, with Nvidia, Micron and Marvell down 6% to 17%. Layered on top, the April Lebanon ceasefire collapsed over the weekend: Israeli strikes on Beirut on 07-Jun drew Iranian missile waves on 08-Jun, and Brent firmed about 1.8% on the day past $94 as the Strait of Hormuz reopening timeline pushed out again. Together with persistent foreign selling, that dragged Nifty −1.04% with only 9 of 50 names green. Because India imports roughly 85% of its crude, it is a relative loser when oil spikes into a risk-off — so Friday's GDP beat, rate hold and bond-reform package were entirely overridden. For the principal the read is that this is a sentiment-and-flow drawdown on a structurally fine India, not a fundamentals event in the book.

### Jio Financial slides toward its one-year low

Jio Financial Services, the rate-sensitive lender in the portfolio, was among the worst tier-one names on the day, down 3.39% to ₹228.86 — now just 2.1% above its 52-week low and below every key moving average. There was no fresh company news: executives were on a routine Montreal investor roadshow and disclosed a small ₹49.5-million top-up into the Jio Allianz insurance venture, neither of which moves the thesis. The drop is the financials cohort selling off on the global risk-off plus a technical breakdown after a two-day bounce faded. The funding backdrop stays net-constructive after Friday's bond reforms, so this is a sentiment and chart signal rather than a deterioration — but the proximity to the one-year low is now the live thing to watch on this holding.

### The high-momentum book gave back the most

The pattern of the day was clean: the names that had run hardest fell hardest. TD Power Systems, the largest tier-one holding, dropped 6.63% — the biggest fall in the book — purely as profit-taking off its late-May all-time high, with no filing behind it and the one-month return still roughly flat. Power Mech Projects fell 4.42% despite a positive standing order, and KPI Green Energy lost 3.97% to extend the worst monthly slide in the book. None of these had a negative catalyst; all are high-beta capital-goods, power-equipment and renewables names that sell off first when global risk appetite turns. The read for the principal is that this is exactly the kind of broad scare that the buy-pipeline accumulation plans were waiting for — the give-back is mechanical, not a thesis break, so it sharpens entry math rather than spoiling it.
