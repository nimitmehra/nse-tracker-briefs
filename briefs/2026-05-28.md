## What Happened

### The Iran ceasefire is being shot at, not signed

The single macro that organises everything else got materially worse overnight. The reported US-Iran ceasefire framework — never a signed order, only rhetoric — degraded into an active exchange of fire. Iran launched a ballistic missile toward Kuwait, which Kuwaiti forces intercepted and both Kuwait and the United States called an egregious violation. The Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps said it struck a US air base in retaliation for US strikes near Bandar Abbas, and US Central Command intercepted five attack drones near the Strait of Hormuz. This repositions four threads at once — Brent, the rupee, the sulphur input cost that squeezes fertiliser makers, and Middle-East order risk for the engineering contractors. It also feeds the inflation-upside case the rate-setters must weigh on 3-5 June. Tellingly, Brent held below $97 and is still down on the week, so the market is pricing a contained flare-up, not a Hormuz closure — both tails are live, the distribution skewed worse than yesterday.

### Crude keeps grinding lower despite the strikes

On the fresh tape the price action diverged from the headlines. Brent settled at $93.02, down 1.35% on the day and 16.4% on the month, extending the de-rating that began on peace-framework hopes — the strikes drove an intraday spike toward $96-97 that did not hold into the close. For India, which imports about 85% of its crude, the soft settlement is net positive for the import bill, downstream refining margins and the rupee, which firmed to 95.68 tracking a soft dollar rather than decoupling weak as it did on 27-May. The bond market is unmoved: the 10-year yield pinned flat at 6.996% for a second session, signalling no belief in durable oil-driven disinflation. The escalation is a risk overlay on a market that, for now, still trusts the eventual-deal base case.

### A trade-deal clock starts ticking toward end-July

A second macro thread sharpened into a binary window. India and the United States hold their next trade round on 1-4 June to finalise an interim agreement, with the reciprocal tariff cut from 25% to 18% on the table — and the US-India Strategic Partnership Forum has now named end-July as the drop-dead date, beyond which India risks tariffs above its Asian competitors. A finished deal would be a cohort tailwind for export-exposed holdings — Garware's films, Acutaas's specialty chemicals, TD Power's generators. The discipline holds: this is a negotiating framework plus executive rhetoric, reported not confirmed, until a signed text or a customs notification lands. The June round just became a catalyst with a deadline behind it.
