## What Happened

### NSE files its record IPO papers, financials re-rate

The day's most consequential event was structural, not a single stock. The National Stock Exchange filed its draft prospectus with SEBI for a roughly ₹30,000 crore share sale — India's largest-ever IPO if it lists, and a pure sale by existing holders rather than fresh capital. The filing set off a value-unlock read across everything that owns NSE shares or trades as a comparable: New India Assurance jumped 7.98%, Aditya Birla Sun Life AMC 6.30%, and ICICI Prudential AMC 5.57%, with the broader financials line (Financial Services +0.67%, PSU Bank +0.66%) doing the heavy lifting that carried the headline up a fifth day. There is no direct portfolio play here, but the read-through touches the book's edge — Jio Financial firmed quietly inside the same bid. The honest framing is that this is a sentiment-and-revaluation event on a marquee filing, worth watching as the listing ecosystem develops, not a same-day deployment trigger.

### India decouples from a hawkish Fed

The US Federal Reserve held its rate at 3.50–3.75% in its first meeting under new Chair Kevin Warsh, but the surprise was hawkish. The policymakers' own projections flipped from cuts to a hike bias — the median end-2026 rate marked up to 3.8%, nine of eighteen members now penciling in at least one hike, and the statement dropped its easing language. That landed overnight, before today's open, and it crushed the global complex — gold fell 3.57% and silver 7.78% as the dollar jumped to a 52-week high. Yet Nifty rose anyway, trading its own domestic catalysts rather than moving as emerging-market beta. The rupee even held firm on a clear dollar-up day. This is India trading as India — a finding worth banking, because it tells you the deployment calculus here is increasingly set by domestic flows and policy, not the US rate path.

### IT takes the Fed hit, the lone sector down

The one corner where the hawkish Fed did bite was the export-IT cohort. Nifty IT fell 1.19%, the only sector down meaningfully on the day, on a clean double-hit: a higher-for-longer US rate path lifts the discount rate applied to IT-services valuations and caps the discretionary-tech-spend recovery, while the firming rupee is a direct headwind to dollar-earner revenue. Infosys, the portfolio's IT holding, was among the top losers at roughly −2.3% — though note the name failed in our price feed, so that figure is cohort-and-index confirmed, not name-confirmed. There is no own-name catalyst here; it is pure macro beta. The read for the book is that the higher-for-longer signal is a standing headwind to the Infosys thesis, which needs the discretionary-spend recovery the Fed just made harder — the next own-name checkpoint is its board-meeting agenda, not today's move.
