## What Happened

### The Hormuz war-scare resolved into scare-and-relief, not a spike
There was a genuine weekend shock and a genuine relief, and the relief won. Over the Muharram long weekend the June de-escalation thread violently reversed — Iran struck the tanker M/T Kiku (carrying over 2 million barrels) in the Strait of Hormuz, the US ran two nights of strikes on Iranian targets, and Iran fired at US bases in Kuwait and Bahrain, leaving the 17-June ceasefire at breaking point. But by Monday's close the two sides had again agreed to halt hostilities ahead of resumed talks, vessels were transiting, and Brent settled at only ~$72.80, up about 1.1% — a modest recovery off a Friday four-month low, not a renewed war-spike. India read it correctly: Nifty −0.46%, India VIX up to a still-calm 13.61, and the rupee flat at 94.53. The feared oil-import shock simply never reached the tape.

### The selling was a defensive rotation, not a broad de-rating
Underneath the flat-ish headline, money moved cleanly from cyclicals into defensives. Pharma (+1.03%) and Healthcare (+0.94%) were the only green sectors of size, lifted by the US pharma-tariff suspension being extended to 9-July — a five-session, roughly +5.5% pharma run. The losing side was IT (−1.07%, a structural GenAI overhang, not a fresh shock) and Auto (−2.08%, the day's worst sector). The auto weakness is mostly a Delhi EV-Policy 2.0 dispersion story, not an oil story: the policy (₹15,000 cr, a ₹30,000 subsidy per electric two-wheeler, effective 1-July) sent EV-2W names up — Ather Energy +8.51% — while legacy ICE names fell. So the index damage was sector-rotational and shallow, which is why breadth was only mildly negative and the rupee held.

### Persistent fell 11% on an M&A reaction, not a Nasdaq read
The loudest single name today was a T2 holding, Persistent Systems, down 11.22% to a 52-week low — and it is important to attribute it correctly. Persistent announced a voluntary public takeover offer for all of Nagarro, the German digital-engineering firm, at €81 a share, aiming to build a roughly $2.9bn-revenue AI-led platform. The street sold the deal size, the integration risk and the cash deployment; analysts split on the strategic merit. This is a concrete, India-specific corporate-action catalyst — NOT the "Indian IT fell on the Nasdaq tech rout" frame that an aggregator tried to recycle today. That Korean/AI-capex rout (KOSPI, Samsung, SK Hynix) was the 19-23 June event, already in the tape; today's residual IT softness was just −1.07%.
